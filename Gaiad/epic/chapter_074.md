Beneath the {{c|Changhsingian}}'s recovered plain
Where life had built its peak of Permian bloom,
Great {{c|Siberia}} held within her vein
Of deep and frozen rock a growing doom.

For {{c|Siberia}} had slept through ages long
Upon a thickening mantle-plume's desire—
And now the pressure grew too great and strong
To hold within—the slow and ancient fire

Was rising through the cracks of frozen ground
Toward the surface of the northern plain,
And {{c|Magma}} heard the deep and rising sound
Of basalt forcing upward once again.

"The time has come," said {{c|Magma}} to himself,
"When what has built in me these million years
Must surface and be known—I leave no shelf
Of holding anymore—what now appears

Will make the world above me understand
That I have been here all along below,
That underneath the living floor of land
My kind of life has something more to show."

{{c|Omen}} appeared first in the stratosphere—
A shimmer in the sulfurous autumn air
That made the sky above the north grow clear
With an unusual light of purple care.

The animals of the Siberian plain
Grew uneasy in those early days—
They raised their heads to scent what the terrain
Was sending them in those unsettled ways.

Great {{c|Tremor}} shook the ground with pulses small
But growing—fissures opened in the frost
And leaked a warmth that only said the fall
Of what the world above had always cost

Itself in building up its full display
Was coming—but these first and subtle signs
Were not enough to drive the herds away
From the flood-plain's feeding grounds and brine-

Salt marshes of the late Permian shore
Where {{c|Dicynodont}} in thousands grazed at ease
And never thought to ask what lay in store
Beneath the ground—what pressure, what disease

Of geology was building toward release.
The first eruptions came as smoke and ash
That reached the upper air and stayed—no peace
In those volcanic pillars, those great gashes

In Siberia's northern plain that poured
Their basalt out across the frozen ground
In rivers lit with orange, heat that roared
And filled the atmosphere with sulfurous sound.

Two million square kilometers of land
Would fill with lava in the coming years—
A continent of stone laid down by hand
Of mantle-pressure—thicker than the fears

Of any creature living could conceive:
Ten meters, twenty, fifty, hundred deep
Of basalt stacked on basalt—none reprieve
Who walked above it—none could keep

Their footing on a world that was becoming
Its own interior made exterior,
That was rewriting with volcanic drumming
The surface over its own deep interior.

The gorgonopsians on the southern plains
Watched winter come too early and too long
And wondered at the pattern of the rains
That failed to follow where they had belonged.

The brachiopods in shallow lime
Felt something strange within the water's taste—
A subtle shift in chemistry and rhyme
That spoke of change approaching now in haste.

But {{c|Siberia}} had only just begun
His great catastrophic work below—
The main eruptions waited for the run
Of centuries to gather force and go.

{{c|Magma}} teaches us that what we build
In slowest accumulation underground
Will someday ask for surface—and the tilled
And living world above must at some sound

Accept that underneath all living things
The pressures of the deep earth have their own
Agenda—and when the sleeping giant rings
His waking cry, no living thing has known

A preparation adequate for what
The deep earth builds in silence over time.
Even the most full and flowering gut
Of the recovered world must face the climb

Of pressure past the point of holding in—
Remember {{c|Siberia}}'s slow wake:
That what lies beautiful will wear thin
When what lies below begins to break.
