# Chapter 163: Zarathustra

On the {{p|Iranian}} plateau, between the {{p|Caspian Sea}} and the {{p|Hindu Kush}},
A prophet appeared who would reshape world religion.

                                                     {{c|Zarathustra}}.
Or, in {{p|Greek}} transliteration, {{c|Zoroaster}}. An {{p|Indo-European}} speaker

Of the {{p|Iranian}} branch. A man of the {{c|R1a}} haplogroup lineage that
Had carried {{p|Indo-Aryan}} peoples onto the {{p|Iranian}} plateau and into
The {{p|Indus Valley}}. A priest in the tradition of the {{p|Indo-Iranian}}
Fire-sacrifice, the same tradition that in {{p|India}} produced the

{{p|Vedic}} religion. And a figure so foundational to {{p|Iranian}} religion,
To {{p|Iranian}} philosophy, and to subsequent {{p|Western}} religious thought
That his actual historical location in time is difficult to pin
Down—estimates range from fifteenth century BCE (placing him near

The {{p|Indo-Aryan}} migration) to sixth century BCE (placing him near
The rise of the {{p|Achaemenid}} Empire). Most modern scholarship
Settles on some point between twelve-hundred and one-thousand BCE—
That is, roughly contemporary with the {{p|Bronze Age Collapse}} in the

{{p|Mediterranean}} and the {{p|Zhou}} conquest in {{p|China}}. The Gaiad adopts
This dating. {{c|Zarathustra}} is another node in the widespread
Religious transformation that followed the collapse of the Bronze
Age order. His reform of {{p|Indo-Iranian}} religion is part of the

Broader Axial shift that would also produce, in their own times
And places, the {{p|Hebrew}} prophets, the {{p|Greek}} philosophers, the
{{p|Upanishads}} of {{p|India}}, {{c|Confucius}} and {{c|Laozi}} in {{p|China}}, and
{{c|Buddha}} and {{c|Mahavira}} in the {{p|Gangetic}} plain. {{c|Zarathustra}} is the

Earliest named prophet of this transformation. He is the first to
Speak, in the surviving record, with the voice of a new religious
Vision.

            And now the Gaiad must say something carefully. A
Common misconception treats {{p|Zoroastrianism}} as the original

Monotheism—the religion that invented the idea of a single
Supreme god and from which {{p|Judaism}}, {{p|Christianity}}, and {{p|Islam}}
Ultimately derive their monotheism. This account is partially true
But oversimplified. The Gaiad has already advanced, in its earlier

Treatment of comparative religious history, a more nuanced view:
That original {{p|Zoroastrianism}} was not strictly monotheistic in
The modern sense. It was dualistic. {{c|Ahura Mazda}}, the Wise Lord,
Was the supreme good deity, creator of truth and light and order.

But opposed to him was {{c|Angra Mainyu}} (later {{c|Ahriman}}), the
Destructive Spirit, who was a co-eternal principle of evil, lies,
Darkness, and disorder. The cosmos was not the creation of a
Single all-powerful god. It was the battleground between two

Cosmic principles. The good principle, {{c|Ahura Mazda}}, would
Eventually prevail, but only through the collaborative effort of
Humans aligning themselves with his side of the cosmic conflict.
This is dualism, not monotheism. And it is dualism of a specific

Type: ethical dualism, in which the fundamental cosmic opposition
Is between good and evil, truth and lie, order and chaos. The
Gods of prior traditions had been morally mixed—capable of great
Good and great evil, subject to passions and whims. {{c|Zarathustra}}'s

Theological innovation was to purify the supreme god of moral
Ambiguity. {{c|Ahura Mazda}} is unambiguously good. The moral
Ambiguity that exists in the world does not come from him; it
Comes from the opposing principle. This ethical sharpening—this

Assignment of cosmic meaning to the moral structure of existence—
Was {{c|Zarathustra}}'s great contribution. It would profoundly
Influence all subsequent {{p|Near Eastern}} and {{p|European}} religion,
Even when the dualism was later flattened into strict monotheism.

The Gaiad has already discussed how later {{p|Zoroastrianism}},
Particularly under the {{p|Sassanid}} empire, became more explicitly
Monotheistic, with {{c|Angra Mainyu}} increasingly described as a
Creature rather than a co-eternal principle. And the Gaiad has

Discussed how ancient {{p|Persian}} religious concepts shaped the
{{p|Jewish}} captivity experience in {{p|Babylon}} and the subsequent
Development of {{p|Second Temple}} {{p|Judaism}}. Here we focus on
{{c|Zarathustra}} himself—the reformer, the prophet, the voice speaking

From the {{p|Iranian}} plateau.

                                  What did {{c|Zarathustra}} teach?
The {{p|Gathas}}, the oldest {{p|Zoroastrian}} texts and the ones most
Likely to preserve his actual teachings, are seventeen hymns in

An archaic form of {{p|Avestan}}—a language closely related to early
{{p|Vedic}} {{p|Sanskrit}}. They are attributed directly to {{c|Zarathustra}}.
They are dense, difficult, ecstatic. And they articulate several
Fundamental ideas: That the cosmos is a moral battleground. That

Every human must choose, in every thought, word, and deed, between
{{c|Asha}} (truth, order, righteousness) and {{c|Druj}} (lie, disorder,
Wickedness). That the choice is real and consequential. That
{{c|Ahura Mazda}} is the supreme reality, worthy of worship, creator

Of all good things. That at the end of time, {{c|Ahura Mazda}} will
Triumph, evil will be eliminated, and a final judgment will
Distinguish the righteous from the wicked. That the righteous
Will be rewarded in a paradise; the wicked will suffer in a state

Of torment; and ultimately, after cosmic purification, all will
Be restored in a renewed creation. These ideas—final judgment,
Afterlife reward and punishment, cosmic restoration—were
Theologically novel in the {{p|Indo-Iranian}} world. They would

Migrate, centuries later, into {{p|Jewish}} apocalyptic literature,
Into {{p|Christian}} eschatology, into {{p|Islamic}} doctrine. The Last
Judgment, heaven and hell, the coming of a savior—these
Conceptions all have clear {{p|Zoroastrian}} antecedents. Whether

{{c|Zarathustra}} originated them wholly or synthesized them from
Earlier {{p|Iranian}} folk beliefs is debated. But he was the first
Known figure to articulate them in a systematic theology.

                                                                He
Also reformed ritual. The old {{p|Indo-Iranian}} religion, which the
Gaiad saw developing on the steppe and arriving in {{p|India}} with
The {{p|Aryans}}, centered on elaborate animal sacrifices conducted
By a hereditary priestly caste. {{c|Zarathustra}} reduced the emphasis

On animal sacrifice. He emphasized instead the sacred fire—
Maintained continuously in fire temples, symbolic of {{c|Ahura Mazda}}'s
Presence and of the principle of {{c|Asha}} in the world. He emphasized
Good thoughts, good words, good deeds as the core of religious

Practice. The old gods of the {{p|Indo-Iranian}} pantheon—{{c|Mithra}},
{{c|Anahita}}, various solar and storm deities—were either demoted to
Subordinate roles beneath {{c|Ahura Mazda}} or reinterpreted as
{{p|Amesha Spentas}} (holy immortals, something like angels). Some

Of them—notably {{c|Indra}}, who had been a central god in {{p|Vedic}}
Religion—were actively demonized, recast as {{p|daevas}} (false gods)
On the side of {{c|Angra Mainyu}}. This is why the word "daeva" has
Opposite valences in {{p|Sanskrit}} and {{p|Avestan}}: {{p|Sanskrit}} {{c|deva}} is

A good god, but {{p|Avestan}} {{c|daeva}} is an evil one. {{c|Zarathustra}}
Inverted the old pantheon, placing as good those beings that
Emphasized truth and moral order, placing as evil those beings
That emphasized power, warrior glory, and the older ecstatic

Fertility cult. The reform was thorough, and it split the
{{p|Indo-Iranian}} religious world permanently. {{p|Iranian}} peoples who
Accepted {{c|Zarathustra}}'s reform became {{p|Zoroastrians}}. {{p|Indo-Aryan}}
Peoples who did not became {{p|Hindus}}. The same starting tradition

Produced two distinct and ultimately opposed religious complexes.

                                                                 And
{{c|Zarathustra}} was, by his own account and by subsequent tradition,
A controversial reformer. He was initially rejected by his own

Community. His teachings were unwelcome. He traveled, seeking a
Patron who would hear him. He found one in {{c|King Vishtaspa}}—a
King probably of eastern {{p|Iran}}, possibly in what is now {{p|Afghanistan}}
Or {{p|Turkmenistan}}. {{c|Vishtaspa}} accepted {{c|Zarathustra}}'s message. His

Court converted. {{p|Zoroastrianism}} spread from {{c|Vishtaspa}}'s
Kingdom outward across the {{p|Iranian}} plateau over the following
Centuries. By the time the {{p|Medes}} and {{p|Persians}} began building
Their great empires in the first millennium BCE, {{p|Zoroastrianism}}

Had become the dominant religion of the {{p|Iranian}} world. {{c|Cyrus
The Great}}, {{c|Darius I}}, {{c|Xerxes}}, {{c|Artaxerxes}}—the {{p|Achaemenid}} kings
Of {{p|Persia}}—were all {{p|Zoroastrian}}, or at least enacted their
Kingship in {{p|Zoroastrian}} terms. Their inscriptions invoke

{{c|Ahura Mazda}} and pledge to uphold {{c|Asha}} and defeat {{c|Druj}}.
When the {{p|Achaemenid}} empire became the largest the world had
Yet seen—stretching from the {{p|Indus}} to the {{p|Aegean}} to {{p|Egypt}}—
{{p|Zoroastrianism}} became the religion of a vast multicultural imperial

Structure. It was the first world religion in the political sense,
The first ideology that commanded an empire spanning multiple
Languages, cultures, and ecological zones.

                                             And the {{p|Persian}}
Empire's encounter with {{p|Judea}} would prove theologically decisive.

When {{c|Cyrus}} conquered {{p|Babylon}} in five-thirty-nine BCE and found
The {{p|Jewish}} exiles there, he issued his famous decree permitting
Them to return to {{p|Jerusalem}} and rebuild their temple. He did
This, in {{p|Persian}} understanding, because it was {{c|Ahura Mazda}}'s

Will that subject peoples be restored to their proper homelands
And worship. {{c|Cyrus}} styled himself the liberator of the {{p|Jews}},
And the {{p|Jewish}} prophet {{c|Isaiah}}—or the anonymous author of
{{p|Deutero-Isaiah}}—went so far as to call {{c|Cyrus}} {{c|Yahweh}}'s

"Anointed one" ({{p|mashiach}}), a {{p|messianic}} title usually reserved
For legitimate {{p|Israelite}} kings. This is an astonishing theological
Generalization: a foreign {{p|Zoroastrian}} king given a {{p|Hebrew}}
{{p|messianic}} title by a {{p|Jewish}} prophet. It reflects the degree to

Which {{p|Zoroastrian}} and early post-exilic {{p|Jewish}} thought had
Converged in their understanding of cosmic morality, divine
Sovereignty, and the role of human agents in historical
Redemption. The {{p|Jews}} who returned from {{p|Babylon}} brought back

With them elements of {{p|Persian}} religious vocabulary and
Conceptual structure. Angels (a {{p|Persian}} innovation, previously
Absent from {{p|Hebrew}} tradition) began to populate {{p|Jewish}} scripture.
The figure of Satan, who in earlier {{p|Hebrew}} texts was a legitimate

Member of {{c|Yahweh}}'s divine council, became reinterpreted along
{{c|Angra Mainyu}} lines as a cosmic adversary. The concept of a
Final judgment, resurrection of the dead, and {{p|messianic}} age was
Absorbed into {{p|Jewish}} apocalyptic thought. By the time of the

Second Temple period and the emergence of {{p|Christianity}} and
{{p|Rabbinic Judaism}}, the {{p|Zoroastrian}} imprint on {{p|Jewish}} religious
Imagination was thorough. {{p|Christianity}} and {{p|Islam}}, inheriting
From {{p|Judaism}}, inherited also from {{p|Zoroastrianism}}. The Western

Monotheistic tradition as we know it is, in substantial measure,
A {{p|Zoroastrian}}-{{p|Jewish}} synthesis. Without {{c|Zarathustra}}, the
Religious history of the West would be unrecognizable.

                                                             And
Yet {{p|Zoroastrianism}} itself would, over time, retreat. The
{{p|Achaemenid}} empire fell to {{c|Alexander}} in the fourth century BCE.
The {{p|Seleucid}} and then {{p|Parthian}} successors continued {{p|Zoroastrianism}}
But in attenuated forms. The {{p|Sassanid}} empire restored {{p|Zoroastrianism}}

As the state religion and systematized its doctrines and texts
In the third and fourth centuries CE, codifying the {{p|Avesta}}. But
The {{p|Sassanid}} empire itself fell to the {{p|Arab}} {{p|Muslim}} conquest
In the seventh century CE. {{p|Iran}} was gradually {{p|Islamized}}.

{{p|Zoroastrians}} became a minority; many emigrated to {{p|India}}, where
Their descendants, the {{p|Parsis}}, still maintain the religion.
Others remained in {{p|Iran}} as a small, tolerated but marginalized
Community. Today there are perhaps two hundred thousand {{p|Zoroastrians}}

Worldwide—a tiny remnant of what had once been the state religion
Of the greatest empire of antiquity. But {{p|Zoroastrianism}}'s
Theological influence vastly exceeds its demographic footprint.
Every {{p|Christian}}, {{p|Muslim}}, {{p|Jewish}}, or secular {{p|Western}} thinker

Who believes in the ultimate triumph of good over evil, in the
Moral structure of the cosmos, in the final judgment of souls, in
Heaven and hell—every such thinker is, whether they know it or
Not, a theological descendant of {{c|Zarathustra}}. The prophet from

The {{p|Iranian}} plateau is a silent presence in half the religious
Imagination of the modern world.

                                     The Gaiad honors him. The
Gaiad recognizes in {{c|Zarathustra}}'s work a genuine theological

Innovation—the sharpening of moral dualism into an explicit
Cosmic principle, the linking of individual ethical choice to
Cosmic outcome, the articulation of a purposeful universe
Moving toward eschatological fulfillment. These are not small

Contributions. They are among the most influential religious
Ideas ever formulated. And they were formulated in obscurity,
By a prophet who had to flee his homeland to find a patron, whose
Teachings spread slowly and whose name would be forgotten outside

{{p|Iran}} for centuries, until {{p|Greek}} writers—{{c|Herodotus}}, {{c|Plutarch}},
Later the {{p|Hellenistic}} traditions attributing to {{c|Zoroaster}}
Every kind of ancient wisdom—brought his name into {{p|Western}}
Consciousness. In our own time {{c|Zarathustra}} has had a strange

Second life: as the protagonist of {{c|Nietzsche}}'s {{p|Thus Spoke Zarathustra}},
Where he becomes the mouthpiece for a nineteenth-century {{p|European}}
Critique of {{p|Christian}} morality. This usage is idiosyncratic, but
It reflects how {{c|Zarathustra}}'s name continues to carry weight as

A symbol of religious innovation and prophetic voice. {{c|Nietzsche}}
Chose the name not randomly but because it stood for the first
Great religious reformer whose work shaped all subsequent Western
Religion. {{c|Nietzsche}} wanted to undo that work and return to a

Pre-{{c|Zarathustran}} valuation. Whether one agrees with {{c|Nietzsche}}'s
Project or not, the acknowledgment of {{c|Zarathustra}}'s foundational
Role is accurate. {{c|Zarathustra}} is where a great deal of the
Modern Western religious and ethical imagination actually begins.

                                                                     {{c|Zarathustra}}.
{{c|Zoroaster}}. {{c|Ahura Mazda}} and {{c|Angra Mainyu}}. {{c|Asha}} and {{c|Druj}}. The fire
Temples and the ethical dualism. The purification of the supreme
God. Good thoughts, good words, good deeds. The final judgment

And the renewal of the cosmos. The {{p|Gathas}}. {{c|King Vishtaspa}}'s
Conversion. The {{p|Achaemenid}} adoption. {{c|Cyrus}} as {{c|Yahweh}}'s anointed.
The transmission into {{p|Jewish}} apocalyptic and {{p|Christian}} eschatology.
The {{p|Parsis}} in {{p|India}}. The long survival of a small religion

With enormous theological consequences.

                                           {{c|Zarathustra}}. The first
Prophet of the Axial Age. The reformer of {{p|Indo-Iranian}} religion.
The invisible ancestor of {{p|Western}} monotheism. Stand.
