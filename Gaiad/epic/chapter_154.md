# Chapter 154: Jacob, Joseph, and the Hyksos

And so the story shifts to a specific {{p|Canaanite}} family.

{{c|Jacob}}—grandson of {{c|Abraham}}, son of {{c|Isaac}}, father of twelve
Sons who will become the twelve tribes of {{p|Israel}}. {{c|Jacob}}'s
Biography is the central patriarchal narrative of the biblical
{{p|Genesis}}—the wrestling with the angel at {{p|Peniel}}, the ladder-dream

At {{p|Bethel}}, the servitude to his uncle {{c|Laban}}, the fraught
Reconciliation with his brother {{c|Esau}}, the favoritism toward
{{c|Rachel}}'s children over {{c|Leah}}'s, the grief over the supposed death
Of {{c|Joseph}}. These are stories well-known in the {{p|Jewish}}, {{p|Christian}},

And {{p|Islamic}} traditions, and the Gaiad will not rehearse them in
Detail. What the Gaiad does do, in this chapter, is place {{c|Jacob}}
And his family historically—in the late {{p|Middle Kingdom}} or the
Beginning of the {{p|Second Intermediate Period}}, around seventeen-

Hundred to sixteen-fifty BCE, in the context of the {{p|Hyksos}}
Arrival in {{p|Egypt}}. And the Gaiad makes a specific claim: {{c|Joseph}}'s
Rise to prominence in {{p|Egypt}} is legible precisely because he is
A {{p|Canaanite}}—a member of the same {{c|J}}-haplogroup {{p|Semitic}} population

That is, at this moment, gaining political power in the eastern
{{p|Nile Delta}}.

                      The biblical account has {{c|Joseph}} sold by his
Brothers to a caravan of {{p|Ishmaelite}} traders, who carry him down
To {{p|Egypt}} and sell him to {{c|Potiphar}}, a high-ranking {{p|Egyptian}}

Official. {{c|Joseph}} rises in {{c|Potiphar}}'s household. Is falsely
Accused by {{c|Potiphar}}'s wife. Is imprisoned. Interprets the dreams
Of two fellow prisoners. Is eventually brought before the pharaoh
To interpret the pharaoh's own dreams—of seven fat cows and seven

Lean cows, of seven full ears of grain and seven withered ears.
{{c|Joseph}} predicts seven years of abundance followed by seven years
Of famine. The pharaoh appoints him vizier—second only to the king
Himself—to manage the stockpiling and distribution of grain. When

The famine comes, {{p|Egypt}} has enough. The surrounding regions do
Not. {{c|Jacob}}'s remaining sons come down to {{p|Egypt}} to buy grain.
{{c|Joseph}} recognizes them. They do not recognize him. A dramatic
Recognition scene follows, after which {{c|Joseph}} brings his entire

Family down to {{p|Egypt}} and settles them in the region of {{p|Goshen}}—
The eastern {{p|Nile Delta}}, the pastoral lands best suited to their
Herding lifestyle.

                       And the Gaiad's historical overlay on this
Narrative is the {{p|Hyksos}} framework. The {{p|Hyksos}}—whose name in

{{p|Egyptian}} is {{c|Heka-khasut}}, "rulers of foreign lands"—were a
{{p|Semitic}} population (primarily {{c|J2}}-haplogroup, with some admixture)
Who had been migrating into the eastern {{p|Nile Delta}} throughout the
Late {{p|Middle Kingdom}}. They spoke a {{p|West Semitic}} language related

To early {{p|Canaanite}} and {{p|Amorite}}. They worshipped {{p|Semitic}} gods
Like {{c|Baal}} and {{c|Anat}}, which they syncretized with {{p|Egyptian}}
{{c|Seth}} (the god of the desert, storms, and foreign regions). Their
Culture was a hybrid—retaining {{p|Canaanite}} dress, names, diet, and

Religious practices while adopting {{p|Egyptian}} administrative forms,
Hieroglyphic writing, and some {{p|Egyptian}} cultic practices. They
Brought with them technologies that the {{p|Egyptians}} had not yet
Developed: the horse-drawn chariot, the composite bow, advanced

Bronze metallurgy. These technologies would, when the {{p|Egyptians}}
Eventually expelled the {{p|Hyksos}}, be retained and weaponized by the
{{p|New Kingdom}} pharaohs against their own neighbors.

Around seventeen-thirty to seventeen-twenty BCE, the {{p|Hyksos}}
Seized political control of the eastern delta and established the

{{p|Fifteenth Dynasty}} at {{p|Avaris}} (the site of modern {{p|Tell el-Dab'a}}).
The native {{p|Egyptian}} {{p|Thirteenth}} and {{p|Fourteenth Dynasties}} were
Displaced from the delta and retreated south. The native {{p|Seventeenth
Dynasty}} eventually established itself at {{p|Thebes}} as the {{p|Hyksos}}-

Resistance regime. For about a century and a half, {{p|Egypt}} was
Divided—the {{p|Hyksos}} ruling the delta, the native {{p|Egyptians}} ruling
{{p|Upper Egypt}}, with {{p|Nubian}} kingdoms further south occasionally
Allied with either side depending on strategic calculations.

And this is the world in which, the Gaiad argues, the biblical
{{c|Jacob}} and {{c|Joseph}} narratives take place. {{c|Joseph}}'s rise to
Vizier-level prominence is possible because he is a {{p|Canaanite}}—
And the pharaoh who appoints him is a {{p|Hyksos}} pharaoh. The

{{p|Hyksos}} rulers would naturally favor another {{p|Canaanite}} in their
Administration. {{c|Joseph}}'s brothers' migration into {{p|Egypt}} is
Possible because {{p|Canaanites}} are at this moment welcome in the
Delta—this is the century of active {{p|Canaanite}} settlement in

{{p|Lower Egypt}}. {{c|Jacob}}'s family settling in {{p|Goshen}} is the norm,
Not the exception; thousands of other {{p|Canaanite}} families were
Doing the same thing. The biblical narrative sits comfortably
Inside the {{p|Hyksos}} period.

And the Gaiad makes a further claim: the {{p|Hyksos}} themselves are
Not the villains that later {{p|Egyptian}} historiography made them out
To be. Later native {{p|Egyptian}} sources—particularly the {{p|New Kingdom}}
Accounts written after the {{p|Hyksos}} expulsion—would demonize them as

Brutal foreign invaders who destroyed temples, oppressed the native
Population, and plundered the wealth of {{p|Egypt}}. But archaeology
Tells a different story. The {{p|Hyksos}} at {{p|Avaris}} actively maintained
{{p|Egyptian}} administrative systems, built {{p|Egyptian}}-style temples

Alongside their own, patronized {{p|Egyptian}} scribal traditions,
Preserved {{p|Egyptian}} cultural practices in their territory. They
Were not destroyers—they were adapters. They governed {{p|Egypt}} as
Foreign rulers, yes, but competent and respectful foreign rulers.

Their rule was not a catastrophe; it was a transition. And the
{{c|Joseph}} story, in its biblical form, is consistent with this:
{{c|Joseph}} serves a pharaoh who welcomes {{p|Canaanites}}, who appoints
One as vizier, who settles an extended {{p|Canaanite}} family in the

Delta with royal favor. This is a {{p|Hyksos}}-era pharaoh, not a
Native {{p|Egyptian}} pharaoh. The later narrative demonization of the
{{p|Hyksos}} distorted the memory. The biblical narrative, which
Preserves the memory of {{p|Egyptian}}-{{p|Canaanite}} friendly relations,

Is closer to the archaeological reality than the later {{p|Egyptian}}
Propaganda is.

                    The {{p|Hyksos}} period lasted about a century and
A half. Six {{p|Hyksos}} pharaohs ruled the delta from {{p|Avaris}}.
Their names—preserved in {{c|Manetho}}'s later king-list and in

Archaeological seals—are {{p|Semitic}}: {{c|Salitis}}, {{c|Apophis}}, {{c|Khyan}},
{{c|Apepi}}. These are not native {{p|Egyptian}} names. They are {{p|Canaanite}}
Names transcribed into {{p|Egyptian}}. And they ruled the delta with
{{p|Egyptian}} administrative competence while maintaining {{p|Canaanite}}

Cultural identity. A model of dual-culture governance that the
Ancient {{p|Near East}} would see many times thereafter—in {{p|Mesopotamia}}
Under various {{p|Amorite}} and {{p|Kassite}} dynasties, in {{p|Mesopotamia}}
Under the {{p|Persians}}, in various later situations where a foreign

Dynasty adopted local administrative forms while retaining its
Own linguistic and religious identity.

                                             And the {{c|Jacob}} family—
The future Israelites—were, in the Gaiad's reading, a sub-population
Of this larger {{p|Canaanite}} presence in the {{p|Hyksos}}-controlled delta.

They came during the {{p|Hyksos}} period. They flourished during it.
They were welcomed, even privileged, during it. {{c|Joseph}}'s rise
Is legible against this background. And the specific tribal names
That the Gaiad has been tracking—{{c|Reuben}}, {{c|Simeon}}, {{c|Levi}}, {{c|Judah}},

{{c|Issachar}}, {{c|Zebulun}}, {{c|Dan}}, {{c|Naphtali}}, {{c|Gad}}, {{c|Asher}}, {{c|Joseph}}
(Later divided into {{c|Ephraim}} and {{c|Manasseh}}), {{c|Benjamin}}—are the
Twelve sons of {{c|Jacob}}, whose descendants will become the twelve
Tribes. At this moment, they are a single extended family living

In {{p|Goshen}}. Over the following centuries (during the {{p|Hyksos}}
Period and extending into the {{p|New Kingdom}}), they will multiply
Into a substantial population—still {{p|Canaanite}}-identified, still
Speaking a {{p|West Semitic}} language, still distinct from the native

{{p|Egyptian}} population but increasingly integrated into the {{p|Egyptian}}
Economy and administration.

                                    And when the {{p|Hyksos}} are finally
Expelled—around fifteen-fifty BCE, by the {{p|Theban}} pharaoh {{c|Ahmose I}},
Who will be the founder of the {{p|Eighteenth Dynasty}} and the inaugurator

Of the {{p|New Kingdom}}—the {{p|Canaanite}} population remaining in {{p|Egypt}}
Will face a transformed situation. Their political patrons will be
Gone. Their protected status will be revoked. And native {{p|Egyptian}}
Authority, restored with vengeful pride after a century of foreign

Domination, will begin to treat them as a suspect population. The
Friendly cohabitation of the {{p|Hyksos}} era will curdle into the
Apartheid of the {{p|New Kingdom}}. And the {{p|Israelite}} enslavement
That the biblical {{p|Exodus}} narrative describes becomes historically

Legible: it is what happens when a population that arrived under
Foreign-dynasty patronage continues to live in {{p|Egypt}} after the
Native dynasty returns to power. The {{c|Jacob}}-family memory of
Welcome and prosperity under the {{p|Hyksos}} pharaoh curdles, over

Several generations, into a memory of oppression under {{p|New
Kingdom}} pharaohs who "knew not {{c|Joseph}}." The biblical phrase—
"a new king arose over {{p|Egypt}} who knew not {{c|Joseph}}"—is, in the
Gaiad's reading, the moment when the {{p|Eighteenth Dynasty}} restoration

Ends the {{p|Canaanite}}-friendly era. The new king does not know
{{c|Joseph}} because {{c|Joseph}} served a {{p|Hyksos}} king, and the new
King has just expelled the {{p|Hyksos}} and does not recognize the
Legitimacy of any {{p|Hyksos}}-era appointment. {{c|Joseph}}'s memory is

Erased. His descendants are enslaved.

                                                But all of that is
Yet to come. The present chapter, ch 154, is set in the {{p|Hyksos}}
Phase itself. {{c|Jacob}} and his sons are flourishing in {{p|Goshen}}.

{{c|Joseph}} is the vizier. The pharaoh welcomes them. The future
Is full of possibility. The family lives in a bilingual,
Bicultural society where {{p|Canaanite}} traditions and {{p|Egyptian}}
Traditions coexist. And the story, for now, is one of arrival,

Of favor, of prosperity, of the extended family gathering around
The aged patriarch {{c|Jacob}} in his tent near the {{p|Nile Delta}},
Blessing his twelve sons and their descendants, pronouncing over
Each of them the prophetic words that will define the character

Of their future tribes. {{c|Judah}} is the lion. {{c|Joseph}} is the fruit-
Ful bough. {{c|Dan}} judges his people. {{c|Zebulun}} dwells by the sea.
{{c|Benjamin}} is the ravenous wolf. The blessings of {{p|Genesis 49}}—
Some of the oldest poetry in the {{p|Hebrew Bible}}—are pronounced.

And then {{c|Jacob}} dies at a great age, and his body is embalmed
By {{p|Egyptian}} embalmers and carried back to {{p|Canaan}} to be buried
In the {{p|Cave of Machpelah}} at {{p|Hebron}}, beside {{c|Abraham}} and
{{c|Isaac}}. The patriarchs are gathered to their fathers. The

Generation that founded the {{p|Israelite}} identity passes. The
Family remains in {{p|Egypt}}, multiplying, waiting for what will come.

{{c|Jacob}}. {{c|Joseph}}. The {{p|Hyksos}}. The {{p|Canaanite}} welcome in the delta.
The moment of prosperity before the reversal. The {{c|J}}-haplogroup
{{p|Semitic}} population flourishing under foreign-dynasty patronage
In a cosmopolitan {{p|Egypt}}. The twelve sons. The prophecies. The tent.

Stand.
