# Chapter 156: The New Kingdom

And the {{p|Hyksos}} were expelled.

Around fifteen-fifty BCE, {{c|Ahmose I}}, the {{p|Theban}} pharaoh of the
{{p|Eighteenth Dynasty}}, completed the campaign his elder brother
{{c|Kamose}} had begun. The {{p|Hyksos}} capital at {{p|Avaris}} was captured.
The {{p|Hyksos}} rulers and a portion of their population were expelled

Back into the {{p|Levant}}. Their fortress-city of {{p|Sharuhen}} in southern
{{p|Canaan}} was besieged for three years and finally taken. {{p|Egypt}} was
Reunified under native {{p|Egyptian}} rule. The {{p|New Kingdom}} had begun.

But the {{p|Egypt}} that emerged from the {{p|Hyksos}} expulsion was
Transformed. It had learned, during the century and a half of
{{p|Hyksos}} rule, the chariot, the composite bow, bronze-age military
Technology that the native {{p|Egyptian}} dynasties had not previously

Possessed. The humiliation of foreign rule had bred a determination
Never to let such a thing happen again. The {{p|New Kingdom}} {{p|Egypt}}
Was militarized, outward-looking, imperial. For the first time in
Its history, {{p|Egypt}} projected power far beyond its traditional

{{p|Nile Valley}} borders. {{p|Egyptian}} armies campaigned in {{p|Canaan}}, in
{{p|Syria}}, as far north as the {{p|Euphrates}} under {{c|Thutmose I}}. They
Campaigned south into {{p|Nubia}}, where they established direct
{{p|Egyptian}} administration over what had previously been nominally

Tributary regions. The {{p|Egyptian}} Empire—the first real {{p|Egyptian}}
Empire in the full imperial sense—was born.

The {{p|Eighteenth Dynasty}} was the great age of this expansion.
{{c|Ahmose I}}, {{c|Amenhotep I}}, {{c|Thutmose I}}, {{c|Thutmose II}}—then

{{c|Hatshepsut}}. One of the most remarkable figures in {{p|Egyptian}}
History. Daughter of {{c|Thutmose I}}, wife and half-sister of
{{c|Thutmose II}}, stepmother of the young {{c|Thutmose III}}. When
{{c|Thutmose II}} died, {{c|Hatshepsut}} initially served as regent for her

Young stepson. But within a few years, she took the title of
Pharaoh herself—a female pharaoh, ruling in her own right, in a
Culture that had almost no precedent for female kingship. She
Reigned for approximately twenty-two years. She was astonishingly

Successful. She prioritized trade over warfare—the famous expedition
To {{p|Punt}} (on the {{p|Red Sea}} coast), which brought back myrrh-trees,
Incense, exotic animals, and African wealth, was {{c|Hatshepsut}}'s
Crowning diplomatic achievement. She built one of the most beautiful

Mortuary temples in {{p|Egypt}}—{{p|Deir el-Bahari}}, on the western bank
Of the {{p|Nile}} opposite {{p|Thebes}}, a terraced colonnaded structure
Cut into the cliff-face. Her sculptures often depicted her with a
Ceremonial false beard (the standard iconography of pharaonic

Kingship), but her feminine features were also retained. She
Navigated the gender-contradictions of her role with a sophisticated
Adaptive strategy. And when she died, {{c|Thutmose III}}—now an adult—
Took the throne. Years later, {{c|Thutmose III}} or his successors

Attempted to erase {{c|Hatshepsut}}'s memory: her images were chiseled
Off public monuments, her cartouches defaced. This was not entirely
Successful. She was rediscovered by modern {{p|Egyptologists}}, and her
Full stature as one of the greatest pharaohs of the {{p|New Kingdom}}

Has been restored.

                         {{c|Thutmose III}} himself was the military
Genius of the dynasty. He conducted seventeen campaigns into the
{{p|Levant}} and {{p|Syria}}, consolidating {{p|Egyptian}} control over the entire

Region. His greatest victory, at {{p|Megiddo}} (the site whose name
Would become, in {{p|Greek}}, "{{p|Armageddon}}" in the {{p|Book of Revelation}}),
Defeated a coalition of {{p|Canaanite}} kings and established {{p|Egyptian}}
Supremacy over the {{p|Levant}} for the next century. {{c|Thutmose III}} is

Sometimes called "{{p|The Napoleon of Egypt}}" by modern historians—a
Tactically brilliant field commander who consolidated an empire.

But the {{p|New Kingdom}}'s expansion and militarization created a
Problem at home. The {{p|Amun}} priesthood—centered at {{p|Thebes}}, the

Dynastic capital—grew enormously wealthy on the spoils of empire.
Tribute from conquered territories poured into the temple of
{{c|Amun-Ra}} at {{p|Karnak}}. The {{p|Amun}} priesthood's power approached
Rivalry with the royal court. And it is in this context—the tension

Between the royal authority and the entrenched {{p|Amun}} priesthood—
That {{c|Akhenaten}} rose to the throne.

                                                  {{c|Akhenaten}}, born
{{c|Amenhotep IV}}, was the son of {{c|Amenhotep III}} and grandson of
{{c|Thutmose IV}}. He reigned from approximately thirteen-fifty-three

To thirteen-thirty-six BCE. And he attempted something that no
Previous pharaoh had attempted: to abolish the traditional {{p|Egyptian}}
Pantheon and replace it with the worship of a single god—the sun-
Disk, {{c|Aten}}—with himself as the sole intermediary between the

Divine and humanity. This is the famous "{{c|Aten}} heresy" or
"{{c|Amarna}} revolution"—sometimes celebrated in popular history as the
First monotheism. The Gaiad's reading is more skeptical. {{c|Akhenaten}}'s
Religious innovation was not quite monotheism in the {{c|Abrahamic}}

Sense. It retained cosmic elements of the traditional {{p|Egyptian}}
Religion. It was, primarily, a political move—an attempt to break
The {{p|Amun}} priesthood's power by redirecting religious authority
To a sun-disk that only the pharaoh had privileged access to.

{{c|Akhenaten}} built a new capital, {{p|Akhetaten}} (modern {{p|Amarna}}),
In middle {{p|Egypt}}. He moved the royal court there. He closed temples
Of {{c|Amun}} and other traditional gods. He persecuted priests of the
Old religion. And he composed, in his own hand, hymns to the

{{c|Aten}}—the {{p|Great Hymn to the Aten}}—that celebrate the sun as
Creator of all life. (Interestingly, the {{p|Great Hymn to the Aten}}
Shares striking similarities with {{p|Psalm 104}} of the {{p|Hebrew
Bible}}—a parallel that has led some scholars to argue that {{p|Hebrew}}

Monotheism was influenced by or descended from {{c|Akhenaten}}'s
Earlier experiment. The Gaiad takes a careful position on this:
The parallel is real, but the historical transmission is murky,
And the {{p|Hebrew}} tradition had its own independent development.)

And here the Gaiad makes a specific and possibly provocative claim.
{{c|Akhenaten}}, despite his reputation in modern pop-religion as a
"Proto-monotheist hero," was—in the Gaiad's reading—the pharaoh
Who persecuted the {{p|Israelites}}. The "{{p|new king who knew not

Joseph}}" of the {{p|Exodus}} narrative may be, in the Gaiad's reading,
{{c|Akhenaten}} himself. Why? Because {{c|Akhenaten}}'s religious revolution
Was anti-foreign-cult. In closing down the temples of the traditional
{{p|Egyptian}} gods, he also closed down the temples of the {{p|Semitic}}

Gods who had been worshipped in {{p|Egypt}} by the remaining {{p|Canaanite}}
Population. The {{p|Israelites}} in {{p|Goshen}}—descendants of {{c|Jacob}}'s
Family, still preserving their own {{p|Semitic}} religious identity—
Found themselves religiously suppressed under {{c|Akhenaten}}'s regime.

The irony is striking: {{c|Akhenaten}}, celebrated by modern writers
As the first monotheist, was in the Gaiad's reading the active
Persecutor of the {{p|Israelites}}, whose descendants would develop
The most important monotheism in subsequent world history. The

Timing also roughly works: {{c|Akhenaten}}'s reign (thirteen-fifty-three
To thirteen-thirty-six BCE) is approximately the right period for
The biblical {{p|Exodus}} tradition to have its historical kernel,
Though the biblical narrative compresses and distorts the timeline.

After {{c|Akhenaten}}'s death, the {{p|Amarna}} revolution collapsed quickly.
His son-in-law (or possibly son) {{c|Tutankhamun}} (originally
{{c|Tutankhaten}}) changed his name to honor {{c|Amun}}, moved the court
Back to {{p|Thebes}}, and restored the traditional cults. {{c|Tutankhamun}}

Is famous in modern times almost exclusively because of the
Discovery of his intact tomb by {{c|Howard Carter}} in nineteen
Twenty-two—the unparalleled treasure-hoard that survived because
The tomb had been lost under the debris of later construction.

In his own time, {{c|Tutankhamun}} was a minor pharaoh who reigned
Briefly and died young (probably from a combination of genetic
Disorders resulting from the royal family's extreme inbreeding,
Plus an accidental injury). The fame is modern. The reign was

Not particularly consequential. After {{c|Tutankhamun}}, the {{p|Eighteenth
Dynasty}} ended in administrative confusion and was succeeded by
The {{p|Nineteenth Dynasty}}, founded by the general {{c|Ramesses I}} and
Including the great {{c|Seti I}} and the even greater {{c|Ramesses II}}.

{{c|Ramesses II}}—"{{c|Ramesses the Great}}"—reigned for sixty-six years
(twelve-seventy-nine to twelve-thirteen BCE), fought the {{p|Battle
Of Kadesh}} against the {{p|Hittites}} (the largest chariot battle in
Human history up to that point, with about five thousand chariots

Engaged), and eventually signed the first known international
Peace treaty in human history with the {{p|Hittite}} king {{c|Hattusili III}}.
The {{p|Egyptian-Hittite Peace Treaty}} (c. 1259 BCE) was so important
That copies were kept in both capitals, and a version engraved in

{{p|Hittite}} on silver tablets was carried to {{p|Thebes}} and deposited
In the temple of {{c|Amun}}. A copy is now in the {{p|United Nations}}
Building in {{p|New York}}—the world's first peace treaty, held up as
The precedent for the modern international order.

{{c|Ramesses II}} built on a scale that rivaled {{c|Khufu}} of the {{p|Old
Kingdom}}: the great temples at {{p|Abu Simbel}}, carved into a sandstone
Cliff in {{p|Nubia}}, with four massive seated statues of the pharaoh
Sixty-five feet high guarding the entrance. The {{p|Ramesseum}}—his

Mortuary temple on the west bank at {{p|Thebes}}. The additions to
{{p|Karnak}} and {{p|Luxor}}. {{c|Ramesses II}} was the last great builder-pharaoh
Of the pre-{{p|Bronze Age Collapse}} era. And then, toward the end of
His long reign, things began to destabilize. Sea peoples appeared

On the horizon. {{p|Mediterranean}} maritime raiders began harassing
The {{p|Egyptian}} coast. The climate was shifting again. And after
{{c|Ramesses II}}'s death, {{p|Egypt}} would face, during the reign of his
Son {{c|Merneptah}} and grandsons, the first major waves of the {{p|Sea

Peoples}}—the great migrations that would contribute to the
{{p|Bronze Age Collapse}} of around twelve-hundred BCE. The {{p|New
Kingdom}}'s long expansion was ending. The system that had made
{{c|Ramesses}}'s grandeur possible was straining toward its final

Failure. And the {{p|Exodus}} narrative—which the Gaiad reads as
The {{p|Israelite}} departure from {{p|Egypt}} in the late {{p|Nineteenth
Dynasty}} or early {{p|Twentieth Dynasty}}—fits into this period of
Growing instability.

                         But that is the next chapter. This chapter
Is the {{p|New Kingdom}} in its full expansionary glory: {{c|Hatshepsut}}'s
Diplomatic triumphs, {{c|Thutmose III}}'s military conquests, {{c|Akhenaten}}'s
Religious revolution and persecution of the {{p|Israelites}}, {{c|Ramesses II}}'s

Sixty-six-year reign and his peace treaty with the {{p|Hittites}}. The
{{p|Eighteenth}} and {{p|Nineteenth Dynasties}}. The {{p|Egyptian}} Empire at
Its peak. The monumental architecture, the international diplomacy,
The religious upheaval, and the slowly tightening pressures that

Would eventually bring the Bronze Age itself crashing down.
