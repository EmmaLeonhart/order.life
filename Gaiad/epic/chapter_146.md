# Chapter 146: The Akkadian Empire

While the pyramids were rising at {{p|Giza}}, to the east and
Across the {{p|Sinai}} and over the {{p|Arabian}} desert, in the
Valley of the two great rivers, a different kind of power
Was consolidating. Not a pyramid-power. Not a tomb-architecture

Of divine kingship. A military power. An imperial power. The
First empire in human history. The empire of {{c|Sargon of Akkad}}.

Before {{c|Sargon}}, {{p|Mesopotamia}} was a landscape of competing
City-states. {{p|Uruk}}, {{p|Ur}}, {{p|Lagash}}, {{p|Kish}}, {{p|Umma}}, {{p|Nippur}}, {{p|Eridu}}—

Each a walled city with its own ruler, its own patron deity,
Its own patch of irrigated farmland. These cities had been
In existence for a thousand years already. {{p|Sumerian}} civilization
Had invented writing (cuneiform on clay tablets), the wheel

(For pottery and for carts), bronze metallurgy, the mathematical
Base-sixty system that still defines how we measure time and angles,
And the political form of the city-state ruled by a {{c|lugal}} (king)
Or {{c|ensi}} (governor-priest). The cities warred with each other,

Traded with each other, and occasionally formed short-lived
Hegemonies when one king managed to subdue the others. The
Legendary {{c|Gilgamesh}} of {{p|Uruk}}—the historical king on whom the
Epic poem would eventually be based—ruled during this period.

{{c|Lugalzagesi}} of {{p|Umma}} briefly united much of {{p|Sumer}} in the
Twenty-fourth century BCE. But no unification lasted long, and
No empire—in the sense of a multi-regional political entity
Ruled from a single center—had yet been built.

{{c|Sargon}} changed that.

                              {{c|Sargon}}'s origin story is already
Legendary: a foundling, abandoned in a reed basket on the river
By his mother, rescued by a gardener, rising to become cupbearer

To the king of {{p|Kish}}, and then—by a coup or by a succession
Crisis, depending on the account—becoming king himself. (The
Reed-basket-on-the-river motif will appear again, later, in the
Biblical {{c|Moses}} story. The parallel is not coincidental. The

Motif is a {{p|Near Eastern}} commonplace that signals foundling-to-
King destiny.) {{c|Sargon}}'s name means "the true king" or "the
Legitimate king"—a throne-name, taken at his accession, designed
To declare that he was the rightful ruler despite his non-royal

Origins. He then began to conquer. First the other {{p|Sumerian}}
Cities. Then the regions to the north—{{p|Akkad}}, which would become
His imperial base. Then further afield: {{p|Elam}} to the east,
{{p|Syria}} and {{p|Anatolia}} to the north, and as far as the {{p|Mediterranean

Coast}} of what is now {{p|Lebanon}}. {{c|Sargon}} claimed to have washed
His weapons in the {{p|Upper Sea}} (the {{p|Mediterranean}}) and the {{p|Lower
Sea}} (the {{p|Persian Gulf}}). The claim, if not literally true, was
Culturally decisive. {{c|Sargon}} had conquered more territory than

Any previous ruler in history. The {{p|Akkadian Empire}} was born.

And its capital city, {{p|Akkad}}, was founded somewhere along the
{{p|Tigris}} or {{p|Euphrates}}—the exact location has never been rediscovered.
{{p|Akkad}} is the lost imperial capital. One of the most important

Cities in the ancient world, ruled by one of the most consequential
Dynasties, and we do not know where it stood. The site has been
Buried or eroded or overbuilt so thoroughly that {{p|Akkad}} is, as
Far as modern archaeology is concerned, a name without a location.

{{c|Sargon}} ruled for fifty-six years. His reign was marked by
Military campaigns, administrative consolidation, and the
Imposition of {{p|Akkadian}} (a {{p|Semitic}} language, carried by
{{c|J}}-haplogroup populations) as the language of empire, gradually

Displacing {{p|Sumerian}} (a language isolate) in common use. {{c|Sargon}}'s
Daughter {{c|Enheduanna}} was installed as high priestess of the
Moon-god {{c|Nanna}} at {{p|Ur}}—and {{c|Enheduanna}} is, incredibly, the
First author whose name has survived in human history. Her

Hymns to {{c|Inanna}}—the goddess of love and war, {{p|Sumerian}} counterpart
Of the later {{p|Semitic}} {{c|Ishtar}}—are the oldest signed literary
Works that exist. {{c|Enheduanna}} is the first named author. The first
Woman in the historical record whose intellectual labor was

Acknowledged, named, and preserved. The daughter of the first
Emperor is also the first author. And her hymns—particularly "{{p|Nin-me-
Šara}}" ("{{p|The Exaltation of Inanna}}")—are still read and studied
Four thousand three hundred years later.

                                                {{c|Sargon}} was succeeded
By his sons {{c|Rimush}} and {{c|Manishtushu}}, both of whom faced rebellions
And assassinations, and then by his grandson {{c|Naram-Sin}}, who took
The empire to its apex. {{c|Naram-Sin}} ruled for approximately thirty-six

Years. He expanded the empire further, campaigned widely, and
Famously declared himself a god—the first {{p|Mesopotamian}} king to
Claim divinity during his own lifetime, a precedent that would
Echo through later {{p|Near Eastern}} kingship and ultimately into the

{{p|Roman}} imperial cult. The {{p|Victory Stele of Naram-Sin}}, carved
Around twenty-two hundred and fifty BCE, depicts him crossing a
Mountain as a divine warrior, trampling enemies beneath his feet,
With the solar and astral symbols of the gods above him. It is

One of the masterpieces of ancient {{p|Near Eastern}} art. And it
Marks the peak of the {{p|Akkadian Empire}}.

                                                  After {{c|Naram-Sin}}, the
Empire declined. His son {{c|Shar-Kali-Sharri}} faced ongoing revolts

And a weakening economic base. And then—around twenty-one fifty
BCE—the empire collapsed. Two forces contributed. The first was
Internal: the economic and political infrastructure of the empire
Had overextended. The second was external: invaders from the

{{p|Zagros Mountains}}, a people called the {{p|Gutians}}, descended into
{{p|Mesopotamia}} and overran the weakened imperial structure. The
{{p|Gutians}} were, according to the {{p|Sumerian}} records, "barbarians"—
A disparaging word that {{p|Mesopotamian}} civilization would apply

Repeatedly to mountain peoples who invaded from the east. They
Ruled {{p|Mesopotamia}} for approximately a century, badly and
Chaotically, until they were expelled by a resurgent {{p|Sumerian}}
Dynasty centered on the city of {{p|Uruk}} (the {{p|Fourth Dynasty of

Uruk}}) and then by the {{p|Third Dynasty of Ur}}, which would
Re-establish {{p|Sumerian}}-language rule and usher in the {{p|Neo-Sumerian}}
Period. That dynasty—{{p|Ur III}}—is the one in which {{c|Abraham}}, in
The Gaiad's reading, will be born. But that is a later chapter.

For this chapter, the focus is the {{p|Akkadian Empire}} itself—the
First multi-regional empire in human history, the first state to
Claim suzerainty over multiple cultures and languages, the first
To institutionalize the mechanisms of imperial administration

That would be refined by every subsequent empire of the ancient
World. The {{p|Akkadian Empire}}'s innovations: a standing army
Recruited from across the empire; provincial governors appointed
By the imperial center; a common imperial language ({{p|Akkadian}});

Standardized weights and measures; an imperial road system; a
Unified taxation regime; and the title of "{{c|King of the Four
Quarters}}" that claimed universal sovereignty over the four
Corners of the earth. These innovations would be inherited by

The {{p|Babylonian Empire}}, the {{p|Assyrian Empire}}, the {{p|Persian Empire}},
The {{p|Macedonian Empire}} of {{c|Alexander}}, the {{p|Roman Empire}}, and
Every subsequent imperial structure in the {{p|Old World}}. {{c|Sargon}}
Is the prototype of the emperor. The word "{{c|Sargon}}" is itself,

Via various linguistic routes, probably the root of the word
"{{c|Caesar}}" and the broader {{p|Indo-European}} vocabulary for kingship.
Every emperor is, in some sense, a descendant of {{c|Sargon of Akkad}}.

The fall of the {{p|Akkadian Empire}} around twenty-one fifty BCE is,
As mentioned in the {{p|Old Kingdom}} chapter, part of the broader
{{p|4.2-kiloyear climate event}}. A severe multi-century drought that
Weakened both the {{p|Nile}} flood and the {{p|Tigris-Euphrates}} system

Simultaneously. The {{p|Egyptian First Intermediate Period}} and the
{{p|Gutian}} collapse of the {{p|Akkadian Empire}} are the two faces of
The same climate crisis. Both civilizations survived the crisis—
{{p|Egypt}} reassembled in the {{p|Middle Kingdom}}, {{p|Mesopotamia}}

Reassembled in {{p|Ur III}}—but both were chastened. The climate
Had taught them that no empire, however well-organized, was safe
From the weather.

                       And the {{p|Akkadian Empire}} left its mark
On the mythology of the later {{p|Near East}}. The {{p|Curse of Akkad}},

A {{p|Sumerian}} literary text, narrates the fall of the empire as a
Punishment for {{c|Naram-Sin}}'s sacrilege—his destruction of the
Temple of {{c|Enlil}} at {{p|Nippur}}. The gods, enraged, summoned the
{{p|Gutian}} barbarians to destroy the empire. The fall is made sense

Of as a moral failure. Hubris leads to divine retribution. The
{{p|Gutian}} invasion is not a climate event; it is the judgment of
The gods on an emperor who overstepped. This is a theological
Reading that will recur throughout {{p|Near Eastern}} historiography:

Empires fall because their kings sinned. It is the same reading
The {{p|Hebrew Bible}} will apply to the kings of {{p|Israel}} and {{p|Judah}}.
It is the same reading the {{p|Greeks}} will apply to {{c|Xerxes}} at
{{p|Salamis}}. The cosmological pattern is already present in the

{{p|Akkadian}} material: the divine order of the world punishes the
Proud emperor, and the empire falls.

                                           And {{c|Sargon}} himself
Becomes, over the following millennia, a legendary figure. The

{{p|Sargon Legend}} circulates in cuneiform manuscripts for the next
Two thousand years. {{c|Sargon}} is remembered as the archetypal
Conqueror, the self-made king, the foundling who became an emperor.
Later kings, including the Neo-Assyrian {{c|Sargon II}} in the eighth

Century BCE, would take his name in order to claim his legacy.
The cultural memory of the first emperor outlasted the archaeological
Record of his capital city. We lost {{p|Akkad}}. We did not lose
{{c|Sargon}}.

            {{p|Akkadian Empire}}. {{c|Sargon}}. {{c|Naram-Sin}}. {{c|Enheduanna}}.
The first empire. The first author. The first divine-king claim.
The first prototype of the imperial form that every subsequent
Empire would elaborate. And the first great collapse—by climate,

By barbarian invasion, by internal exhaustion—of the imperial project.

The {{p|Gutians}} came down from the mountains. The cities burned.
The empire ended. And {{p|Mesopotamia}}, having invented the empire,
Spent the next three thousand years failing to make any single

Empire last. {{p|Akkad}}. {{p|Ur III}}. {{p|Old Babylon}}. {{p|Assyria}}. {{p|Neo-Babylon}}.
{{p|Persia}}. {{p|Seleucid}}. {{p|Parthian}}. {{p|Sassanid}}. {{p|Caliphate}}. Empire after
Empire. Each inheriting the template {{c|Sargon}} had established.
Each, eventually, falling. The cycle begins here.

{{c|Sargon}}. {{p|Akkad}}. The first empire. Stand.
