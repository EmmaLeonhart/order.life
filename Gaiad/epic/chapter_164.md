# Chapter 164: David

And back to the {{p|Levant}}. Back to {{p|Israel}}, newly emerged from the
Bronze Age Collapse, settling in the highlands of {{p|Canaan}} and
Struggling to establish itself as a coherent nation among hostile
Neighbors.

             The biblical narrative presents the early history of
{{p|Israel}} as a sequence: {{c|Joshua}}'s conquest, the period of the
{{c|Judges}}, the kingship of {{c|Saul}}, the kingship of {{c|David}}, the kingship
Of {{c|Solomon}}, and then the division of the kingdom into {{p|Israel}}

(North) and {{p|Judah}} (South). The historical accuracy of this
Narrative is much debated. What archaeologists find in the {{p|Canaanite}}
Highlands during the relevant period (roughly twelve-hundred to
One-thousand BCE) is a proliferation of small agricultural villages

With distinctive architectural and dietary markers—four-room
Houses, collar-rim storage jars, an absence of pig bones. These
Are taken as archaeological indicators of the emerging {{p|Israelite}}
Ethnic identity. The villages are numerous but small; there are

No great palaces, no monumental architecture, no clear signs of
Centralized authority. This fits an early period of tribal
Confederation—the period of the {{c|Judges}}—better than it fits a
Highly developed monarchy. Then, around the tenth century BCE,

There are signs of increased political organization: larger sites,
Some administrative architecture, the beginning of a state
Apparatus. This is the period that the biblical text associates
With {{c|Saul}}, {{c|David}}, and {{c|Solomon}}—the {{p|United Monarchy}}. Whether

The {{p|United Monarchy}} was as grand as the {{p|Hebrew Bible}} claims—
With {{c|Solomon}}'s fabled wealth and wisdom, his vast temple, his
Empire stretching from the {{p|Euphrates}} to the border of {{p|Egypt}}—
Is disputed. Many scholars ("minimalists") argue that {{c|David}} and

{{c|Solomon}} were, at most, local chieftains whose historical
Importance was inflated by later {{p|Judahite}} court propaganda. Other
Scholars ("maximalists") argue that the biblical account reflects
A real, if perhaps geographically limited, polity. The discovery

Of the {{p|Tel Dan Stele}} in nineteen-ninety-three provided what
Most now consider the first extra-biblical attestation of {{c|David}}:
An {{p|Aramaic}} inscription from the ninth century BCE, commissioned
By an {{p|Aramean}} king (probably {{c|Hazael of Aram}}), boasting of defeating

The "House of {{c|David}}" ({{p|bytdwd}}). This proves, at minimum, that by
The ninth century BCE there was a {{p|Judahite}} royal dynasty claiming
Descent from someone named {{c|David}}. Some historical {{c|David}} existed,
Founder of a dynasty. The Gaiad accepts this and proceeds.

What the {{p|Hebrew Bible}} tells us about {{c|David}} is the most intensely
Detailed human portrait of any pre-Axial historical figure. We
Have his military exploits, his psychological turmoil, his political
Calculations, his family's dysfunctions, his personal faith, his

Moral failures, his artistic sensitivity. No other ancient {{p|Near Eastern}}
Monarch is portrayed with anything like this level of interiority.
Even the actual historical {{c|David}}, however modest his realm, must
Have been an extraordinary figure to generate this literary response.

And what a literary response it is. The books of {{p|1}} and {{p|2 Samuel}},
Especially the so-called "Court History" or "Succession Narrative"
(Chapters nine through twenty of {{p|2 Samuel}} and the first two
Chapters of {{p|1 Kings}}), constitute one of the great works of ancient

Prose. Their narrative economy, psychological insight, and moral
Complexity have been recognized as foundational by critics as
Diverse as {{c|Erich Auerbach}}, {{c|Robert Alter}}, {{c|Meir Sternberg}}, and
{{c|Harold Bloom}}. The author of the Court History stands alongside

{{c|Homer}} as one of the two great literary pioneers of the ancient
{{p|Near East}}, and unlike {{c|Homer}}'s world of kings and gods, the Court
History presents a world of intricate human motivation in which
Divine intervention is present but elusive. The prose is

Revolutionary. It treats {{c|David}} not as a two-dimensional hero but
As a man of profound ambiguities—brave and calculating, faithful
And faithless, tender and cruel, a poet and a murderer. No single
Figure in the {{p|Hebrew Bible}} is portrayed with such complexity. And

No figure has been more influential on {{p|Western}} literary
Imagination. Every subsequent portrait of a complex monarch—
{{c|Shakespeare}}'s {{c|Henry IV}}, {{c|Hamlet}}'s troubled father, every tormented
King in the {{p|Western}} tradition—traces ancestry, in some measure,

To the {{c|David}} of the Court History.

                                          Let us narrate {{c|David}}'s
Life as the text gives it. {{c|David}} is the youngest son of {{c|Jesse}}
Of {{p|Bethlehem}}, of the tribe of {{p|Judah}}. He is a shepherd boy, of

Ruddy appearance and beautiful eyes. He is also, the text tells
Us, a skilled musician—a lyre-player whose music can soothe
Troubled spirits. And it is this skill that first brings him into
Contact with {{c|King Saul}}. {{c|Saul}}, the first king of {{p|Israel}},

Suffers from a "tormenting spirit" (we might call it depression
Or perhaps something more severe), and his attendants procure
{{c|David}} to play for him and calm him. {{c|David}} becomes attached to
{{c|Saul}}'s court. Then comes the famous encounter with {{c|Goliath}}, the

{{p|Philistine}} giant. The {{p|Philistines}}—the {{p|Sea Peoples}} who had
Settled the southern coast of {{p|Canaan}} during the Bronze Age
Collapse—are {{p|Israel}}'s principal enemy in this period. Their
Champion {{c|Goliath}} challenges any {{p|Israelite}} to single combat.

{{c|David}}, too young to bear full armor, accepts the challenge armed
Only with a sling and stones, and kills {{c|Goliath}} with a single
Shot to the forehead. The {{p|Philistine}} army flees. {{c|David}} becomes
A national hero, a figure greater in popular acclaim than {{c|Saul}}

Himself. And from this moment, {{c|Saul}}'s relationship with {{c|David}}
Becomes fatally ambivalent. {{c|Saul}} loves him as a son, has him
Marry his daughter {{c|Michal}}, and yet fears him as a rival. The
Text is explicit about the psychological oscillation: {{c|Saul}}

Repeatedly tries to kill {{c|David}}, relents in moments of clarity,
Then tries again. {{c|David}} flees into the wilderness, gathering a
Band of outcast warriors, living as a bandit-chief in the
{{p|Judean}} wilderness. Twice during this period {{c|David}} has {{c|Saul}}

At his mercy, and twice {{c|David}} spares him. {{c|David}}'s dharma (we
Might say, borrowing the {{p|Sanskrit}} term from the {{p|Ramayana}}
Chapter) will not allow him to strike down "{{c|Yahweh}}'s anointed,"
Even though that anointed one seeks his life. {{c|David}} waits. He

Serves as a mercenary under the {{p|Philistine}} king {{c|Achish of Gath}}
For a time—a morally complicated maneuver in which {{c|David}}
Pretends to raid {{p|Israelite}} settlements while actually raiding
Other targets, preserving his {{p|Israelite}} loyalty while eating

{{p|Philistine}} bread. The {{p|Hebrew}} narrator does not try to clean
This up. {{c|David}}'s exile is morally murky. He is a survivor.
Then {{c|Saul}} and his sons, including {{c|David}}'s beloved {{c|Jonathan}},
Are killed at the {{p|Battle of Mount Gilboa}} against the {{p|Philistines}}.

{{c|Saul}}, wounded and facing capture, falls on his own sword. {{c|Jonathan}}
Is slain beside him. {{c|David}} mourns them in one of the most moving
Elegies in world literature ({{p|2 Samuel}} chapter one): "How are
The mighty fallen, and the weapons of war perished!" The love

Between {{c|David}} and {{c|Jonathan}}—celebrated in the text as "passing
The love of women"—has been interpreted variously through the
Centuries. Some commentators see it as a profound friendship,
Some as an expression of same-sex romantic love, some as

Politically tinged loyalty between a prince and his designated
Successor. The text does not disambiguate. What it gives us is
A love so deep that {{c|David}}'s grief echoes through the centuries.

After {{c|Saul}}'s death, {{c|David}} becomes king—first of {{p|Judah}} alone,
With his capital at {{p|Hebron}}, then, after a civil war with {{c|Saul}}'s
Surviving son {{c|Ish-Bosheth}}, of all {{p|Israel}}. He captures the
{{p|Jebusite}} fortress of {{p|Jerusalem}} and makes it his capital—a

Politically astute choice, because {{p|Jerusalem}} was neutral territory
Belonging to neither the northern nor southern tribes. {{c|David}}
Centralizes the monarchy. He brings the {{p|Ark of the Covenant}},
The sacred chest containing the covenant tablets, into {{p|Jerusalem}},

Dancing ecstatically before it (to the disgust of {{c|Michal}}, who
Sees his behavior as undignified). He conducts a series of
Military campaigns against the {{p|Philistines}}, {{p|Moabites}}, {{p|Ammonites}},
{{p|Edomites}}, and {{p|Arameans}}, extending {{p|Israelite}} power. He builds

A substantial kingdom, though probably not the empire the later
Tradition imagines. And at the height of his power, he commits
The great sin that will shape his legacy: he takes {{c|Bathsheba}},
The wife of {{c|Uriah the Hittite}}, one of his most loyal warriors,

While {{c|Uriah}} is away at the siege of {{p|Rabbah}}. When {{c|Bathsheba}}
Becomes pregnant, {{c|David}} arranges for {{c|Uriah}} to be placed in
The most dangerous part of the battle line, so that he will be
Killed. The plan succeeds. {{c|Uriah}} dies. {{c|David}} marries {{c|Bathsheba}}.

But the prophet {{c|Nathan}} confronts {{c|David}} with the parable of
The rich man and the poor man's ewe lamb. {{c|David}}, provoked to
Righteous anger by the parable, condemns the rich man—and {{c|Nathan}}
Replies: "You are the man." {{c|David}} repents. He fasts, he weeps, he

Composes the psalm traditionally identified as {{p|Psalm 51}} ("Have
Mercy upon me, O God, according to thy lovingkindness..."). But
The consequences unfold. The first child {{c|Bathsheba}} bears to
{{c|David}} dies in infancy. His family is cursed. His eldest son

{{c|Amnon}} rapes his half-sister {{c|Tamar}}. His second son {{c|Absalom}}
Avenges her by murdering {{c|Amnon}}, then rebels against his father
{{c|David}}, driving him from {{p|Jerusalem}}. The civil war is
Catastrophic. {{c|Absalom}} is killed in the forest of {{p|Ephraim}},

Caught by his famous long hair in the branches of an oak and
Stabbed by {{c|Joab}}, {{c|David}}'s general (against {{c|David}}'s explicit
Orders to spare him). {{c|David}}'s grief for {{c|Absalom}} is one of the
Most devastating passages in ancient literature: "O my son

{{c|Absalom}}, my son, my son {{c|Absalom}}! Would God I had died for thee,
O {{c|Absalom}}, my son, my son!" A father's anguish, undiluted by
Any official decorum, recorded in the annals of a king. The
Court History allows this. It does not edit the grief out.

{{c|David}} survives the rebellion but is broken. The remainder of
His reign is marked by further intrigues: the rebellion of {{c|Sheba}},
The succession crisis as {{c|David}} grows old, the political maneuvers
By which {{c|Bathsheba}} and the prophet {{c|Nathan}} secure the throne

For {{c|Solomon}}, {{c|David}} and {{c|Bathsheba}}'s second son, over the claims
Of {{c|Adonijah}}, another of {{c|David}}'s sons. {{c|David}} dies old, full of
Days, and is buried in the "City of {{c|David}}" in {{p|Jerusalem}}. The
Biblical tradition remembers him as {{p|Israel}}'s greatest king—the

Standard against which all subsequent {{p|Jewish}} and, later,
{{p|Christian}} messianic expectations are measured. The promised
Messiah is the "son of {{c|David}}," a descendant of the {{c|Davidic}}
Line who will restore {{p|Israel}} to its full glory. Generations of

{{p|Jews}} lived and died awaiting this messianic king. {{p|Christians}}
Came to believe that the messianic king had arrived in {{c|Jesus}} of
{{p|Nazareth}}, identified as a descendant of {{c|David}} through {{c|Joseph}}.
{{p|Islam}} also recognizes {{c|David}} as a prophet, called {{c|Dawud}}. In

{{p|Jewish}}, {{p|Christian}}, and {{p|Islamic}} tradition alike, {{c|David}} is one
Of the central figures of sacred history. His psalms—the {{p|Psalter}},
Traditionally attributed to him in whole or in part—are among
The most frequently read religious texts in human history. For

Two and a half millennia, the {{p|Psalter}} has shaped prayer, worship,
Poetry, music, and private devotion across the {{p|Abrahamic}} world.
Its cries of praise, lament, thanksgiving, and petition are
The religious vocabulary of half the world's population.

And consider what {{c|David}}'s life means structurally. He is the
Ideal of the righteous king who is also genuinely and persistently
Human. He is not a god-king like {{c|Pharaoh}}. He is not a semi-divine
Hero like {{c|Gilgamesh}}. He is a man—a man with whom {{c|Yahweh}} has a

Covenant, yes, a man whom {{c|Yahweh}} has chosen, yes, but a man. A
Man who loves, sins, repents, suffers, and finally dies. His
Covenant relationship with {{c|Yahweh}} is mediated through his human
Agency, his human choices. This is a profoundly different vision

Of kingship from anything in the Bronze Age. Bronze Age kings
Were divine or semi-divine. They stood outside the ordinary
Human order. {{c|David}} stands within it. He is a new kind of
Religious figure: the monarch who is not a god but a servant of

A god. The monarch who is measured against divine moral standards
And can fail to meet them. The monarch who repents publicly.
This is the model that will shape later {{p|Jewish}}, {{p|Christian}},
And {{p|Islamic}} political theology. A king stands under God, not

Beside him. A king can be rebuked by a prophet. A king is
Accountable. The {{c|David}}-{{c|Nathan}} encounter—the prophet confronting
The king about his sin—is one of the foundational moments of
{{p|Western}} political-religious imagination. It asserts that even

The most powerful ruler is accountable to a moral order that
Transcends his own authority. The implications of this for the
Development of democracy, constitutional government, and human
Rights are profound. They trace back, in substantial measure, to

{{c|David}}'s kneeling before {{c|Nathan}} and saying: "I have sinned
Against the Lord."

                        {{c|David}}. Son of {{c|Jesse}}. Shepherd. Harpist.
Giant-slayer. Outlaw. Mercenary. King. Adulterer. Murderer.

Penitent. Psalmist. Father. Mourner. Founder of dynasty. The
Court History's devastating human portrait. The covenant with
{{c|Yahweh}} to establish an eternal house. The {{p|Jerusalem}} capital.
The {{p|Ark of the Covenant}} installed with dancing. The promise of

A messiah from his line. The psalter that has shaped three
Millennia of prayer.

                         {{c|David}}. {{c|Melek David}}. The king whose
Human complexity opened space for a new kind of political and

Religious imagination. The ancestor, by flesh or by adoption,
Of every messianic hope. Stand.
