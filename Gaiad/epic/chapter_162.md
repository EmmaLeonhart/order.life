# Chapter 162: The Bantu Expansion

And now, while the {{p|Mediterranean}} shatters and {{p|China}} is reborn,
We must turn south. Because something vast is happening on the
{{p|African}} continent. A population expansion that, in sheer scope
And demographic impact, will equal any of the movements the Gaiad

Has described—including the {{p|Yamnaya}} expansion, the {{p|Austronesian}}
Migrations, the {{p|Aryan}} movements into {{p|India}}. But unlike those,
The {{p|Bantu}} expansion takes place almost entirely within a single
Continent, and reshapes that continent so thoroughly that the

Continent's demographic map becomes the map of {{p|Bantu}} speakers.

                                                                  The
{{p|Bantu}} peoples originated in what is now the border region between
{{p|Cameroon}} and {{p|Nigeria}}, in {{p|West Africa}}. They spoke a language

Belonging to the {{p|Niger-Congo}} family, specifically the {{p|Bantu}}
Branch. They carried primarily the {{c|E1b1a}} (now called {{c|E-V38}})
{{p|Y-chromosome}} haplogroup—a sub-clade of the {{c|E}} haplogroup that
The Gaiad has already described as the {{p|African}} branch that

Stayed on the continent while its sister clades migrated out. And
Around three thousand BCE—earlier than the event the Gaiad is
About to narrate but laying the necessary foundation—the early
{{p|Bantu}} speakers developed agriculture: {{p|West African}} yam cultivation

Together with oil palm and other local crops. They developed
Pottery. And they gradually acquired, through trade and diffusion
From the north, some of the technologies of {{p|Sahelian}} and {{p|North African}}
Civilization, including eventually iron smelting. Iron smelting

Reached the {{p|Bantu}} region between approximately fifteen-hundred
And one-thousand BCE—a transformation roughly contemporary with
The iron-age transition that was happening in the {{p|Mediterranean}}
And the {{p|Near East}} after the {{p|Bronze Age Collapse}}. With iron tools,

{{p|Bantu}} farmers could clear forest more effectively than before.
With iron weapons, they could defend themselves against rivals
And raid for resources. And with iron, combined with their
Established agricultural package, they had the technological

Preconditions for rapid expansion.

                                     And they expanded. Around
Fifteen-hundred BCE, or possibly earlier, some {{p|Bantu}} groups
Began moving eastward and southward out of their homeland. The

Eastward migration took them along the southern edge of the
{{p|Congo}} rainforest, through what is now the {{p|Central African Republic}}
And {{p|South Sudan}}, into the {{p|Great Lakes}} region of {{p|East Africa}}—
{{p|Lake Victoria}}, {{p|Lake Tanganyika}}, {{p|Lake Malawi}}. There they

Encountered {{p|Afro-Asiatic}}-speaking peoples, {{p|Nilo-Saharan}}-speaking
Peoples, and {{p|Khoisan}}-speaking peoples. They interacted with all
These groups: sometimes peacefully, through trade, intermarriage,
And cultural exchange; sometimes violently, through warfare and

Displacement; and most importantly, through gradual demographic
Replacement, as their agricultural productivity supported much
Larger populations than the hunter-gatherer or pastoralist
Economies they encountered. The {{p|Bantu}} did not necessarily outfight

Other peoples. They outgrew them. Their farming villages produced
Many more children than the {{p|Khoisan}} foraging bands that had
Previously occupied the land. Over generations, the {{p|Bantu}}
Population density rose, while the prior populations either

Assimilated into {{p|Bantu}} farming communities, withdrew to marginal
Lands (forests, deserts, mountains) where farming was less
Practical, or disappeared. The demographic transition was gradual
But inexorable. And it continued for over two thousand years.

The southward migration took the {{p|Bantu}} through the {{p|Congo}} basin
(Either skirting it through the savanna or gradually penetrating
Its river valleys) and into southern {{p|Africa}}—what is now {{p|Angola}},
{{p|Namibia}}, {{p|Zambia}}, {{p|Zimbabwe}}, {{p|Botswana}}, {{p|Mozambique}}, and the

Coastal regions of what is now {{p|South Africa}}. By the beginning of
The common era, {{p|Bantu}}-speaking peoples had reached the {{p|Limpopo}}
River. By three-hundred to five-hundred CE, they had crossed the
{{p|Limpopo}} and were settling in what is now northeastern {{p|South Africa}}.

By one-thousand CE, they had reached the {{p|Great Fish River}} in
Eastern {{p|Cape}}. The total migration, from its origin in the
{{p|Nigerian-Cameroonian}} border region to its southernmost extent,
Covered over three thousand miles and took approximately two

And a half millennia. It is one of the longest sustained population
Movements in human history.

                               The demographic outcome is
Staggering. Today there are approximately four hundred million

{{p|Bantu}}-speaking people, comprising several hundred distinct
Languages including {{p|Swahili}}, {{p|Zulu}}, {{p|Xhosa}}, {{p|Shona}}, {{p|Lingala}},
{{p|Kikuyu}}, {{p|Ganda}}, {{p|Rwanda}}, {{p|Kirundi}}, {{p|Luba}}, {{p|Kikongo}}, {{p|Setswana}},
{{p|Sesotho}}, {{p|Chichewa}}, and hundreds more. They occupy approximately

The southern half of the {{p|African}} continent—everything south of
A line running roughly from {{p|Cameroon}} through {{p|Kenya}}—with the
Exception of a few pockets where pre-{{p|Bantu}} languages survive:
The {{p|Khoisan}} languages of the {{p|Kalahari}} and surrounding areas;

The {{p|Cushitic}} and {{p|Nilotic}} languages of the eastern {{p|Rift Valley}};
The {{p|Pygmy}} populations of the {{p|Congo}} rainforest (who themselves
Have largely adopted {{p|Bantu}} languages while retaining distinct
Ancestry and some cultural elements). The demographic and

Linguistic unity of sub-Saharan {{p|Africa}} from {{p|Kenya}} to {{p|Cape Town}}
Is, essentially, the unity of the {{p|Bantu}} expansion. It is one
Of the defining features of the modern {{p|African}} continent. And
It was created by this long, slow, multi-millennial demographic

Movement, driven not by conquering armies but by farming villages
Gradually spreading into lands that could be farmed.

                                                       Genetically,
The {{p|Bantu}} expansion is visible. Modern sub-Saharan {{p|Africans}}

South of the equator carry predominantly {{c|E1b1a-V38}} or related
{{c|E}} sub-clades on their {{p|Y-chromosome}}. Their maternal lineages
Show some regional diversity—the original {{p|Bantu}} homeland's
Haplogroups are mixed with local haplogroups from wherever the

{{p|Bantu}} settled—but the paternal lineages show striking uniformity
Consistent with the pattern of {{p|Bantu}} men taking local wives as
They expanded. This is a common feature of agricultural expansions:
The men move in force, displace or absorb the men of the prior

Populations, and take local women as wives. The resulting
Population retains the invading male lineages but has mixed
Female lineages. The same pattern is visible in the {{p|Yamnaya}}
Expansion into {{p|Europe}} and the {{p|Aryan}} expansion into {{p|India}}. It

Is the demographic signature of agricultural male-dominant
Expansion, and it characterizes the {{p|Bantu}} spread.

                                                       But what
Makes the {{p|Bantu}} expansion distinctive is that it did not destroy

The prior populations so completely. The {{p|Khoisan}} peoples still
Exist. The {{p|Pygmy}} peoples still exist. The {{p|Cushitic}} and {{p|Nilotic}}
Peoples still exist. {{p|Africa}} did not become a monoculture. It
Became a {{p|Bantu}} majority with significant and culturally vibrant

Minorities. The {{p|Khoisan}} of the {{p|Kalahari}} preserve {{p|Africa}}'s
Oldest linguistic lineages, including the click consonants that
Some scholars believe may descend from the very first human
Languages. The {{p|Pygmies}} of the {{p|Ituri}} and {{p|Congo}} forests preserve

The foraging traditions of {{p|Central African}} hunter-gatherers that
Predate the {{p|Bantu}} arrival by tens of thousands of years. The
{{p|Nilotic}} peoples of the {{p|Sudan}} and {{p|East African}} {{p|Rift Valley}}
Preserve pastoralist traditions linked to the cattle complex of

The eastern savanna. And among the {{p|Bantu}} themselves, there are
Profound cultural and historical differences between, say, the
{{p|Kikuyu}} of {{p|Kenya}} and the {{p|Zulu}} of {{p|South Africa}}, or between the
{{p|Kongo}} peoples of the {{p|Congo}} basin and the {{p|Tswana}} of the

{{p|Kalahari}} fringe. The {{p|Bantu}} expansion unified sub-Saharan {{p|Africa}}
Linguistically but did not homogenize it culturally. Each regional
{{p|Bantu}} population developed its own particular traditions, its
Own political systems, its own religious cosmologies, its own

Economic specializations. By the turn of the common era, the
{{p|Bantu}} world was a patchwork of kingdoms, chiefdoms, and village
Communities ranging from dense agricultural states in the {{p|Great Lakes}}
Region to semi-nomadic cattle-herders in the savanna to forest

Villages in the {{p|Congo}}. The largest and most complex of these
{{p|Bantu}} polities would emerge in later centuries: the {{p|Kingdom of Kongo}},
The {{p|Luba}} and {{p|Lunda}} empires of {{p|Central Africa}}, {{p|Great Zimbabwe}},
The {{p|Mutapa Empire}}, the {{p|Buganda Kingdom}}, the {{p|Zulu Kingdom}}. Each

Would be a product of local {{p|Bantu}} development working with
Particular ecological and political circumstances. But all would
Be rooted in the linguistic and cultural framework established
By the long {{p|Bantu}} expansion.

                                   Consider also the economic and
Technological dimensions. The {{p|Bantu}} carried with them a specific
Agricultural package: yams, oil palm, pearl millet, sorghum, some
Varieties of rice and beans. As they encountered new environments,

They adopted new crops: cattle and small livestock in the {{p|East African}}
Highlands; plantains and bananas (which arrived in {{p|Africa}} from
{{p|Southeast Asia}} via {{p|Madagascar}} and the {{p|Indian Ocean}} trade and
Were transmitted westward into the {{p|Great Lakes}} region by around

One-thousand BCE, just in time to supercharge the {{p|Bantu}} expansion);
Sweet potatoes and other {{p|New World}} crops after the {{p|Columbian}}
Exchange. The {{p|Bantu}} agricultural system was adaptive and
Incorporative. Each new crop that became available was integrated

Into the system, supporting still larger populations. Bananas in
Particular were revolutionary: they could grow in the humid
{{p|Great Lakes}} region where grains struggled, and they produced
Enormous caloric yields per unit of labor. By the time the

{{p|Bantu}} expansion was completing its southward spread, banana
Cultivation was driving a second wave of population growth in
The {{p|Great Lakes}} region and {{p|East African}} highlands—producing
Some of the densest rural populations anywhere in {{p|pre-colonial Africa}}.

Iron technology was also central. The {{p|Bantu}} developed independent
Iron-smelting traditions that produced large quantities of iron
Tools and weapons. {{p|African}} iron production in the first millennium
CE was sophisticated and large-scale, using natural-draft furnaces

That achieved high temperatures with minimal fuel—a design that
In some respects was more efficient than contemporary {{p|European}}
Iron-smelting technology. {{p|Bantu}} smiths were often regarded as
Ritual specialists as well as artisans, and in many societies

They formed hereditary castes with specialized religious functions.
The smith's transformation of ore into metal was understood as
A powerful, quasi-magical act, demanding ritual preparation and
Giving the smith special spiritual authority. This is a theme that

Appears across many {{p|African}} societies and that connects to
Deeper religious conceptions of transformation and creation.

                                                               And
Consider the religious and cultural patrimony the {{p|Bantu}} carried.

{{p|Bantu}} religious traditions share certain common features across
Their vast geographic spread: a high creator god who is often
Remote from daily human affairs; ancestor veneration, with the
Recently deceased remaining present as spiritual forces influencing

The living; nature spirits inhabiting significant features of the
Landscape—particular trees, hills, rivers, caves; specialists in
Communication with the spiritual world (diviners, healers, rain-
Makers); an ethic of community, hospitality, and social harmony

Summed up in the {{p|Zulu}} and {{p|Xhosa}} concept of {{p|ubuntu}} ("I am because
We are"). These shared features do not mean {{p|Bantu}} religions are
Uniform—they are diverse and locally elaborated—but they share
Deep structural affinities inherited from the original {{p|Bantu}}

Homeland and carried southward and eastward during the expansion.
In this they parallel the shared structural features of other
Great language-family expansions: {{p|Indo-European}}, {{p|Austronesian}},
{{p|Semitic}}. A shared linguistic origin tends to preserve a shared

Religious and cultural substratum, even as specific traditions
Develop in locally distinctive ways.

                                        The {{p|Bantu}} expansion is
One of the great movements of human history. It is the story of

How sub-Saharan {{p|Africa}} below the equator became linguistically
And demographically unified. It is the story of how agriculture
And iron transformed a continent. And it is the story of how a
Particular people—originating in a small region of {{p|West Africa}}—

Became, through slow demographic growth and gradual geographic
Spread, one of the world's largest ethnolinguistic families. The
Gaiad has tended, in its narration of this vast process, to
Emphasize demographic and linguistic continuity. But we must also

Note that the {{p|Bantu}} expansion was not peaceful. It involved
Displacement, warfare, and occasional atrocity. The {{p|Khoisan}} and
Other pre-{{p|Bantu}} peoples who were pushed to the margins of their
Former territories did not go willingly. Some were killed; some

Were enslaved; some were assimilated under duress. The {{p|Bantu}}
Expansion was gentler than many conquest migrations in world
History (the {{p|Mongol}} expansion, the {{p|Indo-European}} expansion
Into {{p|Europe}}, the {{p|European}} colonization of the Americas), but it

Was not bloodless. It was a real historical movement with real
Human costs. The {{p|Khoisan}} of the {{p|Kalahari}}, the {{p|Pygmies}} of the
{{p|Ituri}}, the remnant {{p|Cushitic}} populations of {{p|East Africa}}—these
Peoples live in the ecological margins where {{p|Bantu}} farming

Could not displace them. Their survival on those margins is itself
A testimony both to their resilience and to the completeness of
The {{p|Bantu}} occupation of everywhere more agriculturally desirable.

The Gaiad honors the {{p|Bantu}} expansion. The Gaiad honors also the

Pre-{{p|Bantu}} peoples who were displaced or assimilated in its course.
The Gaiad honors the hybrid populations that resulted. The Gaiad
Honors the {{p|Pygmies}} who retained their identity in the depths of
The {{p|Ituri}}. The Gaiad honors the {{p|Khoisan}} who retained theirs in

The depths of the {{p|Kalahari}}. The Gaiad honors the {{p|Nilotic}} and
{{p|Cushitic}} peoples who retained theirs along the {{p|Rift Valley}} and
The {{p|Horn of Africa}}. All of these peoples, together, constitute
The anthropological mosaic of {{p|Africa}}. The {{p|Bantu}} are the largest

Component but not the only component. Sub-Saharan {{p|Africa}} is
More diverse than its {{p|Bantu}} majority might suggest. And the
Gaiad reads the {{p|Bantu}} expansion as one of the most important
Processes of the last three thousand years—because it laid the

Demographic foundation of the modern {{p|African}} continent.
