# Chapter 158: The Mycenaeans

And in the west, on the southern tip of the {{p|Greek}} mainland and
Across the {{p|Aegean}} islands, a third great Bronze Age civilization
Was reaching its peak. {{p|Mycenaean Greece}}.

                                                   Named for {{p|Mycenae}},
The citadel on a hill in the {{p|Argolid}} whose massive "{{p|Cyclopean}}"
Walls of unmortared limestone blocks, some weighing over twenty tons,
Dominated the landscape. {{p|Mycenae}} was the political and cultural

Center of the Bronze Age {{p|Greek}} world. Other major centers were
At {{p|Tiryns}}, {{p|Pylos}}, {{p|Athens}} (already a settled site, though
Not yet the city it would become), {{p|Thebes}}, {{p|Orchomenos}}, and
Several others. The {{p|Mycenaean}} civilization emerged around sixteen

Hundred BCE—roughly contemporary with the {{p|New Kingdom}} beginning
In {{p|Egypt}} and the {{p|Hittite}} kingdom consolidating in {{p|Anatolia}}—
And flourished until the Bronze Age Collapse around twelve hundred BCE.

The {{p|Mycenaeans}} were {{c|R1b}}-haplogroup {{p|Indo-European}}-speakers—
Descendants of the {{p|Yamnaya}} expansion that had reached {{p|Greece}} in
The early {{p|Bronze Age}}. Their language was an archaic form of {{p|Greek}},
Written in a syllabic script called {{p|Linear B}}—adapted from the

{{p|Minoan}} {{p|Linear A}} script that preceded it. Unlike {{p|Linear A}},
{{p|Linear B}} has been successfully deciphered. In nineteen-fifty-two,
{{c|Michael Ventris}}, a young {{p|English}} architect with a passion for
Ancient scripts, demonstrated that {{p|Linear B}} was used to write

Early {{p|Greek}}. The achievement is one of the most celebrated in
The history of decipherment. It opened up an entire Bronze Age
Civilization to modern understanding. We can read the {{p|Linear B}}
Tablets recovered from {{p|Pylos}}, {{p|Knossos}}, {{p|Mycenae}}, and other

{{p|Mycenaean}} sites, and they reveal a palace-centered economic and
Administrative system managing vast agricultural, craft, and
Commercial operations. {{p|Linear B}} tablets are administrative
Records—inventories of livestock, lists of workers, tax collections,

Distributions of rations, inventories of military equipment. They
Are not literature. But they provide a detailed picture of the
Bureaucratic machinery of a {{p|Mycenaean}} palace, managing the economic
Life of its region down to the number of bronze arrowheads in the

Armory and the ration of wheat allocated to each category of worker.

And above the bureaucratic record, the {{p|Mycenaean}} world was also
A warrior society. The {{p|Mycenaean}} elite warriors—depicted in
Frescoes, buried with bronze swords and boar's-tusk helmets and

Gold-masked faces—were an Indo-European warrior aristocracy of the
Type that the {{p|Yamnaya}} expansion had produced across the
Indo-European world. The shaft-graves at {{p|Mycenae}}, excavated by
{{c|Heinrich Schliemann}} in eighteen-seventy-six, contained gold funerary

Masks (including the famous "{{p|Mask of Agamemnon}}," though the
Mask actually predates any possible historical {{c|Agamemnon}} by
Several centuries), bronze weapons, elaborate jewelry, and other
Signs of an extraordinarily wealthy warrior elite. The {{p|Mycenaeans}}

Were, in their cultural self-conception, the heroes who would later
Be memorialized in {{c|Homer}}'s poetry. They valued combat, loot,
Honor, and the gift-exchange diplomacy of aristocratic warrior
Bands. They were the original {{p|Greek}} aristocracy whose memory

Fueled the later {{p|Iron Age}} reconstruction of the "heroic age."

And their relationship with the {{p|Minoan}} civilization is complex.
The {{p|Mycenaeans}} had originally been the junior partners of the
{{p|Minoans}}—culturally influenced by the island civilization, less

Wealthy, less sophisticated. But around fourteen-fifty BCE, after
The {{p|Thera}} eruption had weakened the {{p|Minoan}} civilization, the
{{p|Mycenaeans}} invaded {{p|Crete}}, occupied the major palace centers,
And took over the {{p|Minoan}} administrative and cultural system. The

{{p|Linear B}} tablets found at {{p|Knossos}} date to this {{p|Mycenaean}}
Occupation period. The {{p|Mycenaeans}} adopted {{p|Minoan}} artistic
Styles, religious iconography, and palace architecture—but adapted
Them to their own {{p|Indo-European}} warrior ethos. {{p|Mycenaean}} frescoes,

Unlike the peaceful nature scenes of {{p|Minoan}} art, often depict
Warriors, hunts, and battle scenes. The serenity of {{p|Minoan}}
Civilization was replaced by the martial vigor of the {{p|Mycenaean}}
Overlay. But many {{p|Minoan}} elements survived: goddess-worship, the

Labyrinthine palace-plan, the bull-symbolism. The {{p|Mycenaean}}
Civilization was a hybrid of {{p|Indo-European}} warrior aristocracy
And {{p|Minoan}} palace sophistication. The combination was potent.

And it was wealthy. {{p|Mycenaean}} trade networks extended across the
{{p|Eastern Mediterranean}}. {{p|Mycenaean}} pottery has been found in {{p|Egypt}},
The {{p|Levant}}, {{p|Anatolia}}, {{p|Cyprus}}, and as far west as {{p|Sardinia}} and
{{p|Sicily}}. The {{p|Mycenaeans}} imported copper from {{p|Cyprus}}, tin from

Distant sources (possibly {{p|Cornwall}} or {{p|Afghanistan}}), gold from
{{p|Egypt}}, ivory from {{p|Syria}}, cedar from {{p|Lebanon}}. They exported
Olive oil, wine, textiles, and their distinctive pottery. The
{{p|Aegean}} was a trading lake in which {{p|Mycenaean}} ships sailed in

Every direction, moving goods that the palaces redistributed and
Taxed. The palatial economy was in the middle of the trade network,
And the wealth that flowed through it made {{p|Mycenae}} and its
Sister-cities genuinely rich by Bronze Age standards.

And one episode from this period—probably around the end of the
Thirteenth century BCE, shortly before the Bronze Age Collapse—
Would become the greatest memorial narrative in {{p|Western}} literature.
The {{p|Trojan War}}.

                         The historicity of the {{p|Trojan War}} is
Debated but, the Gaiad accepts, broadly real. There was a city
At {{p|Troy}} (the site called {{p|Hisarlik}} in northwestern {{p|Turkey}}),
And it was destroyed by violence, and the destruction can be dated

To roughly the right period. The site shows evidence of siege and
Burning in its {{p|Troy VIIa}} level, which is usually identified
With the {{p|Homeric}} {{p|Troy}}. Whether the war was fought by a single
Coalition of {{p|Mycenaean}} kings led by {{c|Agamemnon}}, whether the

Participants had the names {{c|Homer}} would give them six centuries
Later, whether {{c|Achilles}} and {{c|Hector}} and {{c|Odysseus}} and {{c|Helen}} and
{{c|Paris}} were historical individuals—these are uncertain. But a war
Between {{p|Mycenaean}} {{p|Greeks}} and the kingdom of {{p|Wilusa}} ({{p|Troy}}'s

{{p|Hittite}} name, which is cognate with "{{p|Ilios}}," {{p|Homer}}'s term) is
Attested in {{p|Hittite}} diplomatic correspondence. There was a real
Conflict over this real city between real {{p|Mycenaean}} forces and
Real {{p|Anatolian}} defenders. {{c|Homer}}'s later epic dressed the event

In the heroic costume of the oral tradition, compressing and
Elaborating and fictionalizing, but the historical kernel was there.

                                                                            And
The {{p|Mycenaean}} civilization is, the Gaiad argues, the {{p|Greek}}
Contribution to the {{p|Bronze Age Collapse}} chapter. Within a

Generation or two after the {{p|Trojan War}}, the {{p|Mycenaean}} palaces
Themselves were destroyed. {{p|Mycenae}} was burned. {{p|Tiryns}} was
Sacked. {{p|Pylos}} was burned (which is why its {{p|Linear B}} tablets
Survive—they were baked hard by the fire instead of being allowed

To dissolve in the normal way). The entire palatial civilization
Collapsed in a generation around twelve-hundred BCE. The cause was
The general Bronze Age Collapse—{{p|Sea Peoples}} raids, possibly
Internal rebellions by the servile population, climate pressures,

And the systemic failure of the Bronze Age international trade
Network. The {{p|Mycenaean}} palaces went down in a coordinated wave
Of destructions that archaeologists can date, across many sites,
To the same twenty-year window. The civilization did not recover.

After the collapse, {{p|Greece}} entered a "{{p|Dark Age}}" that lasted
Approximately three centuries. {{p|Linear B}} writing was forgotten.
The palace economies vanished. Population collapsed. Monumental
Architecture ceased. Trade networks shrank to local exchange.

The {{p|Greeks}} of the {{p|Dark Age}} lived in small villages, farmed at
Subsistence levels, and preserved the memory of the {{p|Mycenaean}}
Heroic age only in oral tradition. When writing finally returned—
Via the {{p|Phoenician}} alphabet, around the eighth century BCE—the

{{p|Greeks}} were a completely different civilization from what the
{{p|Mycenaeans}} had been. They had forgotten {{p|Linear B}} entirely. They
Had forgotten the palace economies. They had forgotten, in most
Cases, the specific historical geography of their Bronze Age

Predecessors. What they remembered was the heroic poetry—the songs
About {{c|Achilles}} and {{c|Odysseus}} and {{c|Agamemnon}} that had been
Transmitted orally through three or four centuries of illiterate
{{p|Dark Age}}. When {{c|Homer}} (whoever he was, whenever exactly he

Lived—probably the eighth century BCE, probably a single poet but
Possibly a tradition) composed the {{p|Iliad}} and the {{p|Odyssey}}, he
Was working from this oral tradition. The poems preserve fragments
Of {{p|Mycenaean}} material culture (the boar's-tusk helmet, the

Catalog of ships, certain personal names, certain geographical
References) that must have been transmitted from the Bronze Age
Without being archaeologically rediscovered until the nineteenth
Century. {{c|Homer}} knew things about the {{p|Mycenaean}} world that he

Could only have known through oral tradition stretching back
Centuries. The poetry is a time-capsule. The {{p|Mycenaean}} civilization
Lives, in attenuated and elaborated form, in the {{p|Homeric}} epics.

And this chapter, before it concludes, gestures toward one other
Thing. The {{p|Dionysiaca}}—the mythological cycle about the god
{{c|Dionysus}}'s campaigns into {{p|India}}. In later {{p|Greek}} mythology,
{{c|Dionysus}} (god of wine, ecstasy, and the irrational) is said to

Have invaded {{p|India}} at the head of an army and established his
Worship there before returning to {{p|Greece}}. This myth is preserved
In {{c|Nonnus of Panopolis}}'s late antique epic {{p|Dionysiaca}}—a
Forty-eight-book epic that is the longest surviving poem of

Classical antiquity. The story is historically fantastical. But
The Gaiad takes it as an eastward gesture, a pointing toward the
Connection between the {{p|Mediterranean}} and {{p|Indian}} worlds that will
Become increasingly important in later chapters. {{c|Dionysus}} into

{{p|India}} prefigures the {{p|Ramayana}}'s expansion westward toward
{{p|Troy}} and {{p|Egypt}}. The mythological cycles of the two civilizations,
Separated by thousands of miles, include cross-pointing gestures
That suggest deeper awareness of each other than the strict

Archaeological record demonstrates. The {{p|Mycenaean}} world, at its
Height, was in communication with {{p|Anatolia}}, {{p|Syria}}, {{p|Egypt}}, and
Probably indirectly with {{p|Mesopotamia}} and beyond. The cultural
Connections of the late Bronze Age were real and extensive. And

The myths preserve traces of those connections, even after the
Archaeological and documentary evidence has fragmented.
