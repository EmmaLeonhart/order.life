# Chapter 148: Minoan Crete

And while {{p|Stonehenge}} stood weathering on the {{p|Salisbury Plain}},
And while {{p|Egyptian}} civilization recovered from the {{p|First
Intermediate Period}} into the {{p|Middle Kingdom}}, on an island in
The middle of the {{p|Mediterranean Sea}}, another palace civilization

Was reaching its own apex.

                                 {{p|Crete}}. The largest island of the
{{p|Aegean Sea}}. The civilization of the {{p|Minoans}}.

{{p|Minoan}} is a modern name, coined by {{c|Arthur Evans}} in the late
Nineteenth century after {{c|King Minos}}, the legendary {{p|Cretan}}
King of {{p|Greek}} mythology. The {{p|Minoans}} themselves called
Themselves something else—we do not know what, because their

Language, written in the script we call {{p|Linear A}}, has never
Been deciphered. The {{p|Minoans}} are the second great undeciphered
Civilization of the Bronze Age. (The {{p|Indus Valley}} is the first.)
We can read the shape of their society—we know how they built, what

They ate, what they wore, what they painted, how they organized
Their economy, how they traded, and roughly how their religion was
Structured. We cannot read their words. The {{p|Linear A}} tablets are
Legible as symbols. They are not legible as language. A thousand

Tablets. Ten thousand fragments. Their speech is silent.

                                                               The
{{p|Minoan}} civilization emerged in its palatial form around nineteen
Hundred BCE and flourished until approximately fifteen hundred BCE.

The great palace complexes—at {{p|Knossos}}, {{p|Phaistos}}, {{p|Malia}}, and
{{p|Zakros}}—were the administrative, economic, and religious centers of
The civilization. They were not fortified. {{p|Minoan}} {{p|Crete}}, astonishingly,
Had no city walls. The palaces rose directly out of the landscape

Without defensive architecture. The interpretation is that the
{{p|Minoans}} had naval supremacy in the {{p|Aegean}} and did not need
Land fortifications; their security was provided by their fleet.
The idea of a civilization secure enough to live in unwalled

Palaces is, by Bronze Age standards, extraordinary. Almost every
Other civilization of the period had walled cities as a matter
Of survival. The {{p|Minoans}} did not.

                                            And inside the palaces,
The culture was visually extraordinary. {{p|Minoan}} frescoes depict

Dolphins, octopuses, flying fish, wild goats, lilies, crocuses,
Saffron-gatherers, bull-leapers, priestesses, processions of
Offering-bearers—a visual vocabulary that is unmistakably alive
With nature, color, and movement. The {{p|Minoans}} loved the sea and

Loved the land and loved the animals and plants that surrounded them.
Their palaces are full of light-wells and air-shafts and colonnaded
Courtyards. They invented indoor plumbing—the palace at {{p|Knossos}}
Had flush toilets, drainage systems, and hot and cold running water

In the royal apartments, four thousand years ago, a luxury that
Most of the world would not match until the twentieth century. The
Palace architecture is labyrinthine, multi-story, open-plan, with
Frescoes in every major room. {{p|Knossos}} itself may have been the

Historical basis for the {{p|Greek}} myth of the {{p|Labyrinth}}—the winding
Maze in which the {{c|Minotaur}} was imprisoned. The palace has some
Fifteen hundred rooms arranged around a central court, connected by
Corridors and stairways that have no single organizing axis. A

{{p|Greek}} visitor, confronted with this layout, might reasonably have
Concluded that the place was built to confuse and conceal. And the
Bull motif—so central to {{p|Minoan}} iconography, with bull-leaping
Frescoes, bull-horns of consecration on every altar, bull-headed

Rhyta—would have lent itself naturally to the {{c|Minotaur}} myth. The
{{c|Minotaur}}, a bull-headed man at the center of a labyrinth, is a
{{p|Greek}} memory of {{p|Minoan}} religion, distorted through the lens
Of centuries of cultural intermediation after the {{p|Minoan}} collapse.

The {{p|Minoan}} religion itself was probably centered on a mother goddess—
A figure depicted in the famous {{p|Snake Goddess}} statuettes, a woman
In an open bodice holding two writhing snakes. The snake symbolism
Is associated with chthonic earth-goddess worship throughout the

Ancient {{p|Mediterranean}}. The {{p|Minoans}} had priestesses, not priests—
Nearly every religious scene in the frescoes features female
Officiants. The religion appears to have been goddess-centered, with
A male consort figure (a young god who dies and is reborn, possibly

Related to the later {{p|Greek}} {{c|Dionysus}}) but with clear priority
On the female divine. This is the old European goddess religion
That {{c|Marija Gimbutas}} argued survived in pockets across the
{{p|Mediterranean}} into the Bronze Age. {{p|Crete}} is one of those pockets.

The mother goddess and her snakes. The saffron gatherers. The
Priestesses with their processional garments. {{p|Minoan}} religion is
The female-centered religion of old Europe, preserved on an island
Long enough to become the Bronze Age's most visually sophisticated

Civilization.

                And the {{p|Minoans}} traded. Their ships reached every
Corner of the {{p|Eastern Mediterranean}}. {{p|Minoan}} pottery has been
Found in {{p|Egypt}} (where {{p|Minoans}} are depicted in {{p|Eighteenth Dynasty}}

Tomb paintings as the "{{c|Keftiu}}"—a people bringing tribute to the
Pharaoh), in the {{p|Levant}}, in {{p|Anatolia}}, in the {{p|Cyclades}}, on
Mainland {{p|Greece}}. {{p|Minoan}} influence shapes the early {{p|Mycenaean}}
Culture on the {{p|Greek}} mainland. The {{p|Aegean}} is, for several

Hundred years, a {{p|Minoan}} lake. And the wealth that flowed through
{{p|Crete}}—in olive oil, wine, copper, tin, ceramics, textiles, and
Whatever else passed through the palace storerooms—made the
Civilization one of the wealthiest of the Bronze Age.

And then, around fifteen hundred BCE, it ended.

                                                    The cause was,
Almost certainly, the eruption of {{p|Thera}}—the volcanic island
Now called {{p|Santorini}}, about seventy miles north of {{p|Crete}}. The

{{p|Thera}} eruption was one of the largest volcanic events in human
History. It ejected an estimated sixty to one hundred cubic
Kilometers of material into the atmosphere. It blasted the center
Of the island of {{p|Thera}} into the sea, leaving the crescent-shaped

Caldera that is now the island of {{p|Santorini}}. The ashfall reached
{{p|Crete}}, {{p|Anatolia}}, {{p|Egypt}}, and as far as {{p|Greenland}} (where the
Layer is still visible in ice cores). A tsunami from the caldera
Collapse would have struck the northern coast of {{p|Crete}}, devastating

Coastal cities and ports. The ash would have poisoned the fields
And disrupted agriculture for years. And the {{p|Minoan}} civilization,
Dependent on its naval commerce and its agricultural base, was
Shattered by the eruption.

                                The {{p|Minoan}} civilization did not
End immediately. The palaces continued to function for another
Generation or two. But the civilization was wounded. And then,
Around fourteen hundred and fifty BCE, the {{p|Mycenaean Greeks}} from

The mainland—previously junior partners of the {{p|Minoans}}, culturally
Influenced by but politically independent—invaded and took over.
The {{p|Mycenaeans}} were {{c|R1b}}-carriers, {{p|Indo-European}}-speakers, the
Bronze Age manifestation of the {{p|Yamnaya}} expansion that had reached

{{p|Greece}}. They occupied {{p|Knossos}} and the other palaces. They replaced
{{p|Linear A}} with {{p|Linear B}}, a script adapted to write the {{p|Mycenaean}}
{{p|Greek}} language (and which, unlike {{p|Linear A}}, was successfully
Deciphered in nineteen fifty-two by {{c|Michael Ventris}}—who showed

That {{p|Linear B}} recorded an archaic form of {{p|Greek}}). The {{p|Minoan}}
Civilization was not destroyed—it was absorbed. And the {{p|Minoan}}
Religious and artistic traditions continued, in hybridized form,
Into the {{p|Mycenaean}} period. The {{c|Snake Goddess}} became, eventually,

The {{p|Greek}} {{c|Athena}}. The bull-leaping became the mythology of the
{{c|Minotaur}}. The labyrinth became a {{p|Greek}} cliché. The {{p|Minoan}}
Substrate persisted in {{p|Greek}} culture, under the {{p|Indo-European}}
Overlay, for the rest of {{p|Greek}} civilization.

And the language—the one actual {{p|Minoan}} language, the one that
{{p|Linear A}} wrote—is still undeciphered. We have the tablets. We can
Read the signs. We cannot match the signs to words in any known
Language. {{p|Linear A}}, like the {{p|Indus Valley}} script, is one of the

Great undeciphered scripts of the ancient world. Scholars have
Proposed various possibilities—that {{p|Linear A}} records an {{p|Anatolian}}
Language, or a pre-Greek {{p|Aegean}} language, or an isolate of its
Own—but no decipherment has been accepted. The {{p|Minoans}} wrote. We

Cannot read them. The silence of {{p|Linear A}} is one of the permanent
Gaps in our knowledge of the Bronze Age.

                                               The Gaiad honors the
{{p|Minoans}} as the great pre-{{p|Greek}} civilization of the {{p|Aegean}}.
The peaceful island civilization. The mother-goddess religion. The

Bull-leapers and saffron-gatherers. The unfortified palaces of
{{p|Knossos}} and {{p|Phaistos}}. The ships that carried olive oil to {{p|Egypt}}.
The priestesses with their snake-bracelets. The flushing toilets
And the light-wells and the frescoes of dolphins swimming along

The palace walls. A civilization unlike the militarized empires of
{{p|Mesopotamia}} and {{p|Egypt}}—smaller, more feminine, more nature-centered,
More sea-oriented, more confident in its own security. A civilization
That tried to live in palaces without walls. And that, for several

Hundred years, succeeded. Until the volcano erupted, and the
Northern {{p|Aegean}} was blanketed in ash, and the {{p|Mycenaean}} warriors
Came down from the mainland, and the mother goddess was folded
Into the pantheon of a sky-father religion, and the {{p|Minoan}} tongue

Fell silent in the mouths of its speakers. The island remained.
The palaces remained. The frescoes—repaired, restored, reconstructed—
Still show the bull-leapers and the saffron-gatherers and the
Dolphins in the light-wells. And we still call them {{p|Minoans}},

By the name the {{p|Greeks}} gave them, which is not their own name.

{{p|Crete}}. {{p|Minoan}}. The unwalled palaces. The mother goddess. The
Bull-leapers. The island civilization. The great peaceful
Civilization of the {{p|Aegean}}, wrecked by a volcano and absorbed

By the {{p|Mycenaeans}}, but surviving—in fragments, in images, in
The substrate of later {{p|Greek}} mythology—into the present.

{{p|Minoan Crete}}. {{p|Knossos}}. {{p|Linear A}} unread. Stand.
