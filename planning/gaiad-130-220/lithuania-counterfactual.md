# Lithuania — the book that was never written

A counterfactual case (see `structure.md` §6). Lithuania was pagan
until **1387** — the latest Christianization in Europe. Had a
*Shahnameh*-scale pre-Christian epic been compiled before or shortly
after conversion, Lithuania would have **the cleanest late-mythic
bridge in the world**.

## Why the hypothetical is so clean

- The **Gediminid dynasty** — Mindaugas, Gediminas, Algirdas, Jogaila
  — is fully historical AND was still pagan at the top of the chain.
  A national epic written by Jogaila's generation would have
  bridge-captured a living pagan cycle via insiders at the threshold,
  exactly the pattern that produces tier-1 cases elsewhere.
- Mindaugas (d. 1263) ruled a fully pagan polity, later briefly
  Christianized and reverted. The mythic-to-historical gap was
  trivially short.
- Perkūnas, Velnias, Sovij, Žemyna — the pagan pantheon was still
  functional in the 14th c., and **the language is Indo-European with
  exceptional archaism**, so even fragmentary survivals give us
  extraordinary linguistic access.

## What actually happened

The epic was never compiled. The surviving mythic material is
fragmentary — Gimbutas, Greimas, and others did **reconstructive work**
in the 20th c., but reconstruction is not capture.

**The absence is itself historically meaningful.** Teutonic Order
pressure suppressed documentation rather than encouraging it — the
Teutonic Knights needed Lithuania to be legibly pagan-therefore-
convertible, not legibly pagan-and-literate. The counter-pressure from
a crusading neighbor ate the window in which the epic could have been
written.

## Gaiad handling

A chapter or at least a sustained note: **"the book Lithuania never
got to write."** The chapter is partly *about* the absence — the
structural conditions under which mythic material fails to cross into
written form, even when all the other preconditions are in place. A
counterweight to the Aztec / Scandinavia / Mongolia chapters, where
the capture *did* happen.

Worth one chapter on its own merits. Also does thematic work: the
Gaiad's larger argument includes what monotheism and imperial
Christianization foreclosed, and Lithuania is the sharpest European
case of that foreclosure.
