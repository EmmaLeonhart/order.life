# Southeast Asia notes — uncertain coverage

**Preamble.** We aren't sure if any of these things really properly
count for dedicated coverage in this gap. The default per
`scope-caveats.md` is defer-to-modern — main SE Asia weight lands in
the modern period, not here. This file holds the *medieval* candidates
so they're not lost, with honest uncertainty attached.

The one thing I definitively want to include from this region in the
gap is **the spread of Islam into Southeast Asia**, probably as part
of a consolidated "Islam in Asia" chapter (see the "Consolidation
option" section below).

## Candidate handles

### Wali Sanga (the Nine Saints of Java)

- Traditional group of nine Sufi missionaries credited with bringing
  Islam to Java in the 15th–16th centuries.
- Operated during / after Majapahit's decline; their teaching
  absorbed Javanese Hindu-Buddhist substrate — gamelan used in dakwah,
  shadow puppetry (wayang) Islamicized rather than suppressed,
  pre-Islamic court ritual preserved inside the new religious frame.
- **Why it matters.** The Wali Sanga story is almost a showcase for
  the syncretic-Islam framing in `islam-syncretic.md` — it's what
  syncretism looks like in practice: Sufi missionaries explicitly
  absorbing, not replacing, the local spiritual vocabulary. If
  anything from medieval SE Asia earns a chapter, this is the
  strongest case.
- Names worth carrying forward if this firms up: Sunan Kalijaga (the
  most syncretic of the nine, ex-bandit, wayang-using teacher), Sunan
  Gunung Jati (founder of the Cirebon / Banten lines).

### Angkor / Jayavarman VII / the Bayon faces

- **Jayavarman VII** (r. ~1181–1218) was the last great ruler of the
  Khmer Empire, the king who built Angkor Thom and the Bayon temple.
- **The Bayon faces.** The Bayon temple is covered with ~200
  enormous stone faces carved into its towers — same face, repeated,
  staring outward in all four directions. Probably Jayavarman VII
  himself as a bodhisattva, possibly Avalokiteśvara. They are one of
  the eerier single visual motifs in world architecture — the
  "everything here is looking at you" quality.
- **The Mahayana-to-Theravada pivot.** Jayavarman VII was Mahayana
  Buddhist; after him the Khmer shifted to Theravada, and the
  civilization declined from its imperial peak. That pivot is part of
  the Asia arc's Mahayana story (see `asia-arc.md`) but shown from
  the losing side — a Mahayana imperial project that didn't survive.
- **Uncertainty.** No late-mythic-cycle bridge work captures Angkor
  the way Kojiki captures Japan. The material is visual / ruinous /
  epigraphic, not narrative. Hard to make it pay chapter cost.

### Srivijaya

- Maritime Buddhist empire, 7th–13th centuries, centered in Sumatra.
  Dominated the Strait of Malacca; major Mahayana learning center
  (Yijing studied there before going to India).
- Natural waypoint in the `asia-arc.md` Buddhism-moves-east spine
  rather than its own chapter — Srivijaya is the *ocean* the Mahayana
  corridor flows through, not a separate node.

### Ramakien and SE Asian Ramayana adaptations

- Thai *Ramakien*, Cambodian *Reamker*, Indonesian wayang Ramayana.
  The Indian epic absorbed into SE Asian courtly and performance
  traditions, often with local resonances (Hanuman as trickster, etc.).
- Probably a footnote to Indian Ramayana coverage rather than its own
  chapter — the interesting move is that the Ramayana *traveled* and
  kept being retold, which is a one-sentence observation more than
  a chapter.

### Pagan (Bagan) kingdom

- Burmese Theravada center, 11th–13th centuries. Thousands of stupas
  on the Irrawaddy plain. Parallel to Angkor as a large Buddhist
  imperial project, ultimately overrun by Mongols.
- Weaker case than Angkor; probably skipped.

## Consolidation option — "Islam in Asia" chapter

Rather than a dedicated Wali Sanga chapter *and* Islam-into-India
material *and* Islam-into-China material spread across the Islam arc,
the simpler move may be **one consolidated "Islam in Asia" chapter**
covering the spread into China, India, and Southeast Asia in a single
beat.

- Keeps it simple.
- Matches the Gaiad's syncretic framing — Islam-in-Asia as one
  phenomenon with regional variants, not three unrelated stories.
- Frees chapter budget elsewhere.
- Wali Sanga becomes a *section* of that chapter rather than its own.
- Hui Muslims, Delhi Sultanate's cultural synthesis, and the Javanese
  Sufi missionaries all sit under one roof.

This is probably the direction, but left open until the writing desk.

## Guardrail

Default remains: medieval SE Asia is deferred to modern coverage.
This file exists so that when the writing desk looks at SE Asia it
has the handles in one place, *with* the uncertainty flagged upfront.
Don't mistake the existence of this list for commitment to cover any
of these.
