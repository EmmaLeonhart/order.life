# Abraham → Isaac → Jacob — the patriarchal sequence

The three-generation patriarchal spine. One of the must-include
biblical narrative clusters (see `must-include.md`), sitting between
the Genesis opening (`genesis-opening.md`) and the Torah proper
(`torah.md`) via Joseph and Moses.

## The sequence

### Abraham

- The covenant pivot. Called out of Ur (Mesopotamia) to Canaan.
- **Ishmael** (via Hagar) and **Isaac** (via Sarah) — the two-branch
  fork that anchors both Arabian and Israelite descent narratives.
  Islam's self-placement via Ishmael runs through here; see
  `arabia.md` and the Adnan / Qahtan lineage material.
- **The Binding of Isaac (Akedah).** The single most structurally
  load-bearing scene — the near-sacrifice and its interruption. In
  the Gaiad's frame this is the moment the *logic of child sacrifice
  gets deflected* in the Abrahamic tradition. The chapter should
  sit with this without over-rationalizing it.
- Sodom and Gomorrah episode is available but not obligatory — the
  Akedah is the keeper.

### Isaac

- Structurally thin on his own; he's the link between Abraham and
  Jacob more than his own narrative anchor.
- Rebekah at the well. Esau and Jacob as the next twin-pair in the
  book (after Cain/Abel, in the Cain/Abel tradition per
  `genesis-opening.md`).
- Isaac's relative thinness is fine — he carries the line forward and
  doesn't demand his own chapter.

### Jacob

- The richest of the three narratively.
- **Wrestling the angel at Peniel** — renaming as Israel. Another
  single-scene anchor, comparable in weight to the Akedah. The
  wrestling scene is the kind of thing the Gaiad keeps rather than
  smooths.
- **Laban, Leah, Rachel** — the complicated marriage narrative;
  twelve sons across four mothers (Leah, Rachel, Bilhah, Zilpah).
- **Twelve sons → twelve tribes.** The genealogical structure that
  later Israelite identity rests on. This is the spine.

## What the chapter(s) do

- Carry the narrative with its texture intact. No rationalization of
  the Akedah, no smoothing of the Jacob-deceiving-Isaac scene, no
  flattening of the Leah/Rachel dynamic. The Gaiad keeps the
  weirdness.
- Anchor the genealogical structure for downstream use: Ishmael →
  Arabian material, Isaac → Jacob → twelve tribes → later Israel,
  → David, → Jesus's claimed descent.
- Name the Abraham-out-of-Ur move as a specific civilizational
  transition — moving from Mesopotamian religious substrate into the
  proto-Israelite frame. This connects loosely to
  `moses-bac-pivot.md` (the alphabet/BAC pivot comes later, in the
  same geographic arc).

## Coverage scope

- **At least one chapter, possibly a short sequence.** Abraham alone
  could carry a chapter; Jacob could carry a chapter; Isaac probably
  doesn't need one standalone.
- Emma: cleanest handling is probably Abraham-focused with Isaac
  folded in and Jacob getting his own beat, but that's a writing-
  desk call.

## Relation to neighbors

- **`genesis-opening.md`** — ends with Noah-as-migration; Abraham
  picks up the Near-Eastern thread.
- **`torah.md`** — Exodus and later; patriarchs are pre-Torah
  narrative material in one sense (Genesis), but structurally
  Genesis is part of the Torah corpus. This file handles the
  patriarchs as *narrative*; `torah.md` handles them as *canonized
  text*.
- **`arabia.md`** — Ishmael as Arabian progenitor runs through
  Abraham.
- **`david.md`**, **`jesus.md`** — downstream genealogical hooks.
- **Joseph** — needs its own file, see `joseph-egypt.md`
  (forthcoming or to-be-created).

## Open handles

- **Joseph** — gets his own file (`joseph-egypt.md`) rather than
  sitting under Jacob. The Joseph narrative is long enough and
  functionally distinct enough (Egypt sojourn, dream-interpretation,
  brothers-reunion) to warrant separate handling.
- **Melchizedek** — the priest-king who blesses Abraham. Strange
  one-scene figure with enormous later interpretive afterlife
  (Hebrews, Christian typology, Mormonism). The Gaiad may want to
  keep him as a single eerie beat within the Abraham chapter, not
  a separate file.
