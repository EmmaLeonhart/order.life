# Byzantium — the Eastern Roman continuation

The gap literally ends at 1453 (fall of Constantinople). Byzantium
is the thread that connects the Roman material (`rome.md`) to the
gap's endpoint.

## Why it needs its own file

- **The endpoint of the gap is the fall of Byzantium.** Ch 221 is
  "The Year of Three Falls" (1453). Without a Byzantium file, the
  gap's own climactic event has no build-up.
- **Byzantium is not just "late Rome."** The Eastern Roman Empire has
  its own theological, cultural, and political identity: Greek
  language, Orthodox Christianity, the Iconoclasm controversy, the
  Schism of 1054, Justinian's law code, Hagia Sophia.
- **Genealogical continuity.** The Byzantine imperial lines connect
  into the West Eurasian super-network. The Macedonian dynasty,
  the Komnenoi, the Palaiologoi all have genealogical connections
  that the data likely carries.

## What the chapter covers

- **Constantine / founding** — the move to Constantinople (330 CE).
  This is also the Sol Invictus / Christianity synthesis moment
  (covered in the Third Century Crisis / Sol Invictus chapter).
- **Justinian** (527–565) — the legal code, Hagia Sophia, the
  attempt to reconquer the West. The peak of Byzantine ambition.
- **The Schism** (1054) — the formal East-West split. Structural
  significance: the super-network's Christian branch divides, and
  the two halves develop differently.
- **The Crusades** — from the Byzantine perspective, the Fourth
  Crusade (1204) is the betrayal that cripples the empire. Not a
  Western-perspective Crusade chapter; a Byzantine-perspective one.
- **The fall** (1453) — Mehmed II, the siege, the end. This is Ch
  221's material; this chapter builds toward it.
