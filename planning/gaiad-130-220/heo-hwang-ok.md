# Princess Heo Hwang-ok — the one named Asian-to-Asian bridge

Roughly 48 CE. Princess of **Ayodhya** (India) who, according to the
Samguk Yusa and Garak-guk legends, traveled to **Geumgwan Gaya** (in
what is now southern Korea) and married King Suro, founder of the
kingdom. Her descendants seed Korean royal lines; some traditions
extend the chain further to **Japan**.

## Why the Gaiad wants her

The book's Asian side is deliberately looser than the West Eurasian
super-network — polytheistic traditions handled on their own terms,
surname / clan framing rather than royal-dynasty chains (see
`structure.md` §7 and CLAUDE.md). Inside that looseness there is
**one named bridge figure**, and it is her.

- A **single genealogical node** linking Indic, Korean, and Japanese
  material across otherwise-separate cultural frames.
- The story is memorable on its own terms: the long sea journey, the
  divine dream that prompts the marriage, the persistence of the
  Gimhae Kim and Heo lineages who still trace to her today.
- Plays against the assumption that Asian cultures sit in siloed
  parallel — here is a concrete named link that breaks the silo.

## Structural placement

Register B / early Register C — late mythic, bridging into fully
historical Three-Kingdoms-era Korea. Adjacent in period to material

  Hyeokgeose, Kim Alji).
- `jimmu.md` (Japan) — the bridge reaches Japan through later
  descendants.

## Gaiad handling

- One chapter, kept tight. The Asian frame is deliberately not
  over-written — she does her structural work in a single beat.
- The chapter can name the **still-living** descendant lineages (Gimhae
  Kim, Heo) rather than leaving the ancestry abstract — consistent with
  the gateway-ancestor treatment in `charlemagne.md` and `bustanai.md`.
