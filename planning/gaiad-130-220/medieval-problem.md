# The Medieval Problem — what organizes chapters after genealogy?

**Status: unsolved.** This file documents the structural challenge,
not a solution. The antiquity side of the 130–220 gap is well-framed;
the medieval side (~500–1453 CE) is not.

## The problem

The antiquity chapters are driven by **building up the genealogical
super-network**. Each chapter earns its place by establishing a
connection: patriarchs → Egypt → Israelite kingdoms → prophets →
Second Temple → Jesus → Rome → Charlemagne. The genealogical spine
gives every chapter a reason to exist — it's proving how the network
connects.

But by ~800 CE, the network is established. Charlemagne, the Caliphs,
Bustanai, the Heian-era Japanese lines — the gateway ancestors are in
place. After this point:

- **Descendant tracing is unnecessary.** Everyone knows most of Europe
  descends from Charlemagne, most of the Arab world from Muhammad. The
  Gaiad doesn't need to explicitly prove this.
- **Ancestry is more interesting than descent.** The backgrounds of
  the gateway ancestors (what came before them) matter more than their
  descendants (what came after). The antiquity chapters are the
  backgrounds; the medieval chapters are the descendants.
- **The genealogical engine that powered antiquity stops being the
  primary organizing principle.**

So: what replaces it? What makes a medieval chapter earn its place?

## What the medieval period actually contains

### The compilations (but these are antiquity chapters)

The Shahnameh (1000), the Eddas (1220), the Kojiki (712), the Lebor
Gabala (1000), the Secret History of the Mongols (1240), the Kebra
Nagast (14th c.), Geoffrey of Monmouth (1136) — all medieval works.
But their *content* is ancient. These chapters belong structurally to
antiquity even though they were written in the medieval period. They
don't solve the medieval problem.

### Things that are actually medieval

- **Christianization of countries.** The spread of Christianity across
  Europe — conversion of the Franks, the Anglo-Saxons, the Scandinavians,
  the Slavs, Lithuania (last pagan state, 1387). Each conversion is a
  local version of the Sol Invictus → Christianity transition.
- **The Viking expansion.** Lindisfarne (793) through the Norman
  Conquest (1066). Raids, settlements, the Danelaw, Normandy, Sicily,
  Vinland, the Rus. The Vikings are everywhere.
- **The Crusades.** Contact and collision between the Christian and
  Islamic worlds. The Fourth Crusade (1204) as betrayal of Byzantium.
- **The Mongol Empire.** Genghis Khan (1206), the largest contiguous
  land empire. The Mongol peace, the trade routes, the destruction of
  Baghdad (1258). The Mongols touch everything.
- **The Islamic Golden Age.** Abbasid Baghdad, the House of Wisdom,
  the preservation and transmission of Greek knowledge. Al-Khwarizmi,
  Ibn Sina, al-Ghazali, Ibn Rushd.
- **Zheng He's voyages** (1405–1433). China's brief moment of naval
  reach before turning inward.
- **The Black Death** (1346–1353). Restructures European society.
- **Timur** (1370–1405). The last great steppe conqueror before
  gunpowder changes everything.
- **Song dynasty China.** Printing, gunpowder, compass — the
  technological leadership of the world.
- **Heian / Kamakura / Muromachi Japan.** Tale of Genji (1008),
  the samurai class, Zen Buddhism.
- **The Delhi Sultanate** (1206). Islam's entry into India as a
  political force.
- **Kievan Rus** (882). The Slavic world enters the network.

## Why this is hard

1. **No single spine.** Antiquity has the Jewish/biblical sequence as
   a narrative through-line. The medieval period has parallel threads
   (Islamic, European, Asian) without an equivalent single spine.

2. **The genealogical driver is gone.** The thing that made every
   antiquity chapter feel necessary ("we need to establish this
   connection") doesn't apply. Medieval chapters need a different
   justification for existing.

3. **The mythic register is gone.** Register B (mythic antiquity) is
   over. Register C (proper history) is all that's left. The texture
   changes — no more mosaic/interpolative mythic material, just
   historiography. This may make the writing feel flatter.

4. **Diminishing genealogical returns.** Each medieval figure who gets
   a chapter needs to justify themselves on non-genealogical grounds.
   Genghis Khan is obviously important, but *why does he earn a Gaiad
   chapter specifically*? What does the Gaiad say about him that a
   history textbook doesn't?

5. **The "working backwards" instinct.** Emma's instinct that it's
   better to work backwards may be relevant here. The 1453 endpoint is
   clear (Byzantium falls, the modern period begins). Working backward
   from 1453 might reveal what the medieval chapters need to set up,
   rather than working forward from antiquity and hoping the momentum
   carries.

## Possible organizing principles (unresolved)

These are candidates, not decisions:

- **Christianization as a thread.** The spread of Christianity across
  Europe could function like the prophetic movement does for antiquity
  — a multi-chapter sequence showing a religious transformation in
  progress. But this is Eurocentric.

- **The contact-zone principle.** Every medieval chapter earns its
  place by showing a *collision* between previously separate worlds:
  Crusades (Christian ↔ Islamic), Mongols (steppe ↔ everyone), Viking
  expansion (Norse ↔ Christian Europe ↔ Islamic world), Zheng He
  (China ↔ Indian Ocean). The medieval period is about what happens
  when the networks established in antiquity start interacting.

- **The "what did the super-network produce?" question.** Now that the
  genealogical connections are in place, what did those connections
  actually create? The answer is: the medieval world. Each chapter
  shows a consequence of the connections antiquity built.

- **Working backward from 1453.** What does the fall of Constantinople
  require as build-up? What does *that* require? Chain backward until
  you hit the antiquity material.

## Working backward from 1453 — initial threads

Starting from the endpoint and chaining backward:

### 1453 and immediate context
- **Fall of Constantinople** (Ch 221) — the endpoint.
- **Japan: Sengoku period starting.** The warring states period is
  beginning around this time. The Asia arc's Japan climax has a
  medieval endpoint too.

### Islamic arc (major medieval thread)
The Gaiad's Islam is presented as **starting borderline Baha'i** —
highly syncretic due to Manichaean influence — and then **losing that
syncretism with the collapse of the Islamic Golden Age**. That
trajectory gives the Islamic arc a clear shape:
- Early Islam = syncretic/Manichaean (see `muhammad.md`,
  `islam-syncretic.md`)
- Islamic Golden Age = the peak of that syncretic, intellectually
  open character (Abbasid Baghdad, House of Wisdom)
- Collapse of the Golden Age = the loss of that character (Mongol
  sack of Baghdad 1258, plus internal theological shifts)
- Spread of Islam to Asia = the syncretic Islam going outward
  (Wali Sanga, Xiao'erjing — some of this may be early modern)

This is a real narrative arc with a beginning, peak, and fall. It
could be the medieval equivalent of the Jewish sequence in antiquity.

### The Seljuks and the Crusades
- The Seljuk Turks are the immediate context for the Crusades
  (Battle of Manzikert 1071 → First Crusade 1096).
- **Crusades coverage is mostly negative.** The Gaiad is not telling
  a heroic-Crusade story.
- The Fourth Crusade (1204) — the sack of Constantinople by
  fellow Christians — connects directly to `byzantium.md` and the
  1453 endpoint (the empire never recovers).

### The Mongols — multi-chapter arc (exception to one-chapter rule)
The Mongols get **multiple chapters** because they affected literally
everywhere. This is not a Genghis Khan biography stretched across
chapters — each chapter is about the Mongol impact on a specific
region. The Mongols are the through-line but each chapter belongs
to the place being hit.

**Regional impacts:**
- **Middle East** — destroyed Baghdad (1258), ended the Islamic
  Golden Age. This is the hinge of the Islamic arc.
- **China** — conquered but treated relatively well (Yuan dynasty).
- **Korea** — conquered.
- **Japan** — **defeated them**. The only place that did (typhoons
  1274, 1281). This is a major beat for the Japan emphasis — Japan
  as the place the Mongols couldn't take. **Nichiren** is in this
  chapter — he's comic relief. (He famously predicted the Mongol
  invasion and claimed vindication when it happened.)
- **Russia** — basically destroyed. The Mongol yoke defines Russian
  history for centuries.
- **Europe** — nearly wiped out, but **Ögedei Khan had a heart
  attack** and the Mongol army had to return for succession. A
  heart attack saved Western Europe.

### Mongol block — an eight-day arc (Sunday to Sunday)

The Mongol-to-Renaissance sequence forms an **eight-day arc**: a
week bookended by two Sundays. Eight-day arcs are relatively common
in the Gaiad (a week that represents something). The structure:

1. **Sunday (before the week)** — Mongols from their own perspective.
   The steppe, the unification, Genghis Khan. Sets up what's coming.
2. **Monday** — Mongols hit China. Yuan dynasty.
3. **Tuesday** — Mongols hit the Middle East. Baghdad destroyed
   (1258), Islamic Golden Age ends.
4. **Wednesday** — Mongols hit Europe/Russia. Russia devastated,
   Western Europe saved by Ögedei's heart attack.
5. **Thursday** — Mongols hit Japan. Defeated. Nichiren as comic
   relief. The only place that beat them.
6. **Friday** — Black Death in Europe. **Parallel to Good Friday.**
   Apocalyptic but Europe survives. Connected to the Mongols via
   Silk Road trade routes. The plague travels the roads the Mongols
   opened.
7. **Saturday** — Kenmu Restoration. Directly caused by the Mongol
   invasions bankrupting the Kamakura shogunate. **Portrayed as a
   positive thing.** Japan restructures after its victory.
8. **Sunday (after the week)** — Beginning of the Renaissance. The
   modern age starting. Resurrection after the Good Friday of the
   Black Death.

The liturgical echo is deliberate: the Black Death as Good Friday
(death/apocalypse), the Kenmu Restoration as Holy Saturday (quiet
restructuring, portrayed positively), and the Renaissance as Easter
Sunday (the new world beginning). Not exact — but the vibes are
there.

The Mongol moment is the medieval period's equivalent of the Bronze
Age Collapse — a single force that restructures everything it
touches. The BAC is Seam 2 (antiquity); the Mongol expansion is
the medieval seam.

## What to do next

This file exists to hold the uncertainty rather than pretend it's
resolved. The medieval period needs its own problem-framing session
comparable to what antiquity just got. The "working backwards from
1453" approach is starting to reveal structure — particularly the
Islamic arc (syncretic peak → collapse) as a possible medieval spine.

### India — deliberate absence as a structural choice
India more or less **ceases to be covered** after the Maurya Empire
(~1st century CE). The decision is that post-Maurya India is not
considered dynamic enough to earn ongoing chapter attention in the
130–220 gap. The caste system is blamed for this stagnation — the
book is explicitly critical.

- The Asia arc (`asia-arc.md`) follows Buddhism east out of India.
  After Buddhism leaves, India thins out in coverage. This is the
  `founder-followership-displacement.md` dynamic in action.
- Islam in India is considered **early modern**, not medieval — the
  Delhi Sultanate and Mughals are probably covered in a post-1453
  chapter rather than in this gap.
- **Possible treatment:** one compressed chapter starting at the
  **Delhi Sultanate (1206)** — Islam's arrival as the opening beat,
  covering ~1,500 years of stagnation, with the Mughals as the
  climax. Starting at the Delhi Sultanate rather than the Mughals
  gives the chapter a wider window and frees the Mongol chapter
  from having to carry India as a side thread.
- This chapter likely sits late in the gap or early in the post-1453
  block — placement TBD.

### The approach to 1453 — the modern age was already starting

The gap ends at 1453, but the Gaiad's position is that **Columbus
did not start the modern age — the modern age was already starting**.
The century before Columbus (roughly 1350–1450) was already moving
fast. Columbus "discovering" the Americas is not a rupture but an
**obvious thing being discovered because modernity had already hit**.
People really should have known about the Americas before Columbus;
the fact that they "discovered" them when they did is evidence of
acceleration already underway.

**The Americas specifically:** The Gaiad's position is not that anyone
should have magically known about the Americas. It's that the Americas
are a third of the planet's landmass — they're not hidden. The moment
you start doing systematic cartography and proper navigational
record-keeping, you find them. You can't not find them. Columbus
isn't a visionary on a fateful journey — he's the first person
operating within an information infrastructure mature enough that the
obvious thing gets noticed. The "discovery" is evidence that
modernity (systematic information-tracking) had already arrived, not
that a heroic individual caused it.

This has structural implications for how the gap's final chapters
work. The ending is not a sharp break — it's a period of acceleration
that makes the break at 1453 / 1492 feel inevitable rather than
surprising. The modern age is defined by information infrastructure,
not by individual acts of exploration.

**The Black Death gets its own chapter.** Confirmed.

Events clustering near the gap's end:
- **Japan: Sengoku period beginning** (~1467). The warring states
  period is starting.
- **Ottoman Empire forming.** The Gunpowder Empires are coming into
  existence.
- **Black Death aftermath** (post-1353). European society restructured.
- **Gutenberg press** (1440). Information technology shift.
- **Zheng He** (1405–1433). China's brief naval reach.
- **Timur** (1370–1405). Last great steppe conqueror.

The common thread: multiple civilizations are simultaneously
accelerating or restructuring. The "modern age" is not a European
event that happens to everyone else — it's a global phase transition
that Europeans happen to name after their own experience of it.

### China — a coherent arc is emerging

China's medieval treatment is NOT a compressed-chapter situation like
Mesopotamia or India. There's a real arc here, organized around
**Buddhism's trajectory in China**:

1. **Fall of the Han Dynasty / Three Kingdoms** (~220 CE) — the
   political collapse that creates the space for Buddhism to enter
   China. Buddhism arrives into a fractured landscape.
2. **Rise of Buddhism in China** — gradual adoption, translation of
   sutras, the growth of Chinese Buddhist schools. Connects to
   `xuanzang.md` (the journey to bring texts back from India) and
   `asia-arc.md` (Mahayana moves east).
3. **Wu Zetian** (~690–705 CE) — the high point. Wu Zetian is a
   Buddhist-aligned ruler; her reign represents Buddhism's peak
   political influence in China. She has specific genealogical work
   in the wikibase (Tang imperial line, Yang clan maternal line;
   possibly Uyghur ancestry added but may have been reverted).
4. **Huichang Persecution** (~845 CE, Emperor Wuzong) — the major
   suppression of Buddhism after Wu Zetian's era. Buddhism doesn't
   die but is permanently diminished as a political force.
5. **Buddhism becomes thoroughly Chinese** — by ~1200, Buddhism in
   China is so thoroughly indigenized it won't go away, but it's no
   longer the dynamic, expanding force it was. Chan/Zen is the
   mature form.
6. **Mongol conquest** — the Yuan dynasty arrives. Not much time
   between the persecution and the Mongols, which compresses the
   late period.

This arc parallels the Islamic arc: both are stories of a religion
arriving, peaking, being suppressed or collapsing, and leaving a
permanent residue. Buddhism in China and Islam in the Middle East
follow roughly similar trajectories but with different causes of
decline (state persecution vs. Mongol destruction).

**Kukai and Saicho timing:** Both came BEFORE the Huichang
persecution. Kukai (774–835) and Saicho (767–822) studied in Tang
China and brought esoteric Buddhism to Japan while China was still
at its Buddhist peak. Kukai died in 835; the persecution hit in
842–846. They carried Buddhism to Japan just before China crushed
it. Narratively perfect: they got out with the goods before the
house burned down. This is the handoff moment from the China arc
to the Japan arc.

### China — full chapter sequence (emerging)

China probably gets quite a few chapters, not a compressed treatment:

1. **Yellow Emperor → Xia Dynasty** — mythic/legendary period
2. **Shang Dynasty** — oracle bones, bronze age China
3. **Spring and Autumn Period** — Confucius, the philosophical
   flowering, the Warring States
4. **Qin** — unification, Qin Shi Huang, the wall, legalism
5. **Han** (possibly combined with Qin) — the empire that defines
   "Chinese." During the Han, the real rise of Buddhism begins,
   along with Manichaeism to some extent.
6. **Post-Han / Buddhism's peak** — Three Kingdoms, Buddhism's
   growth, Wu Zetian, Tang dynasty
7. **Huichang Persecution → indigenized Buddhism** — the suppression
   and its aftermath; Buddhism becomes permanently Chinese but
   loses political force. Kukai/Saicho have already carried it to
   Japan by this point.

This is 5–7 chapter-beats for China across the whole gap. However,
the overall attitude toward medieval China is: **China is mid**. The
"Middle Kingdom" in a double sense. China had stability, could hold
its own in a way India couldn't (not stagnating under caste), but
still got conquered (Mongols, etc.). Until roughly 1950, China was
just mid. Not dramatically interesting enough to drive the narrative.
Japan and Korea viewed it as the origin of civilization, and the
Gaiad respects that, but the treatment is: important waypoint in the
Asia arc, not a dramatic centerpiece. The Manchus are not being
included.

**Xuanzang / Journey to the West:** Already has `xuanzang.md`. The
Buddhists Xuanzang encountered on his journey would be broadly
Theravada-tradition figures. The Journey to the West chapter uses
modern city names for the places he traveled through.

**General rule:** Characters don't continue across chapters unless
they're a deity or in the final compressed-time chapters.
Charlemagne is really significant (unified Western Europe) and should
have a lot, but it's one chapter, not a multi-chapter arc.

**Exception: the Mongols.** The Mongols get multiple chapters because
they're not a character — they're an event hitting every region.
See "The Mongols" section above.

**Wu Zetian specifically:** Tagged as a Gaiad character (P39=Q153802)
in the wikibase. Has heraldic arms (Wu Zetian Arms.svg). Tang
imperial genealogy goes back 13 generations to Western Jin (~300s CE)
but does NOT connect to Adam or the West Eurasian super-network —
it's a purely Chinese genealogical island. Some Indian/Uyghur
ancestry work may have been done and reverted.

### Europe — not empire-by-empire

The medieval European material is NOT being treated as a parade of
specific empires rising and falling. The Gaiad is not doing "the
Carolingian Empire, then the Holy Roman Empire, then the Plantagenets"
etc. What gets covered:

- **Charlemagne** — already has a file, the gateway ancestor chapter.
  A lot of stuff going on with it; this is a major chapter.
- **Byzantine Empire** — already has a file, connects to 1453.
- **The Roman Empire broadly** — covered through the Third Century
  Crisis / Sol Invictus chapter and Byzantium.
- Specific episodes (Vikings, Crusades, Marozia, Ireland) get
  chapters not because they're "European history" but because they
  have specific Gaiad-relevant content.

The bias, to the extent there is one, is that medieval material
trends toward Japan (Asia arc) rather than toward Europe. Europe
gets Charlemagne, Byzantium, and the X-Files episodes; it doesn't
get a comprehensive survey of medieval European kingdoms.

### Islamic Golden Age — substantial but size uncertain

The Islamic Golden Age (Abbasid Baghdad, House of Wisdom) deserves
decent coverage. How much depends on how many chapters the medieval
section has to spare. The Islamic arc (syncretic start → Golden Age
peak → Mongol collapse) is emerging as the medieval period's main
narrative spine, so the Golden Age is probably the peak chapter(s)
of that arc.

### Buddhism persecution chain as structural thread

A structural thread is emerging: Buddhism is persecuted out of India
→ moves to China → persecuted in China (Huichang) → moves to Japan.
Each persecution is a handoff. Tibet fits into this as a side branch
— Buddhism also goes to Tibet, but the main arc follows the
eastward movement. Kashmir Shaivism (viewed positively) is part of
what fills the space Buddhism leaves in India.

## Emerging decisions (from working-backward session)

### Scientific advancement as a medieval theme
The medieval period (both European and Islamic) is the point where
**scientific advancement becomes a real topic**. This is probably
the first time in the Gaiad where science is discussed as a thing
in itself. The Islamic Golden Age's House of Wisdom material and
European scholasticism / early scientific method are both candidates
for coverage. This may be part of what organizes the medieval
chapters — the emergence of systematic knowledge-production.

### Europe: what gets covered, what doesn't
**Covered:**
- The Roman Empire and Byzantine Empire (already have files)
- Western Roman Empire successor states (broadly)
- Western Europe — moving toward being centered on:
  - **Britain** — decent amount of stuff (per `europe-arc.md` tilt)
  - **Germany** — the Teutonic Knights specifically as the thing that
    makes "Germany" a concept. Important.
  - **Russia** — its convergence to Christianity, the Russian empire
  - **France** — implied by Charlemagne / Western Europe focus
- **Christianization of Lithuania** — already has
  `lithuania-counterfactual.md`
- **Russian and Bulgarian empires** — their convergence to
  Christianity specifically

**NOT covered:**
- The Balkans generally — not needed
- Not every European kingdom or empire. No parade of specific
  polities. Episodes and themes, not comprehensive survey.

The European coverage is **Western Europe + Russia**, not all of
Europe. The Balkans, Iberia (except as Crusade-adjacent?), and
smaller Eastern European states are largely skipped.

### Naming convention: modern city names in the classical era
**Structural decision (firm).** The classical era uses **modern names
for cities and places**. The Trojan War features a battle at
**Antalya**, not "ancient Lycia" or whatever the classical name is.
This is a deliberate choice — it grounds the ancient material in
recognizable geography, making the reader aware that these events
happened in places that still exist. The defamiliarization works in
reverse: instead of making the ancient exotic, it makes the modern
ancient.
- India stagnation chapter: starts ~1 CE, covers 1,200 years of
  caste-system stagnation, ends at Delhi Sultanate (1206). See
  `india-stagnation.md`.
