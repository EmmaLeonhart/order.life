# The Medieval Problem — what organizes chapters after genealogy?

**Status: unsolved.** This file documents the structural challenge,
not a solution. The antiquity side of the 130–220 gap is well-framed;
the medieval side (~500–1453 CE) is not.

## The problem

The antiquity chapters are driven by **building up the genealogical
super-network**. Each chapter earns its place by establishing a
connection: patriarchs → Egypt → Israelite kingdoms → prophets →
Second Temple → Jesus → Rome → Charlemagne. The genealogical spine
gives every chapter a reason to exist — it's proving how the network
connects.

But by ~800 CE, the network is established. Charlemagne, the Caliphs,
Bustanai, the Heian-era Japanese lines — the gateway ancestors are in
place. After this point:

- **Descendant tracing is unnecessary.** Everyone knows most of Europe
  descends from Charlemagne, most of the Arab world from Muhammad. The
  Gaiad doesn't need to explicitly prove this.
- **Ancestry is more interesting than descent.** The backgrounds of
  the gateway ancestors (what came before them) matter more than their
  descendants (what came after). The antiquity chapters are the
  backgrounds; the medieval chapters are the descendants.
- **The genealogical engine that powered antiquity stops being the
  primary organizing principle.**

So: what replaces it? What makes a medieval chapter earn its place?

## What the medieval period actually contains

### The compilations (but these are antiquity chapters)

The Shahnameh (1000), the Eddas (1220), the Kojiki (712), the Lebor
Gabala (1000), the Secret History of the Mongols (1240), the Kebra
Nagast (14th c.), Geoffrey of Monmouth (1136) — all medieval works.
But their *content* is ancient. These chapters belong structurally to
antiquity even though they were written in the medieval period. They
don't solve the medieval problem.

### Things that are actually medieval

- **Christianization of countries.** The spread of Christianity across
  Europe — conversion of the Franks, the Anglo-Saxons, the Scandinavians,
  the Slavs, Lithuania (last pagan state, 1387). Each conversion is a
  local version of the Sol Invictus → Christianity transition.
- **The Viking expansion.** Lindisfarne (793) through the Norman
  Conquest (1066). Raids, settlements, the Danelaw, Normandy, Sicily,
  Vinland, the Rus. The Vikings are everywhere.
- **The Crusades.** Contact and collision between the Christian and
  Islamic worlds. The Fourth Crusade (1204) as betrayal of Byzantium.
- **The Mongol Empire.** Genghis Khan (1206), the largest contiguous
  land empire. The Mongol peace, the trade routes, the destruction of
  Baghdad (1258). The Mongols touch everything.
- **The Islamic Golden Age.** Abbasid Baghdad, the House of Wisdom,
  the preservation and transmission of Greek knowledge. Al-Khwarizmi,
  Ibn Sina, al-Ghazali, Ibn Rushd.
- **Zheng He's voyages** (1405–1433). China's brief moment of naval
  reach before turning inward.
- **The Black Death** (1346–1353). Restructures European society.
- **Timur** (1370–1405). The last great steppe conqueror before
  gunpowder changes everything.
- **Song dynasty China.** Printing, gunpowder, compass — the
  technological leadership of the world.
- **Heian / Kamakura / Muromachi Japan.** Tale of Genji (1008),
  the samurai class, Zen Buddhism.
- **The Delhi Sultanate** (1206). Islam's entry into India as a
  political force.
- **Kievan Rus** (882). The Slavic world enters the network.

## Why this is hard

1. **No single spine.** Antiquity has the Jewish/biblical sequence as
   a narrative through-line. The medieval period has parallel threads
   (Islamic, European, Asian) without an equivalent single spine.

2. **The genealogical driver is gone.** The thing that made every
   antiquity chapter feel necessary ("we need to establish this
   connection") doesn't apply. Medieval chapters need a different
   justification for existing.

3. **The mythic register is gone.** Register B (mythic antiquity) is
   over. Register C (proper history) is all that's left. The texture
   changes — no more mosaic/interpolative mythic material, just
   historiography. This may make the writing feel flatter.

4. **Diminishing genealogical returns.** Each medieval figure who gets
   a chapter needs to justify themselves on non-genealogical grounds.
   Genghis Khan is obviously important, but *why does he earn a Gaiad
   chapter specifically*? What does the Gaiad say about him that a
   history textbook doesn't?

5. **The "working backwards" instinct.** Emma's instinct that it's
   better to work backwards may be relevant here. The 1453 endpoint is
   clear (Byzantium falls, the modern period begins). Working backward
   from 1453 might reveal what the medieval chapters need to set up,
   rather than working forward from antiquity and hoping the momentum
   carries.

## Possible organizing principles (unresolved)

These are candidates, not decisions:

- **Christianization as a thread.** The spread of Christianity across
  Europe could function like the prophetic movement does for antiquity
  — a multi-chapter sequence showing a religious transformation in
  progress. But this is Eurocentric.

- **The contact-zone principle.** Every medieval chapter earns its
  place by showing a *collision* between previously separate worlds:
  Crusades (Christian ↔ Islamic), Mongols (steppe ↔ everyone), Viking
  expansion (Norse ↔ Christian Europe ↔ Islamic world), Zheng He
  (China ↔ Indian Ocean). The medieval period is about what happens
  when the networks established in antiquity start interacting.

- **The "what did the super-network produce?" question.** Now that the
  genealogical connections are in place, what did those connections
  actually create? The answer is: the medieval world. Each chapter
  shows a consequence of the connections antiquity built.

- **Working backward from 1453.** What does the fall of Constantinople
  require as build-up? What does *that* require? Chain backward until
  you hit the antiquity material.

## What to do next

This file exists to hold the uncertainty rather than pretend it's
resolved. The medieval period needs its own problem-framing session
comparable to what antiquity just got. The "working backwards from
1453" instinct is probably the right entry point.
