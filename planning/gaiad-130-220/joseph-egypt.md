# Joseph in Egypt

Bridge chapter between the patriarchal sequence (`abraham-jacob.md`)
and the Exodus narrative (`torah.md`, `moses-bac-pivot.md`). Joseph is
functionally distinct enough from his father Jacob to deserve his own
treatment.

## The Joseph arc

- **Sold into Egypt by his brothers.** The coat-of-many-colors, the
  pit, the traders.
- **Potiphar's household and the prison.** Mrs. Potiphar, the
  false-accusation scene.
- **Dream interpretation for Pharaoh.** Seven fat cows / seven lean
  cows; the seven-years-plenty / seven-years-famine reading. Joseph
  rises to vizier.
- **Famine administration.** Joseph implements grain storage and
  redistribution, eventually buying up all Egyptian land for Pharaoh
  as the famine bites. This is often glossed over in Sunday-school
  retellings; the Gaiad should keep the weirdness — Joseph as a
  *state-building* figure, not just a beloved dreamer.
- **Brothers-reunion.** Judah's intervention, Joseph's disclosure,
  the family relocation to Goshen.

## What this chapter does structurally

- **Sets up the Exodus.** Jacob's family in Goshen becomes the
  Hebrews in Egypt, who become the enslaved population, who become
  the Exodus generation. Without Joseph there's no Israelite presence
  in Egypt to escape from.
- **Shows a different kind of biblical hero.** Joseph is not a
  covenant-maker (Abraham), wrestler (Jacob), or lawgiver (Moses).
  He is an administrator, interpreter, diplomat. The Gaiad can use
  this to name the different *kinds* of biblical protagonism — not
  all founding-adjacent figures are in the same structural register.
- **Crosses into Egyptian civilization.** Joseph is the point of
  contact between the proto-Israelite narrative and the Egyptian
  state. Connects to `pharaohs.md` — the Pharaoh-of-Joseph is
  probably a Hyksos or Second Intermediate Period figure in the
  chronological frame most compatible with the Genesis-Exodus
  sequence, though historicity is not the Gaiad's obligation.

## Themes worth keeping

- **The dreams as competence, not magic.** Joseph's dream
  interpretation is treated as a real interpretive skill, not a
  divine party trick. The Gaiad leans on *competence* readings of
  biblical figures where available.
- **Moral ambiguity of the famine administration.** Joseph saves
  Egypt by, essentially, dispossessing its peasantry. This is there
  in the text (Genesis 47) and tends to get edited out of
  devotional retellings. The Gaiad keeps it.
- **The forgiveness scene as sincere-but-complicated.** "You meant
  it for evil, but God meant it for good." The Gaiad can hold this
  as a real emotional moment without flattening the betrayal.

## Relation to neighbors

- **`abraham-jacob.md`** — Joseph is Jacob's favored son; his arc
  begins inside Jacob's family and carries forward.
- **`pharaohs.md`** — Joseph's Pharaoh and the later Exodus Pharaoh
  live in this file's companion. The Gaiad's handling of Egypt as
  a civilization and the handling of specific Pharaohs-in-Hebrew-
  narrative overlap here.
- **`torah.md`** — Joseph's death closes Genesis; Exodus opens
  with "a new king arose over Egypt who did not know Joseph," which
  is the seam this chapter creates.
- **`moses-bac-pivot.md`** — Moses comes centuries later (BAC
  chronology); Joseph's arrangement is the upstream reason there's a
  Hebrew population in Egypt for Moses to lead out.

## Coverage scope

- **Probably one chapter.** Joseph's arc is long but fairly
  self-contained. The dreams/prison/vizier/famine/reunion beats
  could compress into one chapter without losing their force.
