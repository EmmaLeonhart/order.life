# Ramayana

One of the two great Hindu epics, must-include per the Asia arc
(`asia-arc.md`). Sister file: `mahabharata.md`.

## Core narrative

- **Rama** — prince of Ayodhya, exiled to the forest on the eve of
  his coronation by his stepmother's scheming.
- **Sita** — Rama's wife; accompanies him into exile; is abducted by
  the demon-king Ravana of Lanka.
- **Lakshmana** — Rama's loyal brother; accompanies exile.
- **Hanuman** — monkey-god ally; devoted to Rama; leaps to Lanka,
  finds Sita, burns the city.
- **Ravana** — ten-headed demon king; a genuinely complex antagonist
  in the tradition, not a flat villain (he's a learned Brahmin, a
  devotee of Shiva, a competent ruler — *and* he abducts Sita).
- **The war** — Rama's monkey-and-bear army crosses to Lanka, defeats
  Ravana, rescues Sita.
- **The return.** Rama's coronation at Ayodhya.
- **The later books** (Uttara Kanda) — Sita's second banishment on
  suspicion; her return to the earth; Rama's mourning. Often
  editorially uncomfortable in modern retellings; the Gaiad keeps it
  rather than flattening it into a "they lived happily ever after"
  ending.

## What the Gaiad keeps

- **Ravana's complexity.** Not a simple evil. A scholar, a king, a
  devotee — and a figure whose desire for Sita destroys him. The
  Gaiad's tone (see `../gaiad-tone-and-founders.md`) leans toward
  keeping this kind of complication rather than smoothing it.
- **Hanuman as a full figure.** Not comic relief. Central to the
  Hindu devotional landscape; his own cults, his own theology, his
  own structural weight. Probably worth dedicated scene-level
  attention even inside a compressed Ramayana chapter.
- **Sita's ordeal by fire, second banishment, descent into the
  earth.** The Gaiad does not edit these out. They are part of the
  text's texture and raise questions the tradition itself has
  wrestled with — keep the wrestling visible.
- **Ayodhya as a real place.** Feeds into `heo-hwang-ok.md` — the
  Ayodhya → Korea → Japan bridge runs through here. The fact that
  the Ramayana's Ayodhya is remembered as sending a princess to
  Korea (in the 48 CE legend) is a cross-traditional hook the
  Ramayana chapter can quietly set up.

## The Ramayana as traveling text

- **SE Asian adaptations** (see `southeast-asia.md` / might-include)
  — Thai *Ramakien*, Cambodian *Reamker*, Indonesian wayang Ramayana.
  The Ramayana is one of the most *portable* narrative structures in
  world literature; its spread into Southeast Asia is itself part of
  the story and probably deserves naming even without a dedicated
  Ramakien chapter.
- **Tibetan, Burmese, Laotian** versions also exist. The cross-Asian
  distribution of the Ramayana is a data point about how Hindu
  narrative carried eastward even where Hinduism as institution
  didn't.
