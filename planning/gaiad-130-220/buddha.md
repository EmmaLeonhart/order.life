# Buddha — the hardest case, most actively critical portrayal

Emma's own framing: *"my depiction of the Buddha is probably the most
actively critical, even though it comes out well and even though
Buddhism ends up coming out with a good depiction."*

The Buddha is universally venerated, which makes this constrained.
The Gaiad leans hard into critical material that the tradition has
soft-pedaled. Broader Buddhism treatment and open threads live in
`../gaiad-tone-and-founders.md` §8; this file is the per-figure vision.

## The Buddha as a person

- **High caste (Kshatriya), not Brahmin.** Systematically denied the
  ability to pursue a priestly position because of his caste — which
  was what he wanted.
- **Hates the caste system** — AND uses his caste position to his own
  benefit. The tension is the point.
- **Loser of the politics of the Shakya Republic.** A failed political
  career. The Shakya are framed as **Athens-like** — a democratic-edge
  culture on the periphery, somewhat liminal to the core Indo-Aryan
  order. The Sakya-Scythian connection fits here (ambiguous inclusion
  in Hindu genealogies: "some people do, but there's a bit of
  questionableness with it").
- Kept out as a **foreigner**; denied various paths.
- Eventually manages to "get into it" by treating himself better and
  adapting his principles a bit.
- Forms a stable monastic community; this later becomes Buddhism.

## The Buddha as a cult leader

Behaves much like a cult leader, though one whose impact is ultimately
good. The comparison to Jesus is deliberate: both figures have outcomes
that outrun their own trajectories. But the Buddha's cult-leader
characteristics are **more pronounced** in the Gaiad's framing.

On the table as critical material:
- Abandonment of Yaśodharā and Rāhula (name literally
  "fetter"/"obstacle").
- Years of extreme asceticism framed as stubbornness / susceptibility
  to śramaṇa peer culture, not a necessary spiritual stage.

## The Middle Way — self-serving but beneficial

The Middle Path is **self-serving** (justifies eating, sleeping
comfortably, accepting donations — stopping his own suffering), AND
**ultimately beneficial**. Both true at once.

## Byronic hero → transformed

- Before the Middle Path: essentially a **Byronic hero**. Not a bad
  guy, but emotionally troubled, making mistakes, rejecting things,
  tragic.
- After the Middle Path: genuinely transformed. After a life full of
  tragedy, rejection, and mistakes, he really does work things out.

## Enlightenment — cosmologically deflated

Enlightenment is **material and associated with high-level material
success** — closer to modern "manifesting" framings than to traditional
Buddhist cosmology. It clearly transformed him as a person and improved
his life materially. But it is **not a cosmically unique event**: this
kind of stuff happens to people a lot, he wants people to experience it
too, and many do.

**Cosmologically deflated, but ambiguous on cosmology itself.**
