# The Mongol Block — an eight-day arc (Sunday to Sunday)

**Structural decision (firm).** The Mongol-to-Renaissance sequence
is an eight-day arc: a week bookended by two Sundays. This is the
first concrete chapter sequence for the medieval period.

## The sequence

1. **Sunday (before the week)** — Mongols from their own perspective.
   The steppe, the unification, Genghis Khan. Sets up what's coming.
2. **Monday** — Mongols hit China. Yuan dynasty.
3. **Tuesday** — Mongols hit the Middle East. Baghdad destroyed
   (1258), Islamic Golden Age ends.
4. **Wednesday** — Mongols hit Europe/Russia. Russia devastated,
   Western Europe saved by Ögedei's heart attack.
5. **Thursday** — Mongols hit Japan. Defeated — the only place that
   beat them. Nichiren as comic relief.
6. **Friday** — Black Death in Europe. **Parallel to Good Friday.**
   Apocalyptic but Europe survives. Connected to the Mongols via
   Silk Road trade routes — the plague travels the roads the Mongols
   opened.
7. **Saturday** — Kenmu Restoration. Directly caused by the Mongol
   invasions bankrupting the Kamakura shogunate. **Portrayed as a
   positive thing.** Japan restructures after its victory.
8. **Sunday (after the week)** — Beginning of the Renaissance. The
   modern age starting. Resurrection after the Good Friday of the
   Black Death.

## Liturgical echo

The Black Death as Good Friday (death/apocalypse), the Kenmu
Restoration as Holy Saturday (quiet positive restructuring), and
the Renaissance as Easter Sunday (the new world beginning). Not
exact — but the vibes are deliberately there.

## Perspective shifts

The Mongol block is an example of the Gaiad's frequent use of
**the same events told from different perspectives**. The Mongol
expansion is one event, but each chapter tells it from the
perspective of the place being hit. This is a common structural
move in the Gaiad:

- **Mongol block** — same event, four regional perspectives
  (China, Middle East, Europe, Japan) plus the Mongol perspective
  itself.
- **Book of Leo (ch 221–252)** — covers 1492–1830 entirely from the
  indigenous American perspective. The European "Age of Exploration"
  is told from the other side.
- **Mesopotamia** (`mesopotamia.md`) — the longest single-perspective
  compression. 10,000+ years of Mesopotamian history in one chapter.
  Not a perspective *shift* but the longest sustained compression,
  which is its own kind of perspective.
- **Bronze Age Collapse** (`bronze-age-collapse.md`) — Trojan War
  and Exodus as the same event from Greek/Anatolian vs.
  Egyptian/Levantine perspectives.

The multi-perspective approach is a core structural tool, not a
one-off. It allows the Gaiad to cover the same historical moment
from inside different civilizations without pretending there's a
neutral omniscient view.

## Placement in the gap

The Mongol block sits somewhere in the late-medieval portion of the
gap. Exact chapter numbers emerge at the writing desk. The block
covers roughly 1206 (Genghis Khan) through ~1350 (Black Death
aftermath / early Renaissance stirrings).

**Before the block:** the Islamic Golden Age peak, Charlemagne,
Crusades, the Christianization material, Wu Zetian / Huichang
persecution / Buddhism-to-Japan handoff.

**After the block:** ~200 years remain between the Renaissance
beginning and the 1453 endpoint. What fills this gap is unsettled.
Candidates: Timur (1370–1405), Lithuania Christianized (1387),
Zheng He (1405–1433), Gutenberg (1440), Ottoman rise, Sengoku
beginning (~1467), Joan of Arc (1429). This is the "approach to
1453" zone documented in `medieval-problem.md`.
