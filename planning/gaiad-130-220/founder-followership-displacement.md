# Founder-followership displacement — Jesus and Buddha in parallel

Short cross-cutting note. Both Jesus and the Buddha share a structural
feature the Gaiad finds interesting enough to name: **the primary
followership of the religion ends up as a relatively different ethnic
group, in a relatively different location, than the founder.**

## The parallel

- **Jesus.** Jewish founder; Christianity's center of gravity lands
  with Gentile / Greco-Roman followership, then Europe, then
  ultimately everywhere-but-its-origin-community.
- **The Buddha.** North Indian founder; Buddhism's center of gravity
  lands in East and Southeast Asia — China, Tibet, Korea, Japan,
  Southeast Asia — largely *leaving India* within a few centuries.

Islam is partially in the same pattern (Arabian founder, primary
demographic weight eventually in non-Arab populations — Indonesia,
South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, etc.), but the Arabian heartland
retained more continuity than Jewish-Christianity or Indian-Buddhism
did, so the parallel is weaker. Emma: "Christianity kind of had this,
but both [Jesus and Buddha] have it."

## What this does for the Gaiad

- Lets the `jesus.md` and `buddha.md` chapters rhyme structurally
  without either being a straightforward analogue of the other. Both
  founders see their message carried primarily by people who are not
  like them, in places they never visited.
- Interacts with the descendants theme (see
  `gateway-ancestor-ubiquity.md`): the *bloodline* diffuses in one
  direction while the *followership* diffuses in another. For Jesus,
  the bloodline goes into the Roman / European world that also becomes
  the followership, so they partially overlap. For the Buddha, the
  bloodline (via Rāhula, see `buddha.md`) goes east **alongside** the
  Manichaean corridor and follows the same geography as the
  followership — the Gaiad's Rāhula-to-China move deliberately aligns
  bloodline with followership in a way the historical tradition did
  not.
- Quietly undercuts the "home-culture ownership" reading of each
  religion. Neither Jesus nor the Buddha is fully owned by the
  demographic group that now practices their religion most widely —
  and that displacement is itself part of the story, not a footnote.
