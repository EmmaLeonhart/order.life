# Persia — Shahnameh, Zoroastrianism, and the super-network bridge

**Hard requirement.** Persia is load-bearing for three distinct systems
at once: the mythic-cycle bridge (Shahnameh), the monotheism framework
(Zoroastrianism), and the genealogical super-network (Sasanian royals →
Bustanai).

## The Shahnameh as mythic-cycle bridge

The *Shahnameh* (Ferdowsi, c. 1000 CE) is one of the great national
epics: Keyumars → Pishdadian dynasty → Kayanian dynasty → Sasanian
dynasty. The legendary-to-historical transition is **gradual** — the
whole structural point of the Shahnameh is that it makes the transition
continuously, without a sharp mythic/historical break. This is
structurally comparable to Livy for Rome or the Kojiki for Japan, but
with a longer span and a smoother gradient.

## Zoroastrianism — not originally monotheistic

Per the Gaiad's monotheism framework: Persia is one of the four sites
where monotheism emerged (alongside Judea, Rome, and Egypt-failed).
But the Gaiad treats Zoroastrianism as **not originally monotheistic**.
Monotheism emerged gradually through an aniconic period where the
Yazatas were forgotten. The religion is "pneumatic" in character —
spirit-focused rather than god-focused.

This means the Persia chapter carries a theological argument: the
standard story of Zoroaster-as-monotheist is deflated. What happened
was a drift, not a founding revelation.

## Genealogical spine

- The Sasanian royal house connects directly to **Bustanai**
  (`bustanai.md`) through the princess Dara. The Shahnameh
  mythologizes exactly the dynasty that the genealogical data makes
  concrete.
- **Cyrus the Great** — the Achaemenid empire is historically massive
  and sits in the chronology. Probably absorbed into this chapter
  rather than getting his own file.
- The Persian royal material connects into the broader super-network:
  Muhammad-via-Rome (`muhammad.md`), Bustanai as the Jewish-Persian-
  Islamic triple bridge, and the Sasanian collapse as the context for
  early Islam.

## Coverage scope

Probably two chapter-beats:
1. **Mythic Persia / Shahnameh** — the epic itself, the Pishdadian and
   Kayanian material, the Zoroastrian theological trajectory.
2. **Historical Persia** — Achaemenids (Cyrus, Darius), the Persian
   Wars as context, Alexander's conquest, Parthians, Sasanians. The
   Sasanian endpoint feeds directly into the Bustanai marriage and the
   Islamic conquest.

Could compress to one chapter if the Shahnameh material is woven into
the historical spine rather than treated separately. Could expand if
the Zoroastrian theological argument needs breathing room.
