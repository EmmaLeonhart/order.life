# India — 1,200 years of stagnation under the caste system

**Structural decision.** India gets one compressed chapter covering
roughly 1 CE to the founding of the Delhi Sultanate (1206 CE). The
chapter depicts India as being **held back by the caste system** for
this entire period. The caste system is the villain; the Delhi
Sultanate conquest is the endpoint.

## What the chapter covers

- **Starting point: ~1 CE.** After the Maurya Empire / Ashoka's
  edicts, after Buddhism has begun its eastward departure. The Asia
  arc (`asia-arc.md`) follows Buddhism out; this chapter stays behind
  and shows what India became without it.
- **1,200 years of relative stasis.** The Gupta Empire, the various
  medieval Hindu kingdoms — these exist but the chapter's framing is
  that nothing structurally changes. The caste system ossifies Indian
  society. Dynasties rise and fall but the social structure remains
  locked.
- **Endpoint: Delhi Sultanate (1206).** Islam's arrival as a
  political force breaks the pattern. The chapter ends here — the
  Mughals and what follows are a different story (probably early
  modern).

## The argument

The book is **explicitly critical of the caste system**. This is not
a neutral survey of medieval Indian history — it's a chapter about
what happens when a civilization locks itself into a rigid social
hierarchy. The speed of the chapter (1,200 years compressed) is itself
the argument, the same structural move as `mesopotamia.md` (speed as
theme) but applied to stasis rather than chaos.

The contrast is deliberate:
- Mesopotamia: fast chapter because civilizations keep destroying
  each other (chaos as theme).
- India: fast chapter because nothing structurally changes (stasis
  as theme).
- Both are one-chapter treatments of long periods, and in both cases
  the compression says something.

## Kashmir Shaivism

Kashmir Shaivism originated around the Delhi Sultanate period and
fits naturally into this chapter. It's viewed positively — a
significant philosophical-religious development that grows in the
space Buddhism vacated in India. Covered here rather than as a
separate beat.

## What this is NOT

- Not a claim that Indian culture was worthless during this period.
  Temple architecture, literature, mathematics (zero, decimal system),
  philosophy — real achievements occurred. But the chapter's framing
  is that the caste system prevented these from producing the kind of
  dynamic civilizational change that happened elsewhere.
- Not a Buddhist-nostalgia chapter. Buddhism leaving is covered in the
  Asia arc. This chapter is about what stayed.
