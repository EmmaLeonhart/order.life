# Mesopotamia — one chapter, velocity as theme

**Structural decision (firm).** Mesopotamia gets ONE chapter, placed
relatively late in the arc. This is not a deprioritization of
Mesopotamia's historical importance — it's a recognition that the
material-to-theme ratio is inverted compared to Egypt and most other
civilizations.

## The problem

Mesopotamia has **more genealogical material than themes to hang it on**.
This is the reverse of most other regions.

- The genealogy runs Sargon of Akkad → Hammurabi → down to the Assyrian
  kings. Abraham is depicted as a descendant of Sargon of Akkad (they're
  cousins through the Puzur-Ashur node, not a straight line).
- Required coverage: Sargon of Akkad, Gilgamesh (at least a mention),
  Hammurabi, Assyrian Empire (which is more Levantine anyway).
- Unlike Egypt, Mesopotamia lacks strong thematic hooks. Egypt has:
  immortality, pyramid-building, the clash with Judaism, Akhenaten's
  failed monotheism, AND a living contemporary identity. Mesopotamia
  has historically important material but no comparable living themes.

## The solution: speed IS the theme

The single chapter covers the ENTIRE span:

**Sumerian king list → Babylonian king list → Assyrian king list → ends
at Ashurbanipal.**

The chapter moves so fast compared to other civilizations that the
narrative velocity itself becomes the point. The blender of succession
and collapse IS the theme. Where Egypt gets multiple chapters to breathe,
Mesopotamia gets one chapter that accelerates through millennia. The
contrast with how other regions are treated is deliberate — it makes
the chaos and violence of Mesopotamian political history feel like what
it was: a region where empires rose and shattered in rapid succession,
each inheriting and destroying the last.

## Placement

Late in the arc — not among the first civilizational chapters. By the
time the reader reaches Mesopotamia, they've seen Egypt, the patriarchs,
and the Levantine material at a slower pace. Mesopotamia arrives as a
different tempo.

## What it connects to

- **Abraham / patriarchs** (`abraham-jacob.md`) — Abraham as a descendant
  of Sargon of Akkad. The Mesopotamian chapter gives Sargon context
  before or alongside the patriarchal sequence.
- **Assyrian Empire** — more Levantine than purely Mesopotamian; the late
  Assyrian material (Sennacherib, Ashurbanipal) overlaps with the
  Israelite kingdoms and the prophetic movement.
- **Bronze Age Collapse** (`bronze-age-collapse.md`) — Mesopotamia is
  affected by the BAC but not destroyed by it the way the Hittites and
  Mycenaeans are. The chapter's endpoint (Ashurbanipal, ~630 BCE) is
  post-BAC, so the collapse is something this chapter runs *through*.
- **Egypt** (`pharaohs.md`) — the explicit contrast. Egypt gets space;
  Mesopotamia gets speed. Both are ancient Near East but the Gaiad
  treats them very differently.

## Guardrail

Don't expand this to multiple chapters unless a genuinely load-bearing
thematic hook surfaces that justifies slowing down. The velocity is the
decision; resist the temptation to "do justice" to Mesopotamia by giving
it more space. The injustice IS the point — civilizations that destroy
themselves this fast don't get contemplative treatment.
