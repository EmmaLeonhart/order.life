# Indus Valley — the civilization the epic can't read

One of the required scale-correction inclusions (see `structure.md`
and `CLAUDE.md`). Cannot be omitted: it was demographically and
materially enormous, and leaving it out would falsify the book's claim
to comprehensive coverage.

## The problem the chapter addresses

- **Script undeciphered.** The Indus script has resisted decipherment;
  we have no legible inside-view of their religion, politics, or
  self-understanding.
- **No mythic-cycle bridge.** None of the late-mythic-cycle moves in
  `structure.md` §6 work here — there is no Livy, no Snorri, no
  Sahagún. The tradition was not captured at the threshold; it was
  lost entirely on its own terms and only partially recovered by
  archaeology.
- Yet the civilization was **larger, more urbanized, and longer-lived**
  than most of the cases that *do* have clean bridges.

## Why the Gaiad has to include it

- Scale correction: a book that foregrounds Greece and Rome while
  omitting Harappa and Mohenjo-daro would be silently committing the
  standard western-classicist distortion. The Gaiad's whole argument
  is against exactly that compression.
- **Precedes the Indo-European arrival** in the Indian subcontinent —
  the chapter establishes the landscape Manu / Yemo / the R1a-carrying
  migrations eventually enter. Not a smooth replacement; a different
  pre-existing world.
- Possible connections to later South Asian material (Vedic ritual,
  Dravidian substrates, Shiva / proto-Shiva imagery in the seals) that
  the Gaiad can foreground without resolving.
- The Indus is likely **Dravidian or Elamite-related**. But Dravidian
  culture is not uninfluenced by Aryan culture — the "eternal war"
  framing between Dravidian and Aryan is a modern nationalist fiction.
  In reality there was extensive co-existence and mutual influence.
  The Gaiad should not reproduce the adversarial framing.

## Haplogroup associations

Haplogroups **L, T, and H** all have potential links to the Indus
Valley civilization. These are the individually least interesting
major haplogroups for standalone chapters precisely because their
civilizational anchor is this illegible thing. They're most likely
absorbed into this chapter or into the Indo-European arrival
material (where they represent the pre-existing population). See
`haplogroups.md` for the full haplogroup register.

The Indus is in an **uncanny valley of knowledge**: too
well-documented to mythologize freely (like Göbekli Tepe), too
undeciphered to narrate with confidence. Not enough civilization to
make up stories about, not enough mystery to treat as pure myth.
Anything written risks being just plausible enough to be wrong.

## Gaiad handling

- **The chapter is partly about the illegibility itself.** Not a
  pretend-we-know summary. The seals, the urban grid, the lack of
  monumental kingship, the apparent decline — these are what we have,
  and the chapter can work with exactly that.
- Possibly paired with **Cucuteni-Trypillia** (`cucuteni-trypillia.md`)
  as the "two enormous pre-IE civilizations we cannot read" beat.
- Sits in Register A / early Register B. Pre-BAC, long before the
  Moses pivot (`moses-bac-pivot.md`).
