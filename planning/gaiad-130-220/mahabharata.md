# Mahabharata

The other great Hindu epic, must-include. Sister file: `ramayana.md`.
Where the Ramayana is a tighter heroic narrative, the Mahabharata is
sprawling, morally ambivalent, and contains whole other texts
embedded in it (notably the Bhagavad Gita).

## Core narrative

- **The Kuru dynasty** — two branches, the **Pandavas** (five
  brothers, sons of Pandu) and the **Kauravas** (hundred brothers,
  led by Duryodhana).
- **Dice game.** The Pandavas lose their kingdom, their freedom, and
  their shared wife Draupadi in a rigged dice game. Draupadi's
  humiliation in the Kaurava court (the attempted disrobing, Krishna's
  intervention) is one of the most structurally charged scenes in the
  epic.
- **Exile.** Thirteen years, including one in disguise.
- **War.** The **Kurukshetra** battle — eighteen days, both sides
  commit, most participants die. The Pandavas win, but the victory is
  hollowed out by the scale of loss.
- **Aftermath.** Yudhishthira's troubled kingship, the Pandavas'
  eventual pilgrimage to the Himalayas and death.

## The Bhagavad Gita

- Embedded *within* the Mahabharata, at the beginning of the
  Kurukshetra war. Arjuna, seeing his relatives arrayed against him,
  refuses to fight. Krishna — his charioteer and, it turns out, the
  divine — teaches him about duty (*dharma*), action (*karma*),
  devotion (*bhakti*), and the soul's eternity.
- **Philosophically enormous.** The Gita is one of the most
  commented-upon texts in world religion, with its own afterlife far
  larger than its 700 verses in the Mahabharata. Gandhi, Vivekananda,
  Aurobindo, etc.
- **The Gaiad's handling.** The Gita probably gets its own chapter
  beat inside the Mahabharata cluster — it is structurally distinct
  enough to deserve it. But Emma: the Gita does not get pulled *out*
  of the Mahabharata entirely. It lives in its place, on the
  battlefield, at the moment of Arjuna's hesitation. That's the
  texture worth preserving.

## What the Gaiad keeps

- **Moral ambiguity of the war.** The Pandavas are the protagonists
  but do morally compromising things (Yudhishthira's half-truth,
  Bhima's vow-fulfillments, Arjuna's killing of Karna while Karna is
  unarmed). The Kauravas are antagonists but include genuinely
  admirable figures (Bhishma, Drona, Karna). The Gaiad keeps the
  ambiguity.
- **Karna as tragic figure.** Secretly a Pandava (older brother of
  the five), raised by a charioteer, loyal to Duryodhana out of
  gratitude, killed by Arjuna. One of the most humanly compelling
  figures in world epic literature. Probably deserves named attention
  within the chapter.
- **Draupadi.** The shared wife of the five Pandavas, humiliated at
  the dice game, vowing vengeance. Her anger drives more of the plot
  than is often acknowledged in pious retellings. The Gaiad names
  this.
- **The scale of loss.** The epic closes on grief, not triumph. The
  post-war books (Shanti Parva, Anushasana Parva) are long
  meditations on governance, death, duty. Most modern retellings end
  at the battle; the Gaiad should gesture at the long mournful
  aftermath.

## Size and the condensation problem

- The Mahabharata is *long* (200,000+ verses). Fitting it into one
  or two chapters means aggressive compression. Strategy: pick a
  handful of scenes that carry the weight — dice game, Draupadi's
  humiliation, Bhishma's vow and death, Gita, Karna's death, the
  closing grief — and let those carry the structure.
- A compressed Mahabharata chapter is *fine*. The Gaiad is not
  retelling the epics; it is pointing at them and keeping the
  weight.

## Coverage scope

- **At least one chapter, possibly two.** One for the narrative
  spine, one for the Gita embedded in it. Could compress to one if
  chapter scarcity bites.

## Open handles

- **Krishna** as a full figure — lover, trickster, god, charioteer.
  Cross-traditional: the Krishna-bhakti devotional tradition
  (Chaitanya et al.) is post-gap and belongs later. But Krishna-in-
  Mahabharata is here.
- **The Bhagavata Purana's Krishna** (the child/cowherd/lover
  Krishna) is a separate textual tradition, mostly post-Mahabharata
  in composition. Probably a footnote rather than full handling.
