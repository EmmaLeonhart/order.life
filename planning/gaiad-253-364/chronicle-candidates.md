# Chronicle-form chapter candidates — Modern Period

## What is a chronicle-form chapter

A chronicle-form chapter is a list-compressed institutional genealogy.
Instead of narrating one event in detail, it walks the full line of
succession of an institution in one chapter — often spanning centuries.

The Gaiad already uses this form. The established model is **ch 253
(Chronicle of the Popes)**: Peter → Linus → through ~1500 years of
succession → ending around Alexander VI and the Treaty of Tordesillas.
That single chapter carries the entire institutional thread of the
Catholic papacy from antiquity into the Modern Period.

Chronicle-form compresses. It is the opposite of event-narrative. It
works best for:
- Institutions with long unbroken succession
- Institutions where the lineage itself is the point
- Threads that would otherwise consume many chapters

## Why this is a planning question

The Modern Period contains several institutions whose succession
spans most or all of the period. Each of them could be:

- **One chronicle chapter** (Popes-style compression)
- **Several event chapters** (narrating specific succession moments)
- **Absorbed into other thematic chapters** (not its own structural thread)

Deciding which institutions get chronicle-form treatment is a
structural allocation question, not a content question. It shapes
how many chapters the Modern Period has available for other uses.

## Candidate institutions

These are candidates worth considering. NOT prescriptive — the
answer may be "none of these, chronicles are a Popes-only move."

### Near-certain chronicle candidates if any are taken

- **Ottoman Sultans / Caliphs** — Osman I to Mehmed VI, ~1299–1924.
  Compresses the Ottoman thread and the later caliphate into one
  move. Pairs naturally with Atatürk / nation-state forging.
- **Qing Emperors** — Nurhaci to Puyi, 1616–1912. Compresses the
  last Chinese imperial lineage, ends at the Republic. Frames the
  20th-century China material.
- **Tsars of Russia** — Ivan IV (or Rurik line if going further
  back) to Nicholas II. Ends at the Revolution. Frames 20th-century
  Russia material.

### Less certain but structurally possible

- **Mughal Emperors** — Babur to Bahadur Shah II, 1526–1857. Ends
  at the fall to British rule. Possibly too entangled with other
  subcontinental threads to warrant a standalone chronicle.
- **Japanese imperial line through Meiji Restoration** — complicated
  because the imperial line is continuous but the period of Modern
  dynamic change is tight (Edo-Meiji-Shōwa-present). Could be a
  short chronicle.
- **Ethiopian emperors** — Solomonic dynasty to Haile Selassie
  (ends 1974). Specific thread worth considering for the African
  material.

### Probably not worth chronicle-form treatment

- **British monarchs / US presidents** — succession is documented
  elsewhere; not distinctive enough to warrant compression as a
  thematic move.
- **Modern papacy (Alexander VI → Francis)** — already covered by ch
  253's terminal beat at Alexander VI. Could arguably get a second
  papal-succession chapter later, but probably folded into other
  Catholic thematic chapters instead.

## Structural trade-offs

Each chronicle chapter "spent" is one fewer focal chapter available
for specific people/events. Budget is tight:

- Ch 253 (Popes) — spent
- 1 additional chronicle — 108 focal chapters remaining (253+362 fixed)
- 2 additional chronicles — 107 focal chapters remaining
- 3 additional chronicles — 106 focal chapters remaining

The question is whether chronicle-form is doing load-bearing work or
just displacing better-focused chapters.

## Open question

Does the Modern Period want:
- (a) **Just ch 253 (Popes) as the sole chronicle.** Everything else
  is focal. Chronicle-form is a one-time structural move that carries
  the specific thread (antiquity → Modern) and isn't repeated.
- (b) **A small set of parallel chronicles** (Popes + Ottomans + Qing,
  say) that together sketch the institutional skeleton of the early
  Modern world before the focal chapters take over.
- (c) **Chronicles at both ends** — ch 253 opening chronicle, and
  possibly one or more late chronicles that compress something at the
  other end (e.g., a chronicle of computing/AI succession, though
  that might work better as a focal chapter).
