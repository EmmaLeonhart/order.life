# Modern Period — Planning (ch 253–364)

**Budget:** 112 chapters. **Drafted:** ch 329. **Unwritten:** 111.

## Status

Inventory-style event lists have been wiped. A chronological list of
"important historical events" is not a plan — it is a textbook table of
contents, and produces generic coverage instead of a thematically-shaped
book. The middle of the Gaiad earned its form by being genealogical;
the Modern Period needs its own thematic spine, not event-checklisting.

## Confirmed structural anchors

- **CH 253 — Chronicle of the Popes.** First chapter after the Book of
  Lehi. A list-compressed institutional genealogy: Peter → Linus →
  through nearly 1500 years of succession → ending around Alexander VI
  and the Treaty of Tordesillas (the pope who "lit up the world" by
  dividing it), on the threshold of the Reformation. Genealogical-
  chronicle form, not event-narrative form. Carries the Catholic
  institutional thread from antiquity into the Modern Period in one
  move.
- **CH 329 — global 1945 peace tableau** (drafted). Soft-anchored on
  the Niinamesai / harvest-thanksgiving calendar date — the date
  coincidence is elegant but not sacred. The chapter is important
  but not the most important; it can migrate to an earlier slot if
  the thematic shape of the post-WWII stretch demands it. The hard
  anchors are the tail (ch 362/363/364) and ch 253 (Popes).
- **CH 362 — 2026 → time skip.** The handoff chapter. Starts in the
  present, executes a deliberate time-skip forward. Between ch 362 and
  ch 363 lies an implied gap ("what people are actually doing") that
  the book explicitly does NOT narrate — the gap IS the point. The
  book refuses mid-term prediction; only near-present and the far
  cosmic certainties get depicted.
- **CH 363 — Cryonic revival.** Conclusion of the modern period /
  the human sections. The only mid-term "prediction" the Gaiad
  commits to, because cryonics as a practice requires it. Structurally
  outside Modern; the landing of the human arc.
- **CH 364 — Milkomeda.** Galactic merger / Aster–Andromeda reunion.
  Conclusion of the entire Gaiad. Load-bearing because the Milkomeda
  frame is referenced in ch 1 — the book's ouroboros closes here.
  Structurally outside Modern.

**Budget clarification:** Modern Period human-scale content is
**ch 253–362** (110 chapters). Ch 363 and 364 are post-human / cosmic
finale and are NOT allocated from the Modern budget for thematic
planning purposes, even though they fall inside the ch 253–364
numeric range. The landing chapter for Modern's themes is ch 362,
not ch 364.

## Confirmed inclusions

Items the user has explicitly affirmed for the Modern Period. This is
NOT a seed inventory to grow back into an event list — it is the short
list of things that must appear somewhere in ch 253–362.

- **Chronicle of the Popes** (= ch 253 anchor above)
- **Gunpowder empires as parallel modernization** — Ottoman, Safavid,
  Mughal, plus Qing and Muhammad Ali's Egypt in the same category.
  Two distinct theses: (1) the world modernized globally in parallel
  but these states didn't modernize sufficiently to keep up with
  Europe; (2) unified identity was the variable of success —
  successful states gave themselves a single people-identity
  (Safavid-imposed Shi'ism succeeded; Ottoman non-Arabization failed
  and fragmented; Mughal Hindu-Muslim non-fusion failed; Qing
  Manchu-distinctness led to ruling-class dissolution). See
  `early-modern-gunpowder-empires.md`.
- **Japan — Tokugawa through Meiji Restoration.** The late-comer
  success case: a state that looks non-modernizing through early
  modern period, then successfully catches up via Meiji. Strongly
  succeeded at unified identity via invented tradition (State
  Shinto, imperial cult). Paired with the gunpowder empires as the
  contrast case. Meiji Restoration specifically is very important.
- **Zunghar Genocide (1755–58, Qing under Qianlong)** — end of
  pastoralism/nomadism as a world-historical force; paired with the
  Qing chapter and its Manchu-becoming-Chinese-while-extinguishing-
  their-steppe-cousins irony.
- **American and French Revolutions / revolutionary wave** — the
  differentiating moment where the Euro-Atlantic trajectory diverges
  from the gunpowder empires via a specific social transformation
  (popular sovereignty, citizen-soldier state, nationalism).
- **Sikhism / Guru Nanak** — a major old religion with specific
  historical role then and now; people-based; keep
- **Mormonism** — pseudo-ethnic, ties into earlier material; already
  handled in Lehi ch 252 but may need a Modern-side reference
- **Ch 329 global 1945 peace tableau** (= soft anchor above, drafted)
- **Armenian Genocide 1915**, framed specifically as the industrial-
  scale violence that forged Turkey into a nation-state (pair with the
  Atatürk / Turkish Republic arc, not a standalone atrocity chapter)
- **Tech / internet / AI** as elevated density in the closing arc —
  part of the landing into ch 362, not a coda

## Editorial criteria (confirmed)

- **Every Modern chapter must say something special about society.**
  The sharpest formulation of the intentionality rule: each chapter
  is obligated to carry a genuine claim about how society works,
  not merely record an event or figure. A chapter that records
  without saying anything is not a Modern Period chapter. This is
  the filter to apply before any other editorial criterion.
- **Peoples, not ideologies.** Religious or political movements get
  covered when they represent a people or pseudo-ethnic community
  (Sikhism, Mormonism, Judaism, Islam, Donghak, Tenrikyo, etc.) or
  when they are old enough and specific enough to have a distinct
  historical role. Distributed ideological systems with no ethnic or
  pseudo-ethnic basis do NOT get covered, because coverage legitimizes
  the ideas rather than including people. **Explicitly excluded on this
  basis:** Bahá'í, theosophy, Scientology, and similar.
- **Intentionality over coverage.** The Modern Period is not obligated
  to mention every major war, revolution, or figure. If a subject
  doesn't serve a theme the Gaiad has decided to carry, it doesn't
  appear.
- **Tech/AI density** is elevated in the closing arc — part of the
  landing, not a coda.
- **Americas-in-Lehi is resolved.** Pre-1890s Americas is Lehi's
  territory; Modern doesn't re-cover it.

### Thematic tensions to resolve (or to consciously hold open)

- **Thesis-2 tension: unified-identity-wins vs. suppressed-
  syncretism-is-a-casualty.** The Gaiad is simultaneously saying
  that politically-durable states unified their populations
  (often by inventing tradition), and that the syncretism thereby
  suppressed was a real loss. This is a live tension, not a
  contradiction. **Open question: where and how does the book
  voice this tension?** Candidates: (a) a dedicated chapter that
  names the tension directly; (b) woven as subtext through the
  Ottoman-fragmentation and Meiji-State-Shinto chapters; (c) left
  entirely as reader inference, never named in-text. User has
  framed the underlying question as "how should identity be
  constructed when standardising is a very big thing" — an open
  question, not something the Gaiad resolves.
- **Japan-as-catch-up-success question.** Why did Japan succeed at
  catch-up modernization when the gunpowder empires did not? User
  has flagged this as genuinely uncertain. The Japan chapter may
  need to *name* this question without answering it — treating the
  uncertainty itself as the content, rather than forcing a
  synthesis.
- **The Ottoman-Arabization counterfactual.** Is it voiced in the
  book (as a named road-not-taken that would have left us with a
  unified Arab superstate), or left as subtext under the
  fragmentation chapter?
