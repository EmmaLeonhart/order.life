# Early Modern — Gunpowder Empires & Parallel Modernization

## Two distinct theses

The gunpowder empires (+ Qing, + Japan) are doing two separate
thematic jobs in the Gaiad. Both are load-bearing. They are
distinct and should be kept distinct.

### Thesis 1 — Parallel modernization, insufficient acceleration

**The early modern world modernized globally in parallel. Europe
pulled ahead, but did not uniquely modernize.** These states were
"heavily modernizing" — not static, not despotic, not stuck. They
just didn't modernize *sufficiently* to keep up with the pace
Europe was setting in the 18th–19th centuries.

This reframes modernity as a global phenomenon with European
acceleration, not a European export. Against the "oriental
despotism" tradition from Montesquieu onward: these states look
more like early modern Spain than like despotic caricatures.

### Thesis 2 — Unified identity as the variable of success

**Successful early-modern states gave themselves a unified identity
— not a hierarchical or caste-based one.** The variable that
separates the ones that held together from the ones that fragmented
is whether the ruling class managed to construct an identity that
absorbed the ruled population (whether via native continuity,
religious reconstruction, or outright invention).

Regardless of origin, the states that treated their population as a
single people (or successfully made it so) outlasted the ones that
maintained a ruling-class/ruled-class distinction. This is a
descriptive observation, not a normative endorsement — see the
tension note below.

**This thesis connects directly to Axis 1 of the thematic spine
(invented tradition).** The states that successfully invented a
unified identity for themselves persisted; the ones that didn't
fragmented. Meiji State Shinto, Safavid Twelver Shi'ism, and even
the Egyptian-nationalist identity Muhammad Ali tried to construct
are all examples of the successful invention. The Ottoman failure
to Arabize is the counterexample.

### The tension between Thesis 2 and Axis 2 (suppressed syncretism)

The Gaiad is simultaneously saying:

- **Thesis 2:** unified-identity states persisted; caste/hierarchy
  states fragmented.
- **Axis 2:** the suppressed syncretism that unified identity often
  displaced was a real casualty, worth mourning.

These are not contradictory but they are in tension. The book has
to hold both. The tension itself is one of the things the Modern
Period is *about*: identity-construction as simultaneously the
price of political durability AND the cost of cultural pluralism.
The user has explicitly framed this as an **open question** — "how
should identity be constructed when this attempt at standardising
things is a very big thing" — rather than trying to resolve it.

## The gunpowder empires as evidence

Three near-simultaneous Islamic empires, each centered on gunpowder
military technology, each modernizing:

- **Ottoman** (~1299–1924)
- **Safavid** (~1501–1736)
- **Mughal** (~1526–1857)

Plus the structurally similar **Qing** (~1636–1912) and, later,
**Muhammad Ali's Egypt** (~1805–1882).

## The "mismatched ruling class" pattern

A specific early-modern phenomenon that runs through all of these
states: **the ruling class's self-presented identity does not match
the population being ruled**.

| Empire | Ruling identity | Actual population |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Mughal | Mongol-descended (Timurid) | Overwhelmingly Persianate and Indian; court culture Persian, not Mongol |
| Ottoman | Turkish dynasty | Increasingly Arab and Balkan — the Arab world largely unified under a Turkish ruling house |
| Qing | Manchu | Han Chinese majority |

(Safavid less so — they were more actually-Iranian — but even they
imposed Twelver Shi'ism onto a previously Sunni-dominant region as a
deliberate identity-construction project. Same phenomenon in a
different register.)

This pattern is distinctive to early-modern states and separates
them from classical-world empires (where ruling identity and ruled
population often align more closely, or where hybrid identities
never coalesced into this specific "dominant minority" form).

**The Gaiad angle:** what's interesting about the Mughals is NOT
that they are "the only verified Genghis-Khan-lineage heads of
state." That's a dull dynastic curiosity. What's interesting is
that they were **so non-Mongol in practice**. Akbar's specific
descent from Genghis Khan is interesting only because of the
contrast with his deeply Persianate, syncretic Indian court. The
generic "head of state descended from Genghis" trope is boring.

## Per-empire notes

### Ottomans

- Held their own against Europe reasonably well through the 17th c.,
  weaker but still modernizing through the 19th c.
- **Key oddity:** stayed Turkish-speaking rather than Arabizing.
  Much of the Arab world was unified for centuries under the Ottoman
  umbrella, but the ruling house never Arabized.
- **Thesis-2 verdict: failed at unified identity and fragmented.**
  Of the gunpowder empires, the Ottomans are the clearest example
  of failure-by-non-unification. They kept a Turkish ruling house
  over an Arab majority, never absorbed the identity, and when
  pressure came they fragmented rather than consolidating. The
  Armenian Genocide + Turkish Republic forging is the desperate
  too-late attempt to finally construct a unified identity, but
  only for what remained of Anatolia.
- **Counterfactual the Gaiad can name:** if the Ottomans had
  Arabized, there would probably be a unified Arab superstate today.
  The fragmentation of the Arab world into its current state system
  is a direct consequence of the Ottoman ruling-class non-Arabization.
- Functions in the Gaiad as the de facto representative of the
  pre-nationalist Arab world, despite being Turkish.
- Terminal point: Armenian Genocide as the forging of Turkey-the-
  nation-state out of the Ottoman wreckage (already a confirmed
  Modern Period inclusion). Chronicle-form chapter possibility for
  the full sultanic line is open.

### Safavids

- Convergently evolved toward modern government under Shah Abbas
  reforms.
- Imposed Twelver Shi'ism on Iran — a major deliberate religious-
  political identity construction. This is relevant to both the
  **invented tradition** axis (Safavid-era Shi'ism as constructed
  Iranian identity) and the **suppressed syncretism** axis (what
  Sunni-Shia coexistence existed before got reorganized).
- The Ottoman-Safavid border is where the Sunni-Shia geopolitical
  divide as it still exists was hardened.
- **Thesis-2 verdict: succeeded at unified identity, via externally-
  imposed religious construction.** Iran today is still recognizably
  a continuation of what the Safavids constructed. Even though the
  Shi'a identity was externally imposed on a previously Sunni-
  dominant region, it took — and the result is a durable Iranian
  national identity. One of the cleaner examples of invented
  tradition succeeding at its political task.
- User has noted "I don't know what to say about the Safavids" — the
  load-bearing material is probably the Shi'ism imposition + the
  Abbas-era modernization, not dynastic narrative.

### Mughals

- Persianate, syncretic, non-Mongol court culture.
- Akbar's syncretic religious experimentation (Din-i Ilahi,
  Ibadat-khana interfaith debates) is relevant to both the
  **suppressed syncretism** axis (Akbar as high-water mark of
  Hindu-Muslim fusion that didn't hold) and as the Akbar-descended-
  from-Genghis-Khan specific detail.
- **Thesis-2 verdict: failed.** Mughal identity never fused the
  Hindu and Muslim populations — Akbar tried, but the attempt was
  reversed by later emperors (Aurangzeb). No unified subcontinental
  identity emerged; the British stepped into the resulting vacuum.
  The Mughals are the hardest-failing gunpowder empire.
- Terminal point: British conquest 1857.
- Chronicle-form possibility exists but is less load-bearing than
  Ottoman chronicle.

### Qing

- **Not strictly a gunpowder empire**, but thematically grouped
  with them — user: "the Qing aren't really a gunpowder empire, but
  I feel like they kind of feel like they are one." Same
  "mismatched ruling class + heavy modernization + insufficient to
  keep up" profile.
- Ruling Manchus as a dominant minority over Han majority.
- **User interest: the religious practices of the ruling class.**
  The Manchu religious material (the Manchu-language inscriptions
  at the Temple of Heaven, the specific rituals the Manchu court
  maintained distinct from Han practice) is specifically flagged
  as interesting. The ruling-class religion is better documented
  than commonly thought.
- **Zunghar Genocide (1755–58, under Qianlong)** — load-bearing
  moment. Marks the end of pastoralism / nomadism as a political
  force in Eurasia. The last steppe-nomadic polity is extinguished,
  and after this, nomads as a world-historical force essentially
  cease to exist (except Americas, already in Lehi).
- **The irony to name:** the Qing were themselves descended from
  non-Han steppe peoples (Manchu/Jurchen) who had sinicized. A
  dynasty of former steppe-people-turned-sedentary-rulers then
  extinguishes the last steppe-people polity. This is not a
  footnote; it's the shape of the chapter.
- **Thesis-2 verdict: ruling class eliminated by conquest (1911
  revolution and subsequent events). The Qing maintained Manchu
  distinctiveness rather than merging into Han identity; when the
  dynasty fell, the Manchu ruling class effectively ceased to exist
  as a distinct group.** Different failure mode from Ottoman
  fragmentation — not territorial breakup but ruling-class
  dissolution.
- Chronicle-form possibility: full emperor line (Nurhaci → Puyi) is
  a plausible chronicle chapter carrying the whole dynasty.

### Muhammad Ali's Egypt

- Not strictly a gunpowder empire but belongs in the same category
  of convergent modernization.
- State-led industrialization, modern army, attempt to carve a
  modern Egyptian state out of nominal Ottoman overlordship.
- Useful as a case showing that convergent modernization was
  happening even at the supposed-periphery of the Islamic world,
  not just in the great empires.

### Japan (Tokugawa → Meiji)

- **Confirmed Modern Period inclusion. Meiji Restoration is a very
  important inclusion per user.**
- Different in shape from the other empires: Japan has more of a
  tendency toward being ruled as a single bloc than any of the
  others. Oda Nobunaga and the Tokugawa Shogunate don't fit the
  "mismatched ruling class" pattern — the ruling houses and the
  ruled population have been coherently Japanese throughout.
- **Thesis 1 verdict: a late-comer to modernization that succeeded
  where the gunpowder empires did not.** Japan doesn't look like
  it's modernizing through most of the early modern period (Sakoku
  isolation), then Meiji explodes into a rapid, successful,
  catch-up modernization in a few decades.
- **Thesis 2 verdict: strongly succeeded at unified identity,
  largely through invented tradition.** Meiji State Shinto, the
  manufactured imperial cult, the constructed "ancient continuity"
  of the emperor system — classic invented tradition that
  successfully did the unification job the Ottomans failed to do.
- **Open question:** why did Japan succeed at catch-up
  modernization when the gunpowder empires didn't? User: "I'm not
  really sure why it didn't." This is a live question rather than
  a settled claim. Candidate factors: pre-existing linguistic/
  ethnic unity, the specific bureaucratic continuity from Edo,
  the freshness of the Meiji political break, the timing relative
  to industrialization. Not resolved.

## What's NOT included on this thesis

Clarifying the scope:

- **The Aztecs are not in this category.** They didn't face the
  same modernizing pressures and didn't convergently evolve into
  this form. (Also: Lehi covers them. Out of Modern.)
- **Sub-Saharan Africa** — see open question below; some of it may
  belong to Book of Leo rather than Modern.

## Revolutionary wave (late 18th c.)

Distinct from the gunpowder-empires thesis but adjacent:

- **American Revolution and French Revolution** mark a real point of
  social change in the Euro-Atlantic. They shift the social basis
  of the Euro-Atlantic states in a way the gunpowder empires did
  not parallel.
- This is where Europe (and its American offshoots) pulls ahead —
  not because of superior modernization per se, but because of a
  specific political-social transformation (popular sovereignty,
  nationalism, citizen-soldier state) that the gunpowder empires
  did not undergo on the same timeline.
- So the shape of the early modern block is: **parallel
  modernization across the gunpowder empires AND Europe,
  differentiated in the late 18th c. by the revolutionary wave
  that transformed the Euro-Atlantic socially in a way the
  others did not match.**

## Structural implications

This thesis gives the early modern block (ch 254–~290, roughly) a
natural shape:

- Ch 253 (Popes) closes the medieval Catholic thread, ends at
  Tordesillas.
- Early chapters establish the parallel-modernization framing —
  Ottoman, Safavid, Mughal, Qing all treated as modernizing states,
  not as despotisms.
- The Zunghar Genocide chapter lands somewhere in the Qing stretch,
  carrying the end-of-pastoralism thematic weight.
- Muhammad Ali's Egypt appears as a continuation of the pattern
  into the 19th century.
- The revolutionary wave (American and French Revolutions) is the
  differentiating moment — the point where Europe's trajectory
  diverges from the gunpowder empires'.
- From there, the 19th century industrialization and colonialism
  chapters follow, but now framed as divergence from a parallel
  starting point, not as Europe uniquely modernizing.

Vietnam, Siam, the Indonesian sultanates, etc.) could stay in
Modern; the "frequently-conquered no-coherent-state" parts move to
