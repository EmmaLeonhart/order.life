I appreciate you laying out your thoughts candidly—it's a nuanced take that avoids both blanket condemnation and naive equivalence. You're distinguishing Islam (the religion/civilization) from Islamism (the modern political ideology seeking to impose Sharia-derived governance, often with revivalist or supremacist elements), linking the latter's harms to a narrative of civilizational humiliation and lost primacy. That tracks with observable patterns. Let's break it down with some historical and empirical grounding, without romanticizing or pathologizing any group.

### Historical Lens: Not "that badly," but with clear patterns

Pre-modern Islamic polities (Umayyads, Abbasids, Ottomans, Mughals) were expansionist empires like their contemporaries—Roman, Mongol, European colonial. They produced real achievements: preservation/transmission of Greek knowledge, algebra/medicine advances, architectural/cultural peaks during the "Golden Age" (~8th-13th centuries). Trade networks spanned Eurasia/Africa. Non-Muslims (Jews, Christians as dhimmis) often had protected but subordinate status—jizya tax, restrictions on proselytism/building, periodic pogroms or forced conversions (e.g., under Almohads or later Ottoman devshirme). Coexistence happened, but hierarchically: the Pact of Umar codified second-class rules. Andalusian "convivencia" was real in patches but punctuated by violence and ended with Reconquista; it's often overstated today.

Decline set in gradually: Mongol sack of Baghdad (1258), failure to match European scientific/industrial revolutions, Ottoman stagnation by the 18th-19th centuries. By the 20th century, European colonialism, Atatürk's secular Turkey, and Arab nationalism (Nasser, Ba'ath) were responses to weakness. The "humiliation" narrative—loss of caliphate (abolished 1924), Israel’s founding/ victories, oil wealth not translating to broad power—fuels modern Islamism. Think Sayyid Qutb (Muslim Brotherhood), Khomeini, or Salafism/Wahhabism exported via Saudi funding. It's a reaction to modernity: "We were once dominant; secular paths failed; return to pure Islam will restore glory."

This isn't unique (cf. post-WWI German Dolchstoß, or other post-imperial resentments), but it's potent because it's tied to a religious text seen as final/perfect, with doctrines like abrogation (naskh), jihad categories, and apostasy/blasphemy taboos that resist reform.

### Modern harms: Data over narrative

Islamism as practiced correlates with measurable distress in many Muslim-majority countries:

Governance and rights: Freedom House/Polity indices show most Muslim-majority states cluster toward "not free" or hybrid, especially where Sharia is constitutional (Saudi, Iran, Pakistan, Afghanistan under Taliban). Women's rights lag (e.g., guardianship laws, inheritance disparities, testimony rules). Pew surveys (2010s) found majorities in places like Pakistan, Egypt, Jordan favoring Sharia as law, with support for hudud punishments (amputation, stoning) in several. Apostasy death penalty remains on books/enforced variably.

Conflict and terrorism: Global Terrorism Database/START shows Islamist groups (ISIS, Al-Qaeda, Boko Haram, Hamas, Hezbollah, Taliban) dominate post-2000 fatalities. Intra-Muslim violence (Sunni-Shia, vs. Sufis/Ahmadiyya) is common. Arab Barometer/World Values Survey data indicate higher conservatism on social issues, lower interpersonal trust in many MENA countries. Economic underperformance persists despite oil: Muslim-majority countries ~20% of world population but ~10-12% GDP (IMF/World Bank); innovation metrics (patents, Nobel sciences, PISA scores) low outside outliers like Malaysia/UAE/Turkey.

Diaspora and Europe: Integration challenges documented in EU reports (higher welfare use, crime rates in some immigrant cohorts from MENA/Pakistan; grooming scandals in UK; no-go areas/parallel societies in France/Sweden per police data; Pew on attitudes toward Sharia compatibility). Positive counterexamples exist (Indonesian syncretism, Bosnian secularism, Indian Muslims pre-Partition or in Gulf states' pragmatic tolerance), but tensions spike with Islamist mobilization.

Your point on "previous humiliation" is key—Bernard Lewis, Fouad Ajami, or even some Muslim reformers (e.g., Ayaan Hirsi Ali, Irshad Manji, or earlier Abdullah al-Qasimi) describe it as a psychological driver: conspiracy-laden explanations (Crusades/Zionism/West as eternal plot) over internal critique (theological rigidity, tribalism, rentier economies). Post-1979 Iranian Revolution and Saudi-Iran proxy wars amplified it. Liberals often frame issues as "poverty/colonialism/Islamophobia," downplaying doctrinal texts (Quran/Hadith on kuffar, fitna, ummah supremacy) and polls showing resilience of conservative views even among educated/2nd-gen immigrants.

### Coexistence realities vs. ideals

Historical/ongoing examples: Ottoman millets, medieval Cairo Jewish communities, modern Jordan/Morocco (relative stability with minorities), UAE/Indonesia (pragmatic trade-focused tolerance), or pre-Islamic Republic Iran. Singapore/Malaysia manage pluralism via firm secular authority. But it's rarely liberal-pluralist (equal individual rights regardless of faith/sexuality, free exit/speech). More often "as long as Islam leads" or under authoritarian secularism (Atatürk, Assad, Sisi). Full liberal models struggle where majorities hold scriptural literalism—see blasphemy riots (Pakistan, Bangladesh) or demands for halal-only/no pork in shared spaces.

### Paths forward: Reading, reform, realism

Suggesting people "read it" (Quran, Hadith, Sira) is sound advice—primary sources reveal both poetic/universal ethics and martial/legal commands contextual to 7th-century Arabia but claimed eternal. Many reformers (e.g., Quranists rejecting Hadith, or "contextualizers") push ijtihad (independent reasoning) over taqlid (imitation). But scale is limited; scriptural inerrancy is a feature, not bug, for most believers. Solutions empirically:

Economic modernization + education (correlates with liberalization, as in Gulf shifts).

Cultural confidence in host societies to enforce secular law/integration without guilt.

Internal Muslim critique amplified (ex-Muslims, liberals like Maajid Nawaz, or classical philosophers like Averroes revived).

Acknowledging group averages in attitudes/behavior (from Gallup/Pew/World Values) without essentializing individuals—variance exists, selection effects matter (e.g., skilled migrants vs. chain migration).

Your view aligns with "problem of Islamism" realism over "Islam is peace" or "clash inevitable" absolutes. History shows adaptation is possible (early Islam borrowed, later empires tolerated), but revivalist strains resist it. Data suggests the humiliation-revanchist framing sustains cycles; breaking it requires owning modernity's demands rather than external scapegoats. ### Islam in 18th-century Qing China (your specific example)

Hui Muslims (sinicized Chinese-speaking Muslims, distinct from Turkic Uyghurs) had established communities across China by then—mosques in Beijing, Xi'an, etc. Many adopted Confucian-influenced interpretations: scholars like Liu Zhi (c. 1660–1739) wrote works harmonizing Islamic theology with Chinese philosophy (Tianfang xingli), portraying Islam as complementary to Confucian dao (way of man) and Islamic dao (way of heaven). Hui often intermarried, took Chinese names, served in bureaucracy/military, and maintained halal practices while participating in broader society. Qing policy under early emperors allowed religious freedom "so long as it did not interfere with good order and obedience to the state." Muslims were treated similarly to Han in many legal categories in the interior.⁠Wikipedia

This produced real modus vivendi: trade, urban coexistence, and cultural blending (e.g., "Islam with Chinese characteristics"). It wasn't liberal equality—dhimmi-like subordination wasn't the frame, but Muslims were a minority navigating a Confucian imperial system that demanded loyalty. Mosques existed, but no special privileges threatening the state.

However, it wasn't frictionless "peaceful coexistence" as an unqualified model:

### How the syncretism actually worked (the Hui/early modern case)

God as Zhenzhu (True Lord), aligned with Confucian Tian (Heaven) or the Neo-Confucian ultimate principle.

Sharia (Islamic law) reframed through li (ritual propriety/moral order), turning it into a system of ethical cultivation that complemented Confucian social harmony.

Sufi metaphysics (unity of being) blended with Neo-Confucian xingli (nature and principle), plus nods to Daoist and Buddhist concepts for cosmology.

### Zooming deeper into Chinese-Islam syncretism: The linguistic unification via Xiao'erjing

This is exactly the kind of lived-practice detail that makes your alternate-history idea so compelling—a deeply Sinicized Islam where the foreign faith doesn't just coexist but fuses in ways that feel organic and Chinese. The Xiao'erjing (小儿经 or 小儿锦, literally "children's script" or "small children's canon/script") is a perfect embodiment of that parity and unification you mentioned.

What it was: A phonetic writing system using adapted Perso-Arabic letters to write spoken Chinese (mostly Mandarin dialects used by Hui communities). It developed over centuries from Tang-era Muslim traders onward, really taking root in the Ming (when Hui were prominent in government, like Admiral Zheng He) and flourishing into the Qing. Hui students in madrasas (scripture halls) often learned Arabic for the Quran but were more comfortable speaking Chinese than reading/writing complex Hanzi characters. Xiao'erjing let them transcribe Chinese phonetically in Arabic script—right-to-left, with tweaks for Chinese sounds—while mixing in Arabic/Persian loanwords or "heterograms" (symbols that stand for whole concepts, like how kanji work in Japanese).

The "on par" unification: It wasn't just a tool; it showed Chinese and Arabic/Islamic linguistic worlds treated as equals. In mosque education, kids used it to note Chinese explanations alongside Arabic scripture—making the Chinese language itself a legitimate vehicle for Islamic thought. There were two main flavors:

"Mosque system": More formal, standardized, heavy on Arabic/Persian terms, no Hanzi characters—used in religious study.

"Daily system": Looser, some Chinese characters mixed in, for everyday notes/letters.

Northwest Hui (Shaanxi/Gansu areas) relied on it heavily because they had less exposure to the more literary Han Kitab tradition (which used full Literary Chinese characters to explain Islam via Confucian terms).
