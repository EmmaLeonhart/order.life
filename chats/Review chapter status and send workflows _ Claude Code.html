The structural problem

Nearly every other major founder has a built-in critical edge that scholarship has surfaced:

  • Jesus: mental-health / crisis reading
  • Confucius: outsider, failed politician
  • Jimmu: messy coalition, not divine purity
  • Muhammad: political conquest, Satanic Verses incident
  • Yahweh/Judaism: polytheism's violent erasure

The Buddha is the outlier — the figure comes down to us with the sharp edges already filed off by 2,500 years of monastic hagiography. Even Devadatta's critique (the cousin who tried to assassinate him) got folded into the tradition as a morality tale. The person is unusually protected by the religion.

So you have to work harder. Here are the real angles:

The Abandoner

The most obvious critical angle the tradition itself doesn't hide but minimizes. Siddhartha slipped out of the palace while his wife Yasodhara slept, leaving a newborn son Rahula. No goodbye, no consultation. Rahula grew up without a father. Yasodhara — a real named figure in the tradition — is mostly remembered as "the one who was left."

This is the Buddha-human-cost angle. Parallels the Jimmu "compromises" framing: the awakening is real AND the cost is real. The tradition's later redemption (he returned years later, ordained Rahula as a novice monk) doesn't undo the original act, it reframes it. Net-pro-Buddha but acknowledging what was traded.

The Anti-Aryan Outsider

Probably the strongest angle for the Gaiad's structural framework. Siddhartha was Kshatriya, not Brahmin — and the Sakya clan had ambiguous ethnic status. His entire teaching is a direct repudiation of the Brahminic-Vedic order: no caste, no sacrifice, no Vedas, no Brahma-as-creator. The Dissociative Breakdown

Parallel to the Jesus mental-health angle. The Four Sights (age, sickness, death, ascetic) as precipitating trauma. The garudhamma (eight heavy rules subordinating nuns to monks) and the reported statement that admitting women shortened the dharma's lifespan by 500 years. One angle I think is under-appreciated and fits your framework: the Buddha's teaching about Brahma (the creator god) is that Brahma is just a confused being trapped in his own delusion — stuck in samsara, wrongly believing himself to be the creator because he was reborn first in his cycle. He abandoned his family

His treatment of women was ambivalent at best
He initially refused to ordain women into the sangha. He only relented under pressure from his aunt Mahāpajāpatī and his attendant Ānanda, and when he did, he added the Eight Garudhammas — rules that permanently subordinate nuns to monks regardless of seniority. He also reportedly predicted that allowing women would halve the lifespan of the Dharma. That’s a hard passage to rehabilitate.
His enlightenment claims are unfalsifiable and self-serving
He declared himself to have achieved something no one could independently verify, built an institution around that authority, and made himself the sole arbiter of what counted as correct understanding. Critical historians note the structural similarity to other charismatic authority figures who happen to be right about everything.
His early asceticism period raises questions about judgment
He spent years practicing extreme self-mortification — near-starvation, breath retention, etc. — before concluding it didn’t work. A skeptic could read this not as a necessary journey but as stubbornness or susceptibility to the ascetic peer culture of śramaṇa movements he was embedded in.
The “Middle Way” was convenient
After six years of painful failure, he pivoted to a doctrine that happened to justify eating food, sleeping comfortably, and accepting donations. The philosophical framing is elegant, but the material outcome was that he got to stop suffering. A cynical read is available.

Despite some egalitarian rhetoric, the sangha in practice drew heavily from upper-caste men, and several Pali suttas contain statements that are at minimum caste-adjacent. He wasn’t the radical social leveler he’s sometimes portrayed as in modern Western reception.
