## Candidates for late mythic cycles that map onto historical genealogy

Ranked roughly by how late and how clean the bridge is:

### Stronger than Scandinavia in some respects

Mongolia — The Secret History of the Mongols (c. 1240 CE) traces Genghis Khan (1162–1227, fully historical) through a genealogy back to Börte Chino (Blue Wolf) and Gua Maral (Fallow Doe), the mythic progenitors. The transition is shorter in generations than the Norse one — the gap from mythic ancestor to named historical conqueror is compressed. And Genghis's descendants spread across Eurasia, so the gateway-ancestor logic works powerfully.

Māori / Polynesian whakapapa — The strongest case structurally. Māori genealogies do not have a break between gods and people. A whakapapa can be recited from Io (or Ranginui/Papatūānuku) forward through mythic ancestors, through Kupe and the great canoes, to a person alive today. And European contact was 18th–19th century, so you have genealogies running unbroken from literal creator deities to documented historical figures in colonial records. This may be the cleanest case globally.

Ethiopia — The Kebra Nagast (14th c., reflecting earlier material) traces the Solomonic dynasty from Menelik I (son of Solomon and Sheba) to historical emperors and continuing to Haile Selassie in the 20th century. This is a continuous mythic-to-historical royal line extending to living memory.

### Comparable to Scandinavia / Japan

Tibet — Yarlung dynasty kings trace to Nyatri Tsenpo, who descended from the sky by a rope. Documented in the Red Annals (14th c.) and Blue Annals (15th c.). Extends into historical Tibetan Empire kings (Songtsen Gampo, 7th c.).

Korea — Samguk Yusa (1281 CE) preserves the Dangun founding myth (traditional 2333 BCE) and ties mythic founders (Park Hyeokgeose, Kim Alji — eggs and heaven-descent) to the historical Silla, Goguryeo, and Baekje kings. Late compilation, clean bridge.

Vietnam — Hồng Bàng dynasty through Lạc Long Quân and Âu Cơ (dragon-lord and mountain-fairy), codified in Lĩnh Nam chích quái (14th c.) and Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư (15th c.). Bridges to historical Vietnamese dynasties.

Ireland / Celtic — Lebor Gabála Érenn (11th c. compilation, older material) — Milesians, Tuatha Dé Danann, high kings of Tara running continuously into historical kings like Brian Boru (d. 1014). The user is right that the Celtic cycles "really go in."

Wales — Mabinogion + Geoffrey of Monmouth's Historia Regum Britanniae (1136). Welsh royal houses trace through Arthur, Uther, back through Brutus of Troy. This is the vehicle for fixing the British-Arthur problem (see below).

Persia — Shahnameh (Ferdowsi, c. 1000 CE) — Keyumars → Pishdadian → Kayanian → Sasanian. The transition from legendary to historical kings is graduated, not sharp. Very late codification of a very long cycle.

### Honorable mentions

Armenia — Movses Khorenatsi ties Haik (mythic ancestor) to historical Arsacids.

Georgia — Life of Kartli ties Azon/Pharnavaz to Bagrationi dynasty.

Arabia — Pre-Islamic Quraysh genealogies trace to Adnan/Qahtan, bridge to Muhammad's lineage. This is part of why the Jewish/Muslim/Persian bridge you mentioned works genealogically.

## Your Lithuania counterfactual — you're right

Lithuania was pagan until 1387 — the latest Christianization in Europe. If a Shahnameh-scale epic of Lithuanian pre-Christian mythology had been compiled before or shortly after conversion, it would be the structurally cleanest late-mythic bridge in the world, because the gap between "living pagan religious tradition with its mythic cycles intact" and "documented medieval kingdom with clear historical records" is basically zero. The Gediminid dynasty (Mindaugas, Gediminas, Algirdas, Jogaila) is fully historical and was still pagan. Perkūnas, Velnias, Sovij, Žemyna survive only fragmentarily — Marija Gimbutas and Algirdas Greimas did reconstructive work, but it's thin.

The reason this didn't happen is the same reason Lithuania's conversion was so late: Teutonic Order pressure suppressed documentation rather than encouraged it. A Christian monk-scribe tradition of the kind that preserved Irish, Norse, Welsh, and Georgian material never took root in Lithuania until after the conversion was a fait accompli.

Worth capturing this as a chapter or at least a note — the absence of a Lithuanian Shahnameh is itself historically meaningful.

Your rapid-transition hypothesis holds up against this list. Addressing your follow-up about China + the Scandinavia/Japan benchmark argument:

You've sharpened the hypothesis. It's not "late mythic cycle" — it's "living mythic cycle caught by a competent literary tradition at the moment of transition, and recorded in modern legible form." That's a much more specific structural criterion and explains why Scandinavia and Japan are the benchmark cases even though they're the most recent:

Scandinavia: the Icelandic Commonwealth post-Christianization (~1000 CE) had both the living mythic tradition still in cultural memory AND monks trained in Latin literacy who could write it down in Old Norse. ## What got built

`wiki-scripts/wikibase_dump.py` — a stdlib-only Python script (no extra deps beyond Python 3.13 which the actions already use) that walks a numeric range of Wikibase IDs and saves each entity as JSON.

Hits `https://wiki.order.life/wiki/Special:EntityData/{id}.json` first, falls back to `https://wiki.order.life/w/api.php?action=wbgetentities&ids={id}&format=json` if the first endpoint doesn't respond. Both endpoints are namespace-name-agnostic — they work regardless of whether your Wikibase install calls the namespace `Item`, `EntitySchema`, or something custom. So the "I don't know what the item namespace is" problem doesn't block anything.

Retries 5xx/network errors twice with backoff.

Skips missing entities (404 or `"missing":""` marker) without failing the run.

Writes to `wikibase/items/Q{N}.json` and `wikibase/properties/P{N}.json`, one file per entity, sorted-key JSON for clean diffs.

`.github/workflows/wikibase-dump.yml` — manual-dispatch workflow with configurable inputs:

`items_start` / `items_end` (default 1 / 101 → Q1..Q100)

`properties_start` / `properties_end` (default 1 / 101 → P1..P100)

`skip_items` / `skip_properties` toggles

`throttle` between requests (default 0.5s)

On a successful run with changes, it commits to a branch named `wikibase-dump/items-{start}-{end}-props-{start}-{end}-{run_id}` and opens a PR automatically.

## How to run it when you get home

Go to the Actions tab → wikibase-dump → "Run workflow"

Leave defaults (Q1–Q100 + P1–P100) for the first run, or narrow to just properties first (`skip_items: true`) since properties are often <100 in a Wikibase and you'll see the full schema quickly.

Merge the PR it creates. Then run it again for Q101–Q200, Q201–Q300, etc.

Good question and you're right that universal veneration makes this constrained. There ARE critical angles on the historical Buddha (the person, not the religion) that scholars have worked out, and several of them map cleanly onto the Gaiad's existing founder-treatment patterns. Let me lay out what's available.

## The structural problem

Nearly every other major founder has a built-in critical edge that scholarship has surfaced:

Jesus: mental-health / crisis reading

Confucius: outsider, failed politician

Jimmu: messy coalition, not divine purity

Muhammad: political conquest, Satanic Verses incident

Yahweh/Judaism: polytheism's violent erasure

The Buddha is the outlier — the figure comes down to us with the sharp edges already filed off by 2,500 years of monastic hagiography. Even Devadatta's critique (the cousin who tried to assassinate him) got folded into the tradition as a morality tale. The person is unusually protected by the religion.

So you have to work harder. Here are the real angles:

## The Abandoner

The most obvious critical angle the tradition itself doesn't hide but minimizes. Siddhartha slipped out of the palace while his wife Yasodhara slept, leaving a newborn son Rahula. No goodbye, no consultation. Rahula grew up without a father. Yasodhara — a real named figure in the tradition — is mostly remembered as "the one who was left."

This is the Buddha-human-cost angle. Parallels the Jimmu "compromises" framing: the awakening is real AND the cost is real. The tradition's later redemption (he returned years later, ordained Rahula as a novice monk) doesn't undo the original act, it reframes it. Net-pro-Buddha but acknowledging what was traded.

## The Anti-Aryan Outsider

Probably the strongest angle for the Gaiad's structural framework. Siddhartha was Kshatriya, not Brahmin — and the Sakya clan had ambiguous ethnic status. The name Sakya has a genuine scholarly thread connecting it to Scythian, suggesting possibly Central Asian / Iranian origins — a community on the northeastern edge of the Indo-Aryan cultural sphere rather than a core member of it.

His entire teaching is a direct repudiation of the Brahminic-Vedic order: no caste, no sacrifice, no Vedas, no Brahma-as-creator. He's the anti-Vedic founder, from a community whose Aryan credentials were liminal, doing to the Brahmins what Yahweh-monotheism later did to the Canaanite pantheon — except the polarity is reversed. In the Gaiad's existing frame, monotheism's erasure of the older polytheist substrate is tragedy; here the Buddha is the outsider who rejects an entrenched priestly order, which the Gaiad can treat either way but probably treats sympathetically (since the Brahminic sacrificial order is the same register as priestly-fossilized Egypt/Mesopotamia in §6).

This angle ALSO directly leverages your Indo-European / steppe material. Sakya → Scythian → the whole Indo-Iranian branch of the Pontic-Caspian expansion. The Buddha as a figure from the edge of the Indo-European southeastward migration, reacting against what the IE-Brahmin descendants had become. Connects straight into the required-inclusion Sintashta/Indo-Iranian chapter.

## The Dissociative Breakdown

Parallel to the Jesus mental-health angle. The Four Sights (age, sickness, death, ascetic) as precipitating trauma. Six years of near-suicidal asceticism — the famous "I touched my belly and felt my spine" — followed by an abrupt collapse and rejection of the whole ascetic program in favor of the Middle Way. Enlightenment as the stabilization after the crisis, not as serene unfolding.

The tradition admits the asceticism was wrong ("I was nearly dead"). Modern readings (some Buddhist-sympathetic, some not) frame the pre-enlightenment years as genuinely dangerous. This angle parallels Jesus's treatment structurally — "hesitancy about mental health, ultimately pro."

## The Gendered Silence

The garudhamma (eight heavy rules subordinating nuns to monks) and the reported statement that admitting women shortened the dharma's lifespan by 500 years. Ananda's negotiation of Mahapajapati's ordination as a grudging compromise. These are real textual facts. A Gaiad treatment doesn't have to dwell on them moralistically, but their inclusion — as data — produces the X-Files effect without editorializing.

## The Brahma-Deflater

One angle I think is under-appreciated and fits your framework: the Buddha's teaching about Brahma (the creator god) is that Brahma is just a confused being trapped in his own delusion — stuck in samsara, wrongly believing himself to be the creator because he was reborn first in his cycle. Everyone else was reborn after him, so they think he made them.

This is structurally identical to the Yahweh-who-didn't-kill-the-other-gods framing. The Buddha treats the god-question by deflating the creator-god's claim to priority, not by attacking gods as evil. That's the same deflationary-but-pro maneuver you're doing with Judaism. Worth capturing as an explicit symmetry.

## My suggestion

The Anti-Aryan Outsider + Brahma-Deflater combo is probably the strongest single move:

It places the Buddha in the Indo-European migration story you're already telling (Sakya-Scythian connection).

It makes him the figure who relativizes god-as-creator, paralleling your Yahweh move.

It's net-pro the Buddha — he's right, in the Gaiad's frame, about both the caste order and the creator-god question.

It gives you the weird-systematic edge without requiring you to attack a universally venerated figure.

The Abandoner can ride along as the human-cost note without needing to be the primary frame. The Dissociative Breakdown creates symmetry with Jesus if you want the parallel to show.

What I wouldn't lead with: the Gendered Silence. Not because it's not real, but because it's thin material for an epic chapter — it's one datum, not a thematic frame. Better included as a line within the larger treatment than used as the spine.
